Heard It First – Daily Digest

December 21, 2025

Commemoration at Bondi Beach

A minute of silence was observed to remember the victims of the recent attack at Bondi Beach. Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese attended the memorial event but was booed upon his arrival. The moment of remembrance highlights ongoing community grief and raises questions about public sentiment toward the government’s handling of the incident.

Rise in Executions in Saudi Arabia

Rights groups have condemned a record number of executions carried out in Saudi Arabia this year. According to the UK-based campaign group Reprieve, at least 347 people have been put to death in 2025. The increase in executions has sparked international criticism, drawing attention to human rights concerns within the country.

Expansion of Israeli Settlements in the West Bank

Israel has approved 19 new settlements in the occupied West Bank. Far-right Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich stated that the move aims to block the establishment of a Palestinian state. This development could further complicate efforts toward peace and negotiation in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, as settlement expansion remains a contentious issue internationally.

US Actions Against Venezuelan Oil Trade

The United States seized a second oil tanker off Venezuela’s coast following an order by President Donald Trump to "blockade" sanctioned oil tankers entering and leaving the country. This action is part of the ongoing US effort to exert pressure on Venezuela’s government through economic means and restrict its oil trade.

Violence in South Africa

Gunmen opened fire in a tavern in Bekkersdal, South Africa, killing nine people and injuring ten others. The attackers reportedly continued shooting randomly during the incident. This act of violence adds to concerns about public safety and crime in the region, prompting calls for increased security measures.

Release and Response to Epstein Files

A long-awaited release of files related to Jeffrey Epstein has left survivors feeling "nervous and sceptical." US lawmakers who advocated for the documents to be made public criticized the initial release as incomplete. The files include information about high-profile figures such as Bill Clinton and Michael Jackson, attracting widespread attention and ongoing debate about justice and transparency.
