Technology News Briefing — March 31, 2026

1. States Move Forward with AI Regulation Despite Federal Opposition Several U.S. states including California and Utah are advancing their own regulations on artificial intelligence technology, even as former President Trump has directed states to halt such efforts. These state-level initiatives focus on establishing safety and privacy protections for companies developing AI systems, particularly those contracting with government agencies. For example, California Governor Gavin Newsom issued an executive order requiring AI firms working with the state to implement guardrails. This trend reflects growing concern over AI risks and highlights a divide between federal and state approaches to managing emerging technologies.

2. SEC Shifts Toward More Crypto-Friendly Regulations The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has adopted a more welcoming stance toward cryptocurrency under the Trump administration’s influence, moving away from strict enforcement toward crafting policies that accommodate digital assets. This shift aims to provide clearer regulatory frameworks for crypto companies, potentially encouraging innovation while maintaining investor protections. The change marks a departure from earlier regulatory skepticism and may affect how crypto markets evolve in the coming years.

3. Congressional Inquiry into Elon Musk’s Influence on Corporate Transparency Enforcement Democratic lawmakers have requested information from the Treasury Department regarding Elon Musk’s possible involvement in halting the enforcement of the Corporate Transparency Act, a law intended to increase disclosure requirements for businesses. The inquiry examines how Mr. Musk, a prominent technology entrepreneur, may have contributed to suspending certain business disclosure obligations. This investigation underscores ongoing concerns about transparency in corporate governance and the influence of powerful individuals on federal policy.

4. Schools Experience Pushback Against Chromebooks and Digital Learning Tools Some school districts are scaling back on the use of Chromebooks and other digital devices in classrooms, opting instead to return to traditional learning materials such as textbooks and pencils. Restrictions have been placed on access to YouTube and video games on school laptops to minimize distractions. Feedback from students, including some seventh graders, indicates a preference for offline learning. This development highlights challenges educators face in balancing technology integration with effective teaching and student engagement.

5. Helium Shortage Poses Risk to Semiconductor Manufacturing A shortage of helium, a critical gas used in chip production, is causing concern among semiconductor manufacturers, especially those producing AI chips. Approximately one-third of the global helium supply is currently offline due to ongoing conflicts in Iran, disrupting supply chains. Gas companies are working to assure chipmakers that production will continue without interruption. The situation reveals vulnerabilities in the hardware supply chain and could impact the broader technology sector if shortages persist.

6. Social Media Liability Expanded by Verdict Against Meta and YouTube A recent court verdict found social media giants Meta and YouTube liable for creating addictive products. The ruling could have significant implications for how these and similar companies design platforms and moderate content. The case emphasizes growing scrutiny over the societal impacts of social media and may influence future legal and regulatory actions regarding technology companies’ responsibilities to users.
