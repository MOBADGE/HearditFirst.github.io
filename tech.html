Technology News Briefing – March 25, 2026

OpenAI Ends Sora Video Generator Service OpenAI announced it will shut down Sora, its AI-powered video generation platform, just three months after securing a multiyear agreement to feature Disney characters. The abrupt discontinuation marks a reversal for the startup, which had recently expanded into branded video content. The reasons behind OpenAI’s decision have not been detailed, but it signals potential challenges in sustaining AI-driven video tools commercially or creatively. This move affects users and partners relying on Sora for automated video creation, highlighting the uncertainties in emerging AI media technologies.

Meta Penalized for Child Safety Failures Meta has been ordered to pay $375 million following a New Mexico jury verdict that found the company misled consumers about the safety measures on its platforms, which allegedly allowed for the sexual exploitation of minors. This represents one of Meta’s first significant legal setbacks in a growing area of scrutiny over social media companies’ responsibilities to protect young users. The ruling may prompt Meta and other companies to revisit content moderation policies and transparency efforts around child safety on digital platforms.

Arm Holdings to Manufacture Its Own AI Chips In a notable strategic shift, Arm Holdings announced plans to start producing its own computer chips targeted at AI data center applications, rather than solely licensing chip designs as it has traditionally done. This development reflects the increasing demand for specialized processors optimized for artificial intelligence workloads. By moving into manufacturing, Arm aims to capture more value in the chip supply chain and compete more directly with established chipmakers. The initiative could have implications for the AI hardware market and ecosystem competition.

Epic Games Cuts Workforce by About 20 Percent Epic Games disclosed layoffs affecting over 1,000 employees, roughly one-fifth of its workforce, citing a downturn in its flagship game, Fortnite. The reduction signals financial and operational challenges at the company, which has been investing heavily in game development and the metaverse space. The cutback may impact ongoing projects and reflects broader shifts in gaming industry dynamics, particularly when flagship titles lose momentum.

Legal Review of Hewlett Packard Enterprise Deal Continues A federal judge began hearings on a challenge by Democratic state attorneys general against the Justice Department’s approval of Hewlett Packard Enterprise’s $14 billion deal, alleging the settlement was flawed and possibly corrupt. The dispute centers on antitrust concerns and regulatory oversight in the technology sector. The outcome could affect future large-scale mergers and acquisitions involving major tech firms, emphasizing tensions between federal authorities and state-level enforcement efforts.

Remembering Paul Brainerd, Desktop Publishing Pioneer Paul Brainerd, who passed away at 78, was recognized for revolutionizing desktop publishing with the introduction of PageMaker software. His work helped transition printing from manual paste-up techniques to digital layout processes on computers, fundamentally changing publishing workflows. Brainerd’s contributions laid a technical foundation used widely in publishing and design industries for decades.
