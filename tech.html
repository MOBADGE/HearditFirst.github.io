Technology News Briefing – March 31, 2026

1. States Continue AI Regulation Despite Federal Resistance Several U.S. states, including California and Utah, are moving forward with regulations aimed at artificial intelligence (AI) technologies even after a federal directive from former President Donald Trump ordered a halt to such efforts. California Governor Gavin Newsom recently signed an executive order mandating safety and privacy guardrails for AI companies contracting with the state. This initiative reflects ongoing concerns about AI’s impact and the desire for oversight at the state level, highlighting tensions between state authorities and federal leadership. The actions underscore how states are taking independent steps to address AI risks and governance despite federal reluctance to regulate.

2. SEC Shifts Toward Cryptocurrencies With New Policies The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is adopting a more crypto-friendly approach to financial regulation, continuing a trend of closer engagement with digital assets under the Trump administration. Recent policy developments signal a softer stance that aims to incorporate cryptocurrencies into existing financial frameworks rather than imposing outright restrictions. This shift could influence investor confidence and the broader adoption of crypto technologies, marking a notable change from earlier regulatory skepticism.

3. Congressional Inquiry Into Elon Musk’s Influence on Corporate Transparency Enforcement Democratic lawmakers have requested detailed information from the Treasury Department about Elon Musk’s involvement in the suspension of enforcement of the Corporate Transparency Act, a law designed to improve business disclosure and combat illicit activity. The inquiry centers on whether Musk had any role in persuading the administration to delay actions related to corporate transparency. This investigation highlights ongoing scrutiny of Musk’s influence within government circles and the regulatory implications for corporate oversight.

4. Schools Reconsider Digital Devices as Chromebook Backlash Grows Some schools are reversing their reliance on Chromebooks and other digital devices following concerns about distractions and effectiveness. In Kansas, educators have limited access to YouTube and video games on laptops, with a renewed focus on traditional learning tools like textbooks and pencils. Surveys of seventh graders suggest a portion of students prefer offline learning environments. This shift reflects growing debates about the balance between technology use and educational outcomes in classrooms.

5. Helium Supply Shortage Poses Risks to Semiconductor Manufacturing The semiconductor industry faces potential disruptions due to a shortage of helium, an essential gas used in chip fabrication. Roughly one-third of the global helium supply is currently unavailable due to supply chain issues linked to conflict in Iran. Gas suppliers are working to reassure AI chip manufacturers that production will not be interrupted, but the situation underscores vulnerabilities in the supply chain for critical materials needed in advanced technology production.

6. Legal Judgment Against Meta and YouTube for Addictive Design Could Influence Social Media In a recent landmark case, a jury found Meta and YouTube liable for developing products that foster addictive behaviors. This verdict may have significant implications for how social media companies design their platforms and could inspire changes in industry practices and regulations aimed at reducing harmful user engagement. The case draws attention to ongoing concerns about the societal impacts of social media and how legal frameworks address these challenges.

These developments illustrate how technology intersects with regulation, supply chains, and societal concerns, affecting industries from AI to social media and education. The evolving landscape will likely continue to prompt debates on governance, responsibility, and the role of emerging technologies.
