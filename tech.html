Technology News Briefing – April 16, 2026

1. Rethinking Artificial Intelligence with “Jagged Intelligence” A recent article explores the idea of “jagged intelligence” as a new way to understand artificial intelligence (AI). Traditional discussions often compare AI directly to human intelligence, but this approach may oversimplify what AI actually does well. By focusing on specific areas where AI excels unevenly—hence the term “jagged”—experts suggest it is possible to better anticipate which jobs or tasks AI is more likely to impact or replace. This perspective aims to shift debates about AI’s role in the workforce toward practical implications rather than broad generalizations.

2. The Growing Importance of Human Interaction in the AI Era As AI automates many routine and cognitive tasks, certain human skills such as persuasion, negotiation, and emotional reassurance appear to be gaining importance in the workplace. A recent report highlights that while AI can enhance efficiency, it does not replace the nuanced interpersonal work involved in meetings and collaborative settings. This suggests that roles heavily reliant on human judgment and social skills may remain less vulnerable to automation, underscoring the continued value of human-centric work even as AI tools become more widespread.

3. Amazon Acquires Globalstar to Expand Satellite Internet Ambitions Amazon has acquired Globalstar, a satellite communications company, for $10.8 billion. This purchase is part of Amazon’s broader strategy to grow its satellite internet service and position itself as a competitor to SpaceX’s Starlink. By integrating Globalstar’s technology and satellite network, Amazon aims to enhance the coverage, reliability, and capacity of its internet offerings, which could extend connectivity to underserved regions. The move reflects increasing corporate interest in satellite infrastructure as a component of global internet access.

4. Security Concerns Surface Around AI Leadership Authorities arrested a 20-year-old man in Texas in relation to an attack involving a homemade bomb at the residence gate of OpenAI’s CEO, Sam Altman. Investigators revealed that the individual had compiled a list of other AI executives and leaders, along with written materials expressing concerns about the risks of artificial intelligence. This event highlights the heightened tensions and security challenges surrounding AI development and its prominent figures, as public discussion continues about AI’s potential societal impacts.

5. Allbirds, Known for Footwear, to Transition into AI Technology Consumer brand Allbirds announced it plans to pivot away from its traditional sneaker business following a recent sale valued at $39 million. The company will rebrand as NewBird AI and focus on acquiring high-performance computer chips to enter the artificial intelligence sector. This significant shift marks one of the recent examples of non-technology companies redirecting resources toward AI-related ventures, reflecting the expanding influence and perceived business opportunities in AI technology across industries.

6. Changes at Ford as Key EV Leader Departs Ford Motor Company confirmed that Doug Field, a former Tesla and Apple executive who returned in 2021 to lead the company’s electric vehicle (EV) development, is leaving the organization. During his tenure, Field had overseen the creation of new electric models and relevant software efforts. His departure may have implications for Ford’s continuing push into the EV market, an area of growing focus for traditional automakers amid increasing competition and evolving regulations.
