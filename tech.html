Technology News Briefing – March 31, 2026

1. States Advance AI Regulation Despite Federal Opposition Several states, including California and Utah, are moving forward with regulations aimed at managing artificial intelligence technologies, even though former President Trump has directed them to halt such efforts. These state-led initiatives focus on establishing safety and privacy guidelines for AI companies, particularly those contracting with state governments. California Governor Gavin Newsom recently issued an executive order mandating guardrails on AI applications to address concerns about potential risks. This push by states reflects growing attention to AI oversight amid a lack of comprehensive federal policy, highlighting a patchwork approach to AI governance in the U.S.

2. SEC Adopts More Crypto-Friendly Policies Under Trump Administration The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has shifted toward a more accommodating stance on cryptocurrencies during the Trump administration. Previously seen as a regulator often skeptical of digital assets, the agency is now developing policies that facilitate crypto adoption within the financial system. This regulatory evolution aims to balance investor protection with fostering innovation in the crypto market. The change signals a broader acceptance of cryptocurrencies within mainstream finance and could influence how digital assets are integrated into regulated financial products and services.

3. Congressional Inquiry Into Elon Musk and Corporate Disclosure Law Democratic lawmakers have requested information from the Treasury Department regarding Elon Musk’s involvement in suspending enforcement of the Corporate Transparency Act. This law targets opaque business ownership structures to prevent illicit activities such as money laundering. Questions center on whether Musk influenced decisions to delay enforcement, potentially affecting transparency in business disclosures. The inquiry underscores ongoing scrutiny of Musk’s role in policy matters and broader concerns about corporate accountability and regulatory effectiveness.

4. Schools Reconsider Technology Use Amid Chromebook Backlash Several school districts are reevaluating their reliance on laptops, such as Chromebooks, for student learning. Restrictions eliminating access to distractions like YouTube and video games have led some students, including seventh graders, to express a preference for traditional offline learning tools such as textbooks and pencils. This trend highlights challenges educators face in balancing technology integration with maintaining student engagement and focus. The movement suggests a nuanced reassessment of digital resource use in educational environments.

5. Helium Supply Shortage Poses Risks to AI Chip Manufacturing The chip industry faces a potential bottleneck due to a shortage of helium gas, critical for semiconductor production. A significant portion of global helium supplies remains offline because of ongoing conflict-related disruptions in Iran. Gas suppliers are currently working to reassure AI chip manufacturers that the shortage will not impact production schedules. Given the importance of advanced chips for AI applications, any sustained helium supply disruptions could have broader effects on AI hardware availability and development timelines.

6. Legal Verdict Against Meta and YouTube Addresses Product Addiction A recent jury verdict found Meta and YouTube liable for creating addictive products. The case centers on the design features that encourage extended user engagement. This ruling represents a significant moment in social media regulation, potentially leading to increased accountability for tech companies regarding user well-being. The decision may prompt platforms to reconsider design strategies and could influence future legal standards governing digital products and their societal impact.
