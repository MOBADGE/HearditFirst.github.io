Technology News Briefing – March 29, 2026

1. Court Halts Pentagon’s Supply Chain Risk Label on Anthropic A federal judge has issued an injunction preventing the Pentagon from officially designating artificial intelligence company Anthropic as a “supply chain risk.” This ruling marks a preliminary win for Anthropic in its ongoing legal dispute with the Department of Defense. The Pentagon’s label had implicated the AI firm as a potential national security concern, affecting its ability to secure government contracts. The decision matters because it underscores legal tensions around government oversight of AI vendors and could influence how security assessments shape the emerging AI industry’s relationship with federal agencies.

2. Helium Shortage Poses Challenge for AI Chip Manufacturing The global AI semiconductor industry faces potential disruptions due to a helium supply shortage. Roughly one-third of helium production remains offline amid ongoing conflict in Iran, a major gas supplier. Helium is critical in cooling processes during the manufacturing of advanced chips used in AI systems. Gas companies are working to assure chip makers that supply stability will be maintained despite the geopolitical issues. This development is notable because any instability in materials supply can slow AI hardware innovation and production, affecting broader technology development timelines.

3. Binance Under Scrutiny for Allowing Billions in Crypto to Reach Iran Investigations have revealed that the cryptocurrency exchange Binance facilitated nearly $1.7 billion in digital asset transfers linked to Iranian entities. Despite regulatory sanctions against Iran, Binance accounts connected to the country remained active for over a year, with compliance signals reportedly overlooked. The findings have drawn criticism of Binance’s oversight mechanisms and highlight ongoing challenges in enforcing sanctions and preventing illicit finance within the crypto ecosystem. This case stresses the importance of stronger regulatory controls and transparency in cryptocurrency exchanges to prevent misuse.

4. Schools Rethink Technology Use Amid Chromebook Backlash Some U.S. schools are scaling back reliance on Chromebooks and digital tools, citing concerns about distraction and ineffective learning. In parts of Kansas, students have returned to using traditional textbooks and pencils, with restrictions on access to YouTube and games on school devices. Several seventh graders reported preferring offline methods. This trend reflects a reassessment of technology’s role in education, indicating that technology integration may not always produce expected academic benefits and raising questions about balancing digital resources with conventional teaching tools.

5. Meta and YouTube Found Guilty of Addiction Claims in Social Media Case A landmark jury verdict held Meta and YouTube legally responsible for designing products that promote addictive user behaviors. The ruling could have wide-reaching implications for social media companies, potentially leading to increased regulatory scrutiny and demands for design changes to reduce harmful effects on users. The case draws attention to ethical and legal accountability in product development, particularly for platforms that significantly influence public attention and mental health.

6. Elon Musk’s Presence in Trump-Modi Call Highlights Shifting Influence Elon Musk participated in a rare phone call between former U.S. President Donald Trump and Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi. Musk’s involvement as a private citizen in this diplomatic conversation is unusual and suggests his improved relations with political figures. While the specifics of his role remain unclear, the event illustrates how prominent technology leaders continue to intersect with international politics and policy discussions.
