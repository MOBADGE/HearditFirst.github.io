Technology News Briefing – March 24, 2026

1. Tech Workers Intensify Use of AI, Driving Up Costs At several technology companies, employees are increasingly integrating artificial intelligence tools into their daily workflows. Some firms have introduced competitive leaderboards to track AI usage among staff, encouraging more frequent and extensive use. This “tokenmaxxing” behavior, as it’s called, has led to significant increases in cloud computing bills and AI service expenses. While the push to maximize AI use can boost productivity, it also raises concerns about cost management and sustainable deployment of these technologies in corporate environments.

2. New White House AI Policy Seeks to Override State Regulations The White House has announced a federal policy framework aimed at standardizing AI oversight and preempting state-level legislation. The guidelines, introduced under the Trump administration, focus on implementing consumer protections, including safeguards for children and measures to manage energy costs associated with AI systems. The federal approach intends to create a unified regulatory environment across the country, though it faces criticism from some states that view it as encroaching on their authority to regulate technology within their jurisdictions.

3. Arm Holdings Plans Direct Chip Sales for AI Data Centers Arm Holdings, traditionally known for licensing its chip designs rather than manufacturing hardware, is shifting strategy by preparing to sell its own computer chips specifically designed for artificial intelligence data centers. This move marks a notable change in the company’s business model and signals its intent to compete more directly in the hardware market amid growing demand for AI processing power. The development could impact the broader semiconductor industry, affecting partnerships and competition among chipmakers.

4. Legal Challenge Underway Over Hewlett Packard Enterprise’s $14 Billion Deal A federal judge is overseeing hearings related to a legal challenge mounted by Democratic state attorneys general against the Department of Justice’s approval of Hewlett Packard Enterprise’s (HPE) $14 billion acquisition deal. The states allege that the DOJ’s settlement was ineffective and lacked appropriate scrutiny, potentially harming market competition. The case highlights ongoing tensions between state regulators and federal authorities over oversight of major tech mergers and acquisitions.

5. Elon Musk Found Liable in Twitter Investor Lawsuit A jury has ruled that Elon Musk bears responsibility for financial losses suffered by investors in Twitter, now rebranded as X. Plaintiffs contended that Musk attempted to depress the company’s share price deliberately to renegotiate the terms of his $44 billion acquisition offer. The verdict represents a rare setback for Musk in legal disputes connected to his business dealings. The outcome may influence future litigation involving corporate acquisitions and shareholder rights.

6. Passing of Paul Brainerd, Desktop Publishing Pioneer Paul Brainerd, the creator of PageMaker software and a key figure in the development of desktop publishing, passed away at age 78. His contributions revolutionized the printing and publishing industries by allowing users to design and arrange text and graphics digitally, replacing manual paste-up techniques. Brainerd’s work laid the foundation for modern digital layout and design tools widely used in publishing today.
