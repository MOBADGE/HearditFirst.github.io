Technology News Briefing – March 19, 2026

1. Meta Scales Back Ambitions for the Metaverse Meta, the parent company of Facebook, announced significant changes that reduce its focus on the metaverse—Mark Zuckerberg’s vision of an immersive virtual reality world. The company’s latest moves have effectively limited the metaverse project to a marginal role, particularly affecting its VR platform Horizon Worlds. This shift follows years of heavy investment and mixed reception, highlighting the difficulties in building a large-scale, fully immersive digital environment. The downsizing suggests a retreat for Meta from a technology that so far has not achieved broad user adoption or clear commercial success.

2. Expansion and Risks of AI Digital Assistants New artificial intelligence bots are increasingly able to perform complex tasks beyond simple conversation. These AI agents can now edit documents, manage emails, book travel, and handle assorted digital workflows. However, experts warn that while these capabilities can enhance productivity, they also bring significant security and privacy risks. Malfunctions or misuse could lead to unintended errors or exposure of sensitive information. The advancement underscores the growing role of AI in personal and professional spheres, while raising questions about oversight and safeguards.

3. The Blurred Line Between Human and AI Interactions Developers and users of AI agents are exploring creative applications, including using them to simulate conversations with loved ones. Some young coders in Silicon Valley have experimented with AI that can impersonate relatives, such as a son’s voice or mannerisms, leading to mixed feelings about the technology’s impact on real relationships. This trend highlights broader societal concerns regarding the authenticity of AI interactions and its effects on human connection.

4. Pentagon and Silicon Valley Tensions Over Anthropic The U.S. government has identified the AI startup Anthropic as a national security risk, labeling the company a supply chain concern. Officials expressed doubts about whether Anthropic could be a “trusted partner” during wartime. This serious designation has led to a complicated situation where Silicon Valley firms offer discreet support to Anthropic while avoiding direct confrontation with government agencies. The dispute reflects ongoing challenges at the intersection of advanced technology development, government contracts, and national security.

5. Digital Resurrection: Val Kilmer’s AI Performance The estate of the late actor Val Kilmer collaborated with filmmakers to create a digital likeness of Kilmer using AI technology for the new movie “As Deep as the Grave.” Kilmer, who passed away last year from cancer, appears in the film through this AI recreation. This approach exemplifies how AI is being used in the entertainment industry to reproduce performances of deceased actors. The technique raises ethical and artistic questions about consent and the future of digital representation in media.

6. Google’s Position Strengthens in Defense AI Google has been rebuilding its relationship with the U.S. Department of Defense and is currently in a favorable position compared to some competitors facing controversies. As AI companies vie for military contracts, Google’s relatively stable standing may yield advantages in securing Pentagon projects. This situation illustrates the complex dynamics between major tech firms, government agencies, and evolving policies regarding AI’s role in national defense.

This overview reflects key technology developments from the past day, offering insight into evolving priorities and challenges in sectors ranging from virtual reality to AI and defense.
