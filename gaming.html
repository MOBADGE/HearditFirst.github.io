Finji Publisher Overwhelmed by AI-Generated Game Submissions

Finji, the indie studio behind titles like Tunic and Overland, reports a surge in AI-generated game pitches in its submission inbox. CEO Rebekah Saltsman expressed fatigue over the large volume of games, art, and resumes created with generative AI. Finji remains cautious about projects using AI; their updated contracts allow termination if undisclosed AI content is found. Saltsman emphasized that Finji values authentic game design and prefers pitches featuring programmer art and mechanics rather than AI-generated visuals. This stance highlights ongoing concerns about AI’s role in game development, including legal and creative risks. Finji continues to focus on traditionally crafted games such as their upcoming titles Usual June and 868-Back.

PlayerUnknown Productions Hosts Two-Day Modjam Exploring Game Modding Potential

PlayerUnknown Productions recently held an in-studio modjam event, inviting developers to modify their own game, Prologue: Go Wayback, over two days. The jam produced varied experiments, from converting temperate biomes into deserts to creating horror-themed modes and new mechanics like vehicles and a black hole that disrupts gameplay. Studio founder Brendan Greene sees this as a response to community demand for modding capabilities and a test-bed for future game updates. The event also reinforces the value of in-person collaboration in a largely remote development environment. While the concept builds on grassroots mod culture, it also reflects Greene’s broader vision of an open, player-driven gaming ecosystem.

Starfinder: Afterlight Shows Potential With Room for Improvement

Early playtests of Starfinder: Afterlight, a CRPG inspired by the Starfinder 2e tabletop RPG, reveal promising artwork and voice talent but also areas needing significant polish. Developed by newcomer Epictellers, the game captures core tactical elements from the tabletop system, such as action economy and positioning. However, many combat actions and tutorial content remain absent or incomplete in this early build, posing challenges for players unfamiliar with the rules. The visuals and story presentation receive praise, but the UI and gameplay mechanics require further development ahead of a planned public demo this summer. The game aims to appeal strongly to fans of Pathfinder and Starfinder systems while broadening accessibility.

V-color Launches DDR5 Memory Kits with Dummy Modules for Dual-Channel Appearance

Memory manufacturer V-color has introduced “1+1” DDR5 kits combining a functional DIMM with a matching dummy stick. These kits present the look of dual-channel memory setups while actually operating in single-channel mode. This approach aims to offer a lower-cost entry point into DDR5 despite the current premium prices and supply challenges affecting memory hardware. The marketing highlights the aesthetic appeal of paired modules and the option to upgrade later by adding a second functioning stick. However, real performance gains require two active DIMMs since single-channel mode limits bandwidth. Pricing and market adoption will determine the viability of these kits in a constrained memory market.

Former Sims 4 Lead AI Programmer Explains Sims’ Lack of Planning

David “Rez” Graham, lead AI programmer on The Sims 4, explained why Sims characters do not autonomously plan sequences of actions. Sims continually reassess choices moment-by-moment based on needs and motives rather than following long-term schedules, partly to avoid confusing players. Graham noted prior attempts to implement planning features were rejected because actions without clear player cues risked appearing as bugs or nonsensical behavior. Additionally, too much autonomy could reduce player engagement by turning Sims into passive observers rather than active storytellers. The balance between believable AI and player control remains a core design consideration for life simulation games.

Nvidia’s H200 AI GPU Now Approved for Import into China Amid Export Controls

Chinese authorities have approved imports of Nvidia’s H200 AI GPU, the full-capability version of Nvidia’s Hopper architecture, marking a shift from prior restrictions that limited exports to cut-down models like the H20. Despite this approval, the US government may still cap exports to Chinese companies at a limit of 75,000 H200 GPUs per entity, including those made by AMD. Nvidia has resumed production of the H200 to fulfill Chinese orders, important given China accounts for a significant portion of Nvidia’s revenue. The situation reflects ongoing geopolitical tensions balancing China’s AI development ambitions with US export controls aimed at limiting advanced technology transfers.
