WCW’s Costly Mortal Kombat-Inspired Gimmick Quickly Abandoned

In the 1990s, World Championship Wrestling (WCW), backed by billionaire Ted Turner, was known for lavish spending and some questionable creative decisions. One notable example is the Glacier gimmick, a wrestling character heavily inspired by Mortal Kombat’s Sub-Zero. WCW invested hundreds of thousands of dollars in costumes, pyrotechnics, and elaborate entrances costing around $400,000 each time. The character was promoted using video vignettes that closely mimicked Mortal Kombat’s style.

Despite the significant investment, the gimmick failed to gain traction, especially as competing storylines emphasizing realism like the New World Order (NWO) rose in popularity. Legal pressure from Midway, Mortal Kombat’s rights holder, forced WCW to quickly modify Glacier’s likeness and scale back the concept. The character lingered on as a low-tier performer before the gimmick was abandoned. This episode illustrates WCW’s excesses and the pitfalls of attempting obvious knockoffs without proper licensing.

Clint’s Role and Future as a Marriage Candidate in Stardew Valley

Stardew Valley fans have a new reason to focus on Clint, the town blacksmith, who is one of two new marriage candidates planned for the upcoming 1.7 update. While not currently eligible for marriage, Clint can be befriended from day one. He has a consistent schedule, mostly working behind his smithy counter and spending evenings at the Saloon.

Players looking to improve relationships with Clint can do so by gifting him items such as artichoke dip, rare gems, and smelted bars. Clint has fewer heart events compared to other characters but shares new recipes upon reaching friendship milestones. His birthday falls on Winter 26. The Stardew Valley 1.7 update will expand Clint’s role, making him available for marriage alongside another new candidate, Sandy.

Roblox Introduces Roblox Plus Subscription with New Monetization Features

Roblox has announced Roblox Plus, a subscription plan replacing Roblox Premium starting April 30. Subscribers receive discounts on in-game purchases (10% initially, rising to 20%), free access to private servers without individual fees, and the ability to send Robux without transaction fees—provided users are age-verified at 18 or older.

Notably, Roblox Plus removes the monthly Robux stipend and bonus found in Premium, requiring users seeking regular Robux access to purchase additional bundles at extra cost. The subscription incentivizes creators by paying them based on subscriber engagement in paid private servers and offering bonuses for recruiting subscribers. This change reflects Roblox’s focus on rewarding creators and potentially altering player spending habits, though it introduces new costs for subscribers.

US Government Targets Gamers to Address Air Traffic Controller Shortage

The US Department of Transportation has launched a campaign aimed at recruiting gamers to apply for air traffic controller positions. The effort uses gaming imagery and social media clips to connect with younger demographics believed to have skills relevant to the high-pressure role.

This push responds to a significant shortage of controllers, with approximately 4,000 fewer staff than targets as of late 2025. Recent government shutdowns worsened the staffing crisis, triggering increased retirements. The campaign highlights the career’s good pay—averaging $155,000 after three years—and accelerated hiring processes, though air traffic control remains a demanding job that requires strong mental resilience.

Pete Hines Reflects on Leaving Bethesda Amid Management Challenges

Pete Hines, Bethesda Softworks’ long-time marketing and PR executive, shared insights into his 2023 retirement after nearly 25 years with the company. Hines explained that he left because he felt unable to protect the studio and maintain its efficient operations amid changes he viewed as damaging.

Although he did not directly name Microsoft, which acquired Bethesda in 2020, his comments suggest dissatisfaction with post-acquisition management shifts. Hines praised Todd Howard and the Bethesda team for their ambition and the complexity of their games, emphasizing the need for respect for their open-world design despite occasional technical issues.
