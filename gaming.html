Intel Core Ultra 7 270K Plus Review: A Significant Refresh

Intel recently released the Core Ultra 7 270K Plus, part of its Arrow Lake Refresh line. Unlike typical refreshes that involve modest clock speed bumps, this chip features eight performance cores (P-cores) and sixteen efficiency cores (E-cores), matching the higher-end Ultra 9 285K in core count but with slightly increased clock speeds. The processor supports DDR5 memory up to 7200 MHz natively, improved interconnect clock speeds, and introduces Intel’s Binary Optimization Tool, which optimizes thread scheduling for better game performance.

In gaming benchmarks, the 270K Plus consistently outperforms previous Arrow Lake models and is competitive with many AMD Ryzen chips. While it doesn't surpass the Ryzen 7 9800X3D in gaming, it holds a strong position at a lower price point of $299. For content creators, it offers solid multi-threaded performance that, while not matching higher-end Ryzen 9 processors, provides good value for the price. The chip also demands more power and generates more heat than previous models, suggesting a robust cooling solution is necessary for heavy workloads.

Intel Core Ultra 5 250K Plus Review: More Cores and Better Performance at $199

Alongside the 270K Plus, Intel launched the Core Ultra 5 250K Plus, priced at $199. This model shares six P-cores with its predecessor, the 245K, but gains four additional E-cores, bringing it to a total of 18 cores and 18 threads. It also benefits from increased internal clocks for inter-tile communication, cache, and memory, enabling support for DDR5-7200 without overclocking.

Gaming tests show the 250K Plus performs similarly or slightly better than the Core Ultra 7 265K in some games, with notable improvements in titles like Baldur’s Gate 3 and Cyberpunk 2077, particularly in frame rate stability. Content creation tasks reveal strong single-core and multithreaded results, making it one of the top-performing processors in its price category. Its power consumption is moderately increased compared to the 245K, but remains manageable with mid-range cooling solutions.

Cat Parents Surpasses 100,000 Steam Wishlists in Three Days

The indie game Cat Parents has gained unexpected attention by amassing over 100,000 wishlists on Steam within just three days of its announcement. In this cozy simulation, players rescue and care for stray cats, customizing their home and daily routines. The game's rapid accumulation of wishlists places it among notable upcoming titles, signaling strong player interest for animal care and casual life simulation games. The developers expressed surprise and gratitude for this early success, though a release date has yet to be announced.

Intel VP Comments on Longer-Lasting CPU Sockets

Intel’s Enthusiast Channel VP, Robert Hallock, indicated a shift in Intel's approach toward motherboard socket longevity. Historically, Intel's platform sockets supported fewer CPU generations than AMD's AM4 or AM5 sockets, which afforded users more upgrade flexibility. Hallock expressed optimism that future Intel sockets will support more CPU generations, aligning more with customer expectations for platform stability. This comment follows the impending transition from the current LGA 1851 socket, used by Arrow Lake processors, to a new LGA 1954 socket for Nova Lake, meaning a required motherboard upgrade for users moving to the newest generation.
