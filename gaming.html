Indie Pass Subscription Service Faces Mixed Reception

A new subscription service called Indie Pass, launching April 13 for $6.99 per month, offers access to a catalog of 70 indie games curated from publisher indie.io’s library. Developers earn revenue based on how much time subscribers spend playing their games. While this model aims to improve discoverability for indie titles, it has met with criticism.

Concerns include the time-based compensation possibly favoring games designed to keep players endlessly engaged, the questionable value given many indie games are already inexpensive, and the sentiment against subscription services that prevent outright game ownership. Industry figures like 3D Realms co-founder George Broussard have called such services harmful to developers. Some players remain cautiously open if the revenue split is fair and it helps discover lesser-known games.

Indie Pass’s challenge will be convincing developers and players to adopt yet another launcher and subscription fee amid skepticism and discoverability issues. Its success may hinge on effectively balancing revenue, developer support, and consumer appeal.

Slay the Spire 2 Adds End-of-Run Badges in Latest Beta Update

The card game Slay the Spire 2 introduced a new badges feature in its recent beta patch. These badges appear at the end of each run to highlight unique achievements such as defeating bosses without damage or uncovering easter eggs. Developer Mega Crit plans to extend badge visibility to run history and stats screens later.

Besides badges, the update includes adjustments to map generation via Ascension difficulty, reworks of several character cards, enemy behavior changes, new visual effects, and numerous bug fixes. The patch aims to improve the distinctiveness of each run and refine game balance.

The beta received a mixed but notably less negative response compared to a previous balance update that prompted substantial review backlash. Players interested in trying the patch can access it through the public beta branch on Steam.

AI Startup Tesana Aims to Broaden Game Development

Tesana, an AI-driven game creation platform, lets users generate game code by typing descriptive prompts about environments, mechanics, and characters. After attracting around 10,000 paying customers since its recent launch, Tesana’s co-founder Johannes Vermandois envisions vastly increasing the number of people making games—from seasoned developers to newcomers.

Tesana integrates outputs from AI tools like Claude and asset generators to assemble playable games, though current results are rough and experimental. Vermandois sees such AI-assisted prototyping as a way to raise the quality and diversity of indie games by lowering entry barriers. He compares the appeal to enjoying human artists despite the availability of AI-generated music.

While skepticism remains about AI’s role in artistic creativity and market saturation, Tesana’s approach represents a growing trend of AI applied to game development, with implications for access and innovation in the medium.

The Gaming Historian Ceases Video Production, Shares Nintendo Legal Archives

Norman Caruso, known as The Gaming Historian, announced he is stepping back from creating his well-regarded documentary-style YouTube videos after more than 15 years. His departure follows burnout after producing an ambitious video on The Oregon Trail.

As a final gesture to fans, Caruso has uploaded a large collection of scanned court documents related to a famous 1982 trademark case between Universal Studios and Nintendo over Donkey Kong. These documents, previously researched but unpublished, are now publicly accessible on the Internet Archive. He also provided them to the Video Game History Foundation.

Caruso plans to continue his history work through podcasting but will no longer produce new video content. His extensive video catalog remains available online as a resource on video game history.

Skyblivion Lead Expresses Cautious Optimism Amid Potential Delay

Development of Skyblivion, the large-scale mod that remakes The Elder Scrolls IV: Oblivion with Skyrim’s engine, remains ongoing with a planned release in 2026 after a 2025 delay. The project team recently issued a call for additional experienced modders to assist with polishing the game.

Project lead Rebelzize stated all quest locations have been completed and quality-assured, and the major remaining obstacle—the Imperial City area—has progressed significantly. While acknowledging risks of unforeseen setbacks, he expressed cautious optimism about maintaining development momentum.

Rebelzize urged the community to be patient and considerate toward the team, emphasizing their commitment to delivering a functional game without compromising quality despite challenges of a small volunteer-based effort.
