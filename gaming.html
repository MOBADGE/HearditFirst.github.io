**Resident Evil Requiem’s Ending Choice Draws Criticism**

Capcom’s latest entry, Resident Evil Requiem, has been praised for its gameplay and overall quality. However, some players and critics find fault with its final choice, which revisits a controversial decision mechanic from Resident Evil 7. Near the game's conclusion, players controlling Grace must choose whether to release or destroy Elpis, an entity tied to the story's resolution. Choosing to release Elpis spares Leon and results in a favorable, canon ending involving a cure for infections and a bonus boss fight. Opting against releasing it leads to Leon's death and catastrophic consequences, clearly marked as the wrong choice.

This binary ending structure has frustrated some, echoing the divisive "save" choice in Resident Evil 7 where players had to choose between saving Mia or Zoe, affecting the game's canon and sequel continuity. Critics argue such forced choices can diminish player agency or create jarring narrative disparities. While the mechanic intends to add tension, it risks alienating players who dislike having a clearly 'bad' ending forced upon them. The discussion highlights ongoing challenges in game design regarding meaningful choices and narrative consistency.

**PNY GeForce RTX 5070 Ti OC Review Highlights Solid Performance**

PNY’s GeForce RTX 5070 Ti 16 GB OC graphics card presents a balanced option within Nvidia’s 50 series lineup, combining a high core count (8,960 CUDA cores) with ample 16 GB of GDDR7 memory. The card features a triple-fan cooling solution and a sturdy build with a metal backplate, designed to handle the 300W power envelope comfortably.

In benchmark testing across several modern games at various resolutions, the RTX 5070 Ti OC generally performs on par or slightly better than competing RTX 5070 Ti models, maintaining average frame rates suitable for 1080p and 1440p gaming. It runs warmer than some rivals, peaking around 70°C, and exhibits the highest power draw among tested RTX 5070 Ti cards, consuming up to 350W under load. The card’s boost clock averages 2,847 MHz in demanding games, reflecting strong overclocking headroom out of the box.

Priced around $1,000 (£840), the PNY offering sits mid-range for the 5070 Ti market. However, its price competitiveness is challenged by AMD’s RX 9070 XT, which delivers similar performance at notably lower cost. The review suggests buyers on the fence should consider the Radeon alternative, though the PNY card’s build quality and out-of-the-box speed make it a solid if not the most economical choice.

**Australian Gamer Denied Replacement for DDR5 RAM Amid Price Surge**

An Australian customer, Goran, who purchased a 32 GB DDR5 memory kit from retailer Umart in 2024, faced difficulties obtaining a replacement after the kit was found defective two years later. Despite Australian consumer laws obligating retailers to offer refund or replacement for major faults, Umart opted to refund only the original purchase price of around AU$155 (~$110), while the current price of the same kit has quadrupled to roughly AU$700 due to ongoing supply shortages.

Umart stated it lacked replacement stock and argued the issue did not meet the legal definition of a major failure, as the defect was remediable with a replacement of the same model. The customer was offered a refund or could pay the difference to upgrade to a current model, which raised concerns about fairness given the extreme price increase. Corsair, the actual manufacturer, confirms lifetime warranties on their DRAM kits and is reportedly investigating the case after public exposure through a YouTube channel.

This incident underscores the complications consumers face when hardware prices spike rapidly, and highlights tensions between retailer policies, legal obligations, and warranty servicing in the context of volatile component markets.
