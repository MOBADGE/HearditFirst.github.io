Lenovo Confirms Long-Term Support for Ryzen Z1 Handhelds

Concerns about AMD abandoning support for its Ryzen Z1 chip series in handheld gaming PCs were addressed by Lenovo today. The company officially stated it will continue supporting its AMD Ryzen Z1 Extreme-based Legion Go handheld with necessary driver and BIOS updates until at least October 2029. This announcement came after reports suggested driver update support for Lenovo’s original Legion Go had ended, raising questions about the chipset’s future. Lenovo emphasized collaboration with AMD on update cadence, signaling ongoing investment in this hardware platform. This reassurance matters for owners of Ryzen Z1 handhelds who rely on driver updates for optimal performance and longevity, especially as the original Legion Go remains relatively new and commands a premium price second-hand.

Lenovo Reveals Legion Go Fold: A Foldable Handheld PC Concept

At Mobile World Congress 2026, Lenovo showcased the Legion Go Fold, a concept handheld gaming PC featuring an 11.6-inch foldable screen situated between two detachable controllers. The device can function as a large portable gaming or productivity device with the screen used horizontally or vertically, or fold down to a smaller handheld size of 7.7 inches. It runs on an Intel Core Ultra 7 258V processor with 32 GB RAM and uses an integrated Arc 140V GPU, indicating modest gaming performance. The foldable device also converts into a laptop-like form factor with a built-in keyboard and trackpad. Lenovo describes the device as aimed at users wanting one portable machine for both work and gaming. The concept highlights ongoing experimentation with form factors in the handheld PC space, though no release date was provided.

RedOctane Revives Rhythm Gaming with Stage Tour

RedOctane, known in the early 2000s for Guitar Hero, returned last year under Embracer to launch a new rhythm game titled Stage Tour. The game resembles the gameplay style of Rock Band rather than Guitar Hero, focusing on band gameplay with multiple instruments and solo modes. It promises new features, live events, and seasonal content without plans for a traditional sequel, indicating a live service model. However, the game’s design and footage strongly echo rhythm games from nearly two decades ago, raising questions about its appeal amid a niche market. With crowdfunding for a PC Rock Band port having previously underperformed, it remains uncertain how Stage Tour will be received, though it is slated for a holiday 2026 release with a closed alpha test planned.

Cherry Xtrfy MX 8.2 Pro TMR TKL Keyboard Review

Cherry’s new MX 8.2 Pro TMR TKL keyboard employs advanced Tunnel Magnetoresistance (TMR) sensor technology, offering precise key actuation and lower power consumption compared to traditional mechanical or Hall effect switches. This wireless tenkeyless keyboard boasts high polling rates, hot-swappable switches, and innovative features like Dynamic Keystroke and rapid trigger, targeting competitive gamers seeking top performance. Despite these specs, its $250 price tag is considered steep given that other high-quality keyboards with Hall effect tech or mechanical switches are available at much lower prices. The keyboard’s firm build, satisfying typing experience, and long Bluetooth battery life are positives, but the premium cost may limit appeal primarily to esports enthusiasts or those valuing bleeding-edge technology.

Memory Prices Poised to Rise Again Amid Supply Constraints

Reports from South Korea indicate that major DRAM manufacturers Samsung and SK Hynix have notified customers about upcoming price increases in the second quarter of 2026 due to sustained high demand and constrained production capacity. Smaller suppliers are expected to accept significant price hikes to secure inventory, with some price increases potentially more than doubling compared to previous contract terms. This development follows ongoing memory shortages driven in part by increased AI computing needs, impacting prices throughout the PC and electronics supply chain. Analysts predict that rising DRAM and SSD costs will contribute to an estimated 17% increase in average PC prices this year alongside a projected 10.4% drop in shipments. This trend suggests that consumers seeking bargains on gaming hardware and PCs may face higher costs and limited availability in the near term.
