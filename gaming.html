Valve Faces Second Class-Action Lawsuit Over Loot Boxes

Valve is facing a second class-action lawsuit alleging that its use of loot boxes in games like Counter-Strike 2, Team Fortress 2, and Dota 2 constitutes illegal gambling. Filed shortly after a similar lawsuit from New York state, the complaint claims Valve designed loot boxes as a "carefully engineered revenue model" that targets consumers, including children, through casino-style mechanics. Players pay to open loot boxes with randomized contents that can sometimes be worth thousands of dollars but are typically much less valuable. The suit argues that the unlocking process mimics slot machines and meets Washington state’s legal definition of gambling, as users are essentially staking money on uncertain outcomes that yield valuable virtual items. Valve has faced regulatory scrutiny in European countries for loot boxes before, but this lawsuit marks potential for legal consequences in the US. Valve recently introduced a feature in Germany allowing players to see loot box contents before opening, signaling an attempt to comply with laws.

Nagoshi Studio Loses NetEase Funding Three Months After Gang of Dragon Reveal

Nagoshi Studio, founded by Yakuza creator Toshihiro Nagoshi after his departure from Sega, will lose NetEase funding in May, just three months after revealing their debut game, Gang of Dragon, at The Game Awards 2025. The studio’s project, which features actor Ma Dong-Seok in a crime syndicate-themed action game, remains vague with little gameplay information available. Bloomberg reports that NetEase withdrew after estimating that the game needed an additional $44.4 million to complete, and the studio has struggled to find alternative funding. NetEase has recently cut investment in several other Western studios amid broader cost reductions, making this another example of shifting priorities. Nagoshi Studio may continue development if it can secure new financial backing or buy out its assets from NetEase.

Bungie Integrates Numerous Halo-Inspired Weapons Into Marathon

Bungie’s new extraction shooter, Marathon, features several weapons clearly inspired by the Halo series, reflecting the studio’s history before separating from Microsoft. The game includes firearms such as the CE Tactical Sidearm, designed as an homage to Halo’s iconic pistol, and the BR33 Volley Rifle, which resembles Halo 2’s Battle Rifle, complete with attachments that approximate Halo multiplayer features. Other weapons like the V11 Punch echo the Plasma Pistol’s shield-draining mechanics. These callbacks are both nostalgic references and functional design choices within Marathon’s gameplay. Bungie has blended these classic elements with new mechanics, continuing its tradition of weighty movement and distinctive weapon handling. This design approach connects Marathon to Bungie’s past while offering fresh experiences.

Diablo 4’s Lord of Hatred Expansion Adds New Difficulty Tiers To Restore Challenge

Blizzard plans to address complaints about Diablo 4’s lack of meaningful challenge and reward progression in its upcoming Lord of Hatred expansion by expanding the existing four torment difficulty tiers to twelve. Currently, players can upgrade gear but often find no reason to increase difficulty as monsters can be defeated very quickly. The new difficulty tiers aim to create more incremental and meaningful challenges, making powerful damage numbers impactful by forcing players to adapt strategies. The highest difficulty will be very demanding, intended mainly for the most committed players. Blizzard is also adding a loot filter to manage the increased item drop volume and a crafting system with the Horadric Cube to enhance gear customization. These changes seek to improve long-term player engagement by making both difficulty and rewards more significant.

Steam Game Pickmon Copies Designs From Pokémon, Zelda, and Overwatch

A recently listed Steam game called Pickmon has drawn attention for its extensive and obvious copying of character designs from popular franchises including Pokémon, The Legend of Zelda, and Overwatch. The game features creatures closely resembling iconic Pokémon such as Pikachu and Meganium, as well as characters visually similar to Link from Breath of the Wild and Overwatch’s Roadhog. Pickmon attempts to distinguish itself mainly by altering minor details and changing the capture mechanic from balls to cards. While such imitation may not cross the threshold for legal action, it raises questions about originality and ethics in game design. This follows a precedent set by Palworld, another game criticized for borrowing heavily from Pokémon. The phenomenon reflects ongoing challenges in enforcing intellectual property rights within certain aspects of gaming.
