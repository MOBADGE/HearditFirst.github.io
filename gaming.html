Five New Steam Games Released This Week

Several new games have launched on Steam recently, offering a variety of experiences for PC players willing to explore beyond the usual headlines. Notable releases include:

- **Stiff Neck**: A visual novel blending psychological horror with a surreal, out-of-time setting. It centers on a small-town cop dealing with strange events including a cursed doll plague. This game may appeal to those who enjoy narrative-rich, text-heavy storytelling. - **Which Sausage, Mate?**: A life simulation about a Polish man who loses his mining job and struggles with retail work, budgeting, and making tough life choices. It features multiple endings influenced by player decisions about priorities such as food, rent, and medicine. - **Wasteland Bites**: Combines a post-apocalyptic setting with food truck management and combat against mutants and aliens. The goal is to reach a rumored safe beach, balancing culinary creativity with violent encounters.

- **It Has My Face**: A roguelike played from a first-person perspective where players seek out doppelgangers in a procedurally generated world. It supports both single-player and an online multiplayer mode pitting eight players against each other.

- **Grime 2**: A sequel to the 2021 metroidvania with a focus on invigorated, parry-based combat. Defeated enemies provide molds that become combat tools or attacks. It features apocalyptic exploration and cinematic boss fights.

These new releases offer diverse gameplay styles, from narrative and simulation to action and roguelike elements, catering to various player interests.

Pillars of Eternity Adds Turn-Based Combat Mode

Obsidian Entertainment has introduced a major update to **Pillars of Eternity**, adding a full turn-based combat mode alongside the original real-time-with-pause system. Released 11 years after the original game’s launch, this new mode limits players to one action per turn but allows multiple turns per round based on character stats like recovery. This addresses balance issues seen in the turn-based system of the sequel, Deadfire.

The update changes gameplay dynamics, making tactical decisions more significant and improving options for certain character builds such as Ciphers. Early impressions suggest the turn-based mode may provide a smoother experience for new players and fresh challenges for veterans. Obsidian’s lead designer has commented on the evolving appeal of turn-based versus real-time combat systems in RPGs.

This update signifies ongoing support and reimagining of classic games to meet modern player preferences.

Zelda: Twilight Princess PC Port Nears Completion

The fan-led project **Twilight Princess: Courage Reborn** has released a new showcase video highlighting progress on the PC port of *The Legend of Zelda: Twilight Princess*. This port is built from a complete decompilation of the original GameCube code, allowing native PC support including 60fps gameplay and mouse/keyboard inputs.

The project currently supports Windows, Mac, and Linux, with plans for an open-source release. While it requires players to provide original game assets, this fan effort aims to bring a popular Nintendo title to PC platforms for the first time.

Interest around the port is already high, with mods such as the addition of the character Linkle being developed even before release. This port reflects increasing capabilities of fan projects to revive and expand classic console games.

Linux Kernel to Drop Support for Intel 486 CPUs

After nearly three decades beyond the final Intel 486 CPU desktop releases, Linux kernel developers will begin removing support for this platform starting with Linux 7.1. Intel’s 486 line debuted in 1989 and officially ended in 1998 for general desktops, although it lingered in embedded systems until 2007.

Linus Torvalds and other maintainers cite the obsolescence of 486 hardware and the maintenance burden it imposes on the kernel. While the change likely affects very few users—if any—still running recent kernels on 486 machines, it represents one more step towards focusing development on modern hardware.

Support for later processors like Pentium remains intact for the foreseeable future, maintaining Linux’s broad compatibility with older but still relevant systems.
