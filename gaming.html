Magic: The Gathering Avoids Harry Potter Crossover, Revisits Strixhaven

Magic: The Gathering is returning to its original Strixhaven setting with the upcoming Secrets of Strixhaven expansion, rather than pursuing a Harry Potter crossover despite Hasbro's partnership with Warner Bros involving the Harry Potter universe. Strixhaven, introduced five years ago, presents a university of magic featuring a wider curriculum beyond typical wizardry classes, emphasizing diverse studies from art to zoology. This contrasts with the Hogwarts model that focuses mainly on magic disciplines and has been critiqued for its portrayal of dangerous student trials.

The Strixhaven setting appeals for its portrayal of young adult students choosing their magical colleges instead of being assigned houses arbitrarily. It also avoids certain high school tropes like Quidditch in favor of a more coherent sport concept named Mage Tower. The expansion will be available in Magic Arena from April 21, with physical releases following on April 24. This move signals a preference to develop the game’s original fantasy world rather than leaning heavily on external intellectual properties.

Crimson Desert Patch Adds Hide Helmet Feature and Quality of Life Improvements

Action RPG Crimson Desert received a new patch that introduces a frequently requested hide helmet option, allowing players to customize when their headgear is visible—in combat, in cutscenes, always, or never. This change improves aesthetic customization without impacting the protective benefits helmets confer in-game. The update also includes a new cat armor set and helmet, an expanded camp storage capacity to 1,000 slots, and a “classic” option for movement controls.

Since its launch, Crimson Desert has undergone multiple updates addressing performance and control issues. Despite some early controversies, such as the inadvertent use of AI-generated assets, the game has generally improved and found a steady player base. These ongoing refinements highlight the developer’s attention to player feedback and interest in polishing the single-player experience.

Early Footage Emerges of Elden Ring Movie Set in UK

A fan-uploaded video on TikTok may provide the first visual indication of the Elden Ring movie directed by Alex Garland. The footage shows a detailed ruined church set closely resembling the Church of Marika from the game’s world. It appears to be constructed by Hedgehog Construction near a known filming location in the UK, possibly Windsor Great Park.

Although unconfirmed, the set’s design and props strongly align with Elden Ring’s aesthetic. The movie remains largely under wraps, with little official information beyond casting rumors and Garland’s enthusiasm for the project, including his extensive script used to secure the directing role. The presence of this detailed set suggests active production but leaves many details about plot and relevance to the game’s story still unknown.

Datamined Elden Ring Cutscene Sheds Light on Miquella’s Backstory

Dataminer Lance McDonald has uncovered a work-in-progress Elden Ring cutscene featuring Miquella the Kind, a major DLC antagonist. The scene shows Miquella planting a sapling, the origin of the Haligtree, and using his own blood to nurture it. Dialogue suggests a vision of growth and abundance, addressed both to his twin (likely Malenia) and the player.

This content predates the DLC and hints at a more benevolent characterization of Miquella alongside his darker motives revealed later. The cutscene reinforces connections between the base game and expansion storylines, providing new context for longtime players interested in the lore and the complex relationships between characters.

Warframe Developer Commits to No AI-Generated Content in Games

Digital Extremes community director Megan Everett has firmly stated that Warframe and the upcoming Soulframe will not contain any AI-generated content, emphasizing a commitment to human-created art and assets. This stance contrasts with widespread adoption of generative AI tools across the gaming industry, where developers increasingly use AI for aspects like dialogue, level design, and voice acting.

Everett expressed frustration with AI-generated art that can be difficult to distinguish from human work and highlighted a recent incident in which AI fan art was mistakenly shared on a community stream. Despite growing skepticism and controversy surrounding AI use, Digital Extremes’ position reflects ongoing debates about preserving artistic integrity amid emerging technologies.
