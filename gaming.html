Blizzard’s Overwatch Update Inspired by Marvel Rivals

At this year’s Game Developers Conference (GDC), Overwatch 2 director Aaron Keller revealed that the multiplayer shooter Marvel Rivals influenced Blizzard’s recent major update to Overwatch. Keller noted how Marvel Rivals’ approach of launching with a large roster of heroes excited new players and sparked discussions among Blizzard’s team about dropping a substantial number of heroes at once. While Blizzard did not add 30 new heroes, they introduced five new characters simultaneously in the latest season, a larger batch than usual. This move appears to have contributed to a sustained increase in player counts, marking a positive shift in Overwatch’s revitalization efforts. Additional new heroes are planned for later this year, indicating continued investment to keep the game fresh and competitive.

Baby Steps Embraces Unconventional Level Design

Game designer Gabe Cuzzillo spoke at GDC about the philosophy behind Baby Steps, a climbing challenge game recognized as one of 2025’s best. Contrary to polished and heavily playtested designs typical in single-player games, Baby Steps intentionally incorporates an “indifference to the player,” leaving players to creatively interact with obstacles without clear guidance or consistent rewards. As a form of playful trolling, the developers placed piles of cans on top of climbing challenges they had not fully tested. Players managed to climb all but one of these can stacks, illustrating both the unpredictability of player behavior and the game’s open-ended design approach. This method questions traditional level design norms and emphasizes player discovery and community interaction.

Industry Divided Over Generative AI in Games

At GDC, a clear divide emerged between technology investors enthusiastic about generative AI and many game developers skeptical or hostile to the technology. Moritz Baier-Lentz, a venture capitalist involved in AI startups, expressed disappointment that the games industry is “demonizing” generative AI, viewing it as a promising new tool. However, a survey cited at the conference showed that over half of game developers consider generative AI harmful to the industry, with only a small minority supportive. Concerns include job security after recent layoffs, unauthorized use of artists’ work, questionable output quality, and ethical considerations. Industry representatives from companies like Nvidia acknowledged current limitations but emphasized AI as an assistive tool rather than a replacement for human creativity. The tension highlights ongoing uncertainty about AI’s role and impact on game development.

Bungie Addresses Player Feedback on Marathon’s Audio Changes

Following an update to the multiplayer shooter Marathon, players reported that increased sound range for gunfire and explosions made the game’s PvP experience overwhelming and frustrating. Bungie acknowledged the issue on social media, admitting that the audio change was an “overcorrection” and promising to adjust it in an upcoming patch. The goal was to provide players with more situational awareness, but extending sound range too far impacted gameplay negatively by prompting constant conflict and reducing strategic play options. Bungie’s response aims to strike a better balance so players can remain informed without the sound design feeling excessive.

Arc Raiders Director on Challenges of Live Service Games

Caio Braga, production director of the extraction shooter Arc Raiders, reflected at GDC on development challenges in the wake of several recent high-profile live service game failures. Braga emphasized the difficulty of releasing such games and expressed hope that other studios receive the same opportunities Embark had to pivot and improve their product. He pointed out that Arc Raiders benefited from creative freedom and a smaller team size, conditions not commonly afforded in large studios. Braga urged caution against treating successful live service releases as simple formulas, noting that game development requires flexibility and space for trials rather than strict adherence to expectations.

Balancing RPG Difficulty Is a Complex Task, Says Obsidian

Obsidian senior systems designer Robert Donovan discussed the challenge of setting difficulty levels in The Outer Worlds 2 during a GDC talk. Donovan highlighted how RPGs often become easier as players grow stronger, creating a reverse difficulty curve. The team sought a balance where “weak things are weak and strong things are strong,” aiming for meaningful character progression without marginal upgrades. Playtesters frequently found the game too easy, leading to a “hits to kill” approach to ensure combat felt fair and engaging. Donovan acknowledged that any difficulty setting will divide opinions but emphasized prioritizing a normal difficulty that suits most players. Adjustments were made in the game’s final stages, including increasing the final boss’s challenge, though the variation in player skill and preferences makes perfect balance elusive
