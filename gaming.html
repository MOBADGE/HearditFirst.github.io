**Growing Opposition to Generative AI Among Game Developers**

A recent GDC survey of over 2,300 game industry professionals reveals that 52% now view generative AI as having a negative impact on the industry, a sharp increase from 18% two years ago. The proportion of developers actively using generative AI tools remains steady at around 33%, with business and management roles more likely to employ AI than artists, designers, and programmers. Common applications include research, brainstorming, office tasks, and prototyping rather than direct creation of game assets. Opinions among developers are polarized: some see AI as a productivity aid, while others characterize it as unethical plagiarism that undermines creativity, with some stating they would rather leave the industry than use AI. These findings underscore ongoing tensions around AI‚Äôs role in game development and its implications for creative work.

**US Immigration and Gender Policies Affect International Game Developers‚Äô Travel**

The 2026 State of the Game Industry report highlights that nearly one-third (31%) of non-US game developers have canceled plans to travel to the United States due to current immigration and gender identity policies. Among LGBTQ+ respondents, this figure rises to 47%. These cancellations reflect concerns about safety and legal uncertainties amid recent US government actions restricting gender recognition and immigration enforcement. Additionally, 60% of non-US industry leaders and investors report that US policies have influenced their ability or willingness to engage in business with American companies. While GDC executive leadership has not yet observed a drop in attendance ticket sales, these travel hesitations could impact international networking and business opportunities within the US-based industry events, potentially affecting diversity and global collaboration.

**One-Third of US Game Industry Workers Laid Off Over Past Two Years**

Nearly half of those laid off have struggled to find new games industry jobs, with 36% of those laid off one to two years ago still without new employment.
