Steam's Top Sellers: Windrose Surpasses Pragmata

This week on Steam, Windrose, a pirate-themed survival crafting game, has unexpectedly outperformed Pragmata, Capcom’s high-profile sci-fi third-person shooter. Since its surprise early access release on April 14, Windrose has sold over 1 million copies and peaked at more than 220,000 concurrent players, making it the second highest-grossing game on Steam right now behind Counter-Strike 2. Pragmata, despite a strong concurrent player peak of nearly 69,000 and availability on multiple consoles, sits third in revenue.

Windrose’s success highlights the continued appetite among PC gamers for pirate-themed experiences after years of mixed reactions to notable titles like Sea of Thieves and Skull & Bones. Its straightforward naval combat and survival gameplay appear to resonate well with players, establishing a substantial player base early in its lifecycle. The performance also invites comparisons among live-service and early access games, underscoring how genre preferences and pricing can influence market reception.

New 5th-Generation OLED Gaming Monitors from Philips and AOC

Philips and AOC have announced new gaming monitors featuring 5th-generation QD-OLED panels, set for release in Q3 2026. These monitors address previous OLED shortcomings like font fringing while maintaining the technology’s advantages of deep contrast and high brightness. The AOC AGP346UCSD model offers a 34-inch, 360 Hz WQHD screen with HDMI 2.1 and DisplayPort 2.1, though its USB-C port provides only 15W power. The Philips Evnia 34M2C8600P trades some connectivity features for USB-C power up to 65W, a white chassis, and integrated Ambiglow RGB lighting.

Both monitors deliver peak brightness of 1300 nits with a 300-nit full brightness baseline and use V-RGB pixel substructure to improve visual clarity and HDR performance. Prices are approximately €999 ($1176) for the AOC and €899 ($1050) for the Philips unit. These options represent an expansion in OLED gaming monitors, giving consumers alternatives beyond existing models like MSI’s. The monitors aim to balance high performance with aesthetic and practical features, although US availability remains unconfirmed.

Byte Magazine Archive Now Available Online

Over 200 issues of Byte Magazine, a seminal computing publication active from 1975 to 1998, are now freely accessible via the Internet Archive. Byte chronicled the evolution of personal computing and remains a valuable historical resource, capturing the optimism and innovation of early computer technology. The collection includes iconic cover illustrations by Robert Tinney, offering a glimpse into the industry’s cultural context in past decades.

Though the archive has some gaps and lacks issues like the final July 1998 edition, the breadth of material allows readers to explore topics ranging from early hardware developments to software trends. The availability provides researchers and enthusiasts with an opportunity to study the roots of modern computing and to contextualize changes in how technology was understood and discussed over time.

Preservation of PC Gamer’s Demo Discs

The Internet Archive has also preserved 758 PC Gamer demo discs released over the years, making them available for download and play. These discs often contained demos, mods, and exclusive content not found in retail game releases, serving both promotional and archival functions. The collection spans floppy disks to more recent DVDs, featuring notable classics like Quake and Telltale’s Sam & Max.

This preservation effort offers a historical snapshot of PC gaming culture, showcasing how magazines like PC Gamer connected players with upcoming titles and community content. The discs capture a period when physical media and magazines played an integral role in game distribution and fan engagement, providing a resource for those interested in gaming history.
