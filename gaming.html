**Blockchain Gaming Absent from GDC 2026**

For the first time in several years, the Game Developers Conference (GDC) held in San Francisco features no sponsored talks or panels about blockchain gaming or NFTs. Previous years, including GDC 2023, showcased multiple sessions focused on blockchain integration in gaming, such as guides on building blockchain games and discussions on emerging blockchain futures. This year, the closest relevant content is a single talk on alternative payment methods mentioning digital wallets without direct reference to blockchain. The blockchain presence at the expo has also diminished, with just one blockchain-related banner spotted. Instead, generative AI has taken a prominent role, with numerous talks and exhibitors related to AI technology. However, surveys indicate that the majority of game developers attending GDC view generative AI skeptically, with over half considering it harmful to the industry.

**Valve Faces Additional Class-Action Lawsuit Over Loot Boxes**

Valve has been hit with a second consumer class-action lawsuit regarding loot boxes in titles like Counter-Strike 2, Team Fortress 2, and Dota 2. The suit alleges that Valve’s loot box systems exploit gambling mechanics through psychological tactics designed to extract money, including from minors. Players buy keys to unlock loot boxes for random virtual items, which can be highly valuable and sold on Valve’s Steam Community Market or third-party platforms. The complaint highlights that loot box opening resembles slot machine visuals and involves significant chance, fitting Washington state’s legal definition of gambling. Previous legal battles have generally failed to categorize loot boxes as gambling, but this case leverages the visible real-world cash value of in-game items. Valve has responded with compliance efforts in some regions, like introducing a system for German players to preview loot box contents. The lawsuit comes amid ongoing regulatory scrutiny but represents an escalation in legal pressure facing Valve.

**Nagoshi Studio Loses NetEase Funding Months After Game Reveal**

Nagoshi Studio, led by Toshihiro Nagoshi, creator of the Yakuza series, will lose financial backing from NetEase in May. This follows the studio’s debut project, Gang of Dragon, being revealed at the 2025 Game Awards three months ago. Initially touted as a project bigger than Yakuza, the scope was later reduced, but little additional information about the game has been released beyond a brief cinematic trailer featuring actor Ma Dong-Seok. According to reports, NetEase decided to cease funding after determining the project requires an additional $44.4 million to finish, and Nagoshi Studio has been unable to secure alternative investors. Although NetEase allows the studio to continue work, it requires the buyout of associated assets or branding. This move fits a broader pattern of NetEase cutting support for multiple Western studios following a previous period of heavy investment.

**Bungie’s Marathon Features Multiple Weapons Inspired by Halo**

Bungie’s new extraction shooter, Marathon, includes several weapons clearly inspired by the studio’s earlier Halo series. Some in-game guns closely resemble iconic Halo firearms both visually and mechanically. For example, the "CE Tactical Sidearm" mirrors the Halo Combat Evolved pistol in design and function, while the "Magnum MC" reflects the classic Halo Magnum’s impact and recoil. Another weapon, the "V11 Punch," operates similarly to Halo’s Plasma Pistol, complete with shield-draining charged shots. Additional weapons like the "BR33 Volley Rifle" and "V22 Volt Thrower" have rare attachments that further evoke Halo’s Battle Rifle and Needler respectively. The incorporation of these design elements points to Bungie maintaining stylistic and gameplay themes from its past franchises, blending familiar mechanics into Marathon’s new context.

**Blizzard Expands Diablo 4 Difficulty to Reinstate Meaningful Challenge**

Ahead of the upcoming Lord of Hatred expansion for Diablo 4, Blizzard plans to increase the game’s torment difficulty tiers from four to twelve. Current difficulty levels increase monster health, damage, and loot quality but often fail to make gear upgrades feel impactful, with players quickly reaching a stage where combat becomes trivial despite flashy damage numbers. The new higher torment tiers aim to provide a near-impossible challenge, encouraging players to use their growing power more effectively and enabling meaningful progression through incremental difficulty adjustments. Designers emphasize the strategy behind difficulty as an opt-in risk/reward choice, with the highest levels intended to be aspirational rather than mandatory.
