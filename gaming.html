Hardware Hacking: Framework Laptop Inside a 2006 MacBook

A creator known as Edoogg successfully transplanted a modern Framework Laptop 13 motherboard, featuring an Intel Core i7 1280P and 64 GB DDR4 RAM, into the shell of a 2006 black MacBook. The project, which took three months and involved learning soldering and 3D modeling, required notable modifications such as soldering USB cables to original keyboard and trackpad components, using super glue to mount parts, and installing a custom LED to mimic the Apple light on the laptop lid. Edoogg chose the old MacBook model purely for its aesthetic and rarity rather than technical benefits. While the build is functional, the creator admits some jankiness remains and suggests that future iterations would benefit from custom PCBs and better mounting solutions. This DIY project reflects the ongoing interest in repurposing vintage hardware with modern internals but also highlights the technical challenges involved.

Linux on PS5: A Modder Brings Steam Machine Capability

Software engineer Andy Nguyen demonstrated that the PlayStation 5 Slim can run Linux, effectively turning the console into a Steam Machine. Using a known exploit to bypass Sony’s stringent security (limited to older firmware versions), Nguyen installed Ubuntu and successfully ran demanding games like GTA 5 Enhanced with ray tracing enabled. The key to this achievement was adapting open-source Mesa drivers to support the PS5’s customized AMD RDNA GPU. While the project faces limitations including thermal throttling and incomplete hardware monitoring, it showcases the modding community’s ability to transform locked-down consoles into more versatile gaming PCs. However, given firmware updates can patch the exploit, only users on affected firmware can attempt the procedure. The development also raises questions about the potential visibility of PS5 hardware stats in PC-centric databases.

Marathon’s Thief Drone Gets Deadly with Claymores

In the extraction shooter Marathon, players have discovered that attaching claymores to the Thief Class’s remote-controlled pickpocket drone creates a potent new offensive tactic. Normally a utility class focused on looting and evasion, the Thief’s drone is now being used to deliver explosive landmines at players from unexpected angles, increasing its lethality significantly. Claymores have a generous blast radius, allowing skilled players to bait or trap opponents effectively. This creative use of game mechanics could elevate the strategic value of the Thief class, though it also increases frustration for those on the receiving end. The current workaround might be to increase drone noise to alert players, but developers have yet to comment on whether they will address this interaction.

Master Chief Voice Actor Objects to White House Video Use

Steve Downes, the voice actor for Master Chief in the Halo series, has publicly demanded that the White House remove his voice from a propaganda video posted on their official social media account. The short video titled “JUSTICE THE AMERICAN WAY” features various pop culture figures’ images and voices to promote support for the ongoing war between the US, Israel, and Iran—a conflict responsible for significant civilian casualties according to reports. Downes stated he was not consulted and does not endorse the use of his voice or the video’s message, calling it “disgusting and juvenile war porn.” The video remains online at this time. This incident follows similar disputes where performers have opposed government use of their work for political messaging without consent.

Counter-Strike 2 Introduces X-Ray Scanner for Loot Containers in Germany

Valve will launch an “X-ray scanner” feature for Counter-Strike 2 players in Germany starting March 16, allowing them to see the contents of loot containers before purchase. However, once a container’s contents are revealed, players must buy the revealed item to scan another container, preventing them from selectively picking preferred items across multiple containers. This system, previously introduced in France in 2019, aims to comply with regional loot box regulations rather than overhaul the container mechanics. It addresses legal concerns around gambling-like practices by making the opening process more transparent but does not reduce costs or alter drop probabilities. The move reflects increasing regulatory pressure on loot boxes in gaming markets worldwide.
