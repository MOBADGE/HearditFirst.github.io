**Code Vein 2 Review: A Soulslike with Accessible Combat and Mixed Execution**

Code Vein 2, released on January 29, 2026, offers a standalone action RPG experience set in a gothic post-apocalyptic world with vampire-like Revenants. Despite its complex-sounding time travel storyline revolving around killing legendary heroes to prevent a double apocalypse, the gameplay centers on straightforward hack-and-slash combat with some soulslike elements such as limited healing and invincible dodges.

The game is notable for its accessible combat that favors light attacks, allowing players less skilled in precise timing to progress without needing to master heavy attacks or special skills. Players can customize weapons and stats to create varied playstyles, although this depth feels largely optional. Accompanying AI companions independently contribute to combat, enhancing a fast-paced, aggressive approach.

Visually, the game features a mix of striking environments and diverse dungeon settings ranging from prisons to sunken cities. However, the open world‚Äôs promise falls short, as time travel does not significantly alter the landscape, and much exploration involves repetitive, unengaging item gathering. Technical issues, such as inconsistent frame rates even on capable hardware, negatively impact the experience.

Narratively, Code Vein 2 attempts emotional depth through operatic tragedy and character connections but relies heavily on anime clich√©s and short cutscenes, limiting player investment. The soundtrack receives praise but cannot fully compensate for texture pop-ins and storytelling shortcomings. Overall, the game presents an enjoyable action RPG under the surface but is weighed down by design and technical flaws.

**Out of Action: A New Multiplayer FPS with Classic Features and Advanced Movement**

Out of Action, launched recently in early access by solo developer Doku Games, is attracting attention as a refreshingly straightforward multiplayer first-person shooter. Despite being a work in progress without full polish, it combines core FPS features such as 12-player matches, team and free-for-all modes, objective gameplay, deep loadout customization, and a server browser supporting dedicated servers‚Äîfeatures often missing in modern shooters.

The game's movement system stands out, allowing players to chain actions like dodging, diving, rolling, sliding, wall running, and double jumping. Although there is no sprint function, navigating maps effectively requires mastering movement combos. Combat is challenging and unforgiving, with small hitboxes, bullet travel mechanics, and no aim assist. This results in a skillful gameplay experience reminiscent of older titles like Tribes and GunZ: The Duel.

Out of Action currently has a modest but active community, and server lobbies persist beyond matches, making it easy to continue playing with others. While still needing more maps and modes, the game's approach to combining classic FPS fundamentals with modern mechanics offers a promising alternative to bigger releases, especially for players seeking skill-based gunplay without microtransactions or complicated systems.

**Arc Raiders: Trophy Display Project Detailed**

The Headwinds update for the third-person shooter Arc Raiders introduces the Trophy Display, a new long-term player project focused on collecting parts from various Arc machines. Players can donate specified robot parts across five progressive stages, each requiring different materials sourced from distinct enemy types and areas within the game world.

Completion rewards include valuable blueprints for weapons and gear, Raider Tokens, rare items such as a guitar, and emotes. However, given that the upcoming Expedition system resets player progress, it remains unclear whether these rewards will persist after account resets. Players may want to consider timing their project completion to maximize reward retention.

This project adds an additional layer of content for dedicated players and encourages exploration and combat across the game's diverse zones.

**GOG Galaxy to Embrace Linux with AI-Assisted Development**

GOG has announced Linux as its next major development frontier for its GOG Galaxy client software. A recent job posting seeks a senior software engineer to help develop and optimize the client with Linux support, a significant move given the platform's historical ambivalence toward Linux.

While this news is positive for Linux users who prefer DRM-free games and ownership-focused digital storefronts, the job posting also raises some concerns. It mentions the need to "actively use and promote AI-assisted development tools," although the exact implications of this remain unclear. GOG has not provided further details on how AI will be integrated into their development processes.

Overall, this marks a renewed commitment to Linux support, which could expand GOG's user base, but also highlights the growing role of AI in software development workflows.
