**Peak’s New Update Adds Extensive Customization Options** The climbing adventure game Peak has released a significant update called the "Play It Your Way Update," offering players a broad range of custom settings. These include toggling various game challenges such as fall damage, spiders, hunger, and weather effects like snow storms or wind. Players can also modify the item pool, such as restricting or allowing specific tools like the blowgun. A notably casual option named "Grapple Mode (Stupid)" grants unlimited use of a rescue claw, effectively removing many challenge elements for a more relaxed experience. Additionally, a mini run mode lets players tackle a single biome rather than the entire expedition, catering to those with limited time. These features are separated from the main game mode and do not provide standard rewards, focusing instead on freedom and experimentation. *Why this matters:* The update significantly broadens how players engage with Peak, accommodating different playstyles and time commitments, potentially making the game more accessible.

**FTC Addresses Payment Processors Over Content Restrictions** The U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) issued warning letters to Mastercard, Visa, PayPal, and Stripe addressing concerns about financial services denying access based on customers' political or religious views. This follows Valve’s disclosure that Mastercard-related policies pressured Steam to delist several adult-themed games. Mastercard denied direct involvement, attributing the removals to payment processors applying Mastercard’s rules on "illegal or brand-damaging transactions." FTC Chair Andrew Ferguson emphasized that access to financial services is essential for participation in commerce and public life. However, the FTC clarified that the letters mainly reiterate an existing executive order concerning financial institutions’ roles in political surveillance and do not guarantee changes to current practices. *Why this matters:* While the FTC’s action brings attention to how payment networks intersect with content regulation, its limited scope leaves ambiguity about how adult or politically sensitive games will be treated moving forward.

**‘Too Many F*cking Nazis’ FPS Launches Amid Controversy** A new first-person shooter titled "Too Many F*cking Nazis" debuted on Steam last week. The game’s premise involves fighting hordes of time-traveling Nazis using a variety of inventive weapons. The title’s blunt language has propelled heated discussions on the Steam community forums, drawing criticism from some users who perceive the use of "Nazi" as a politically charged slur. Despite the forum backlash, developer comments suggest early development with plans to diversify enemy types. The game aligns with a recent trend of titles purposefully including World War II Nazis as enemies, offering players cathartic combat encounters rather than historical simulations. *Why this matters:* The game reveals ongoing tensions in gaming communities about political language and content sensitivity, while also indicating a continued player interest in direct combat against WWII-era antagonists.

**Eidos Montreal Lays Off 124 Employees, Studio Head Departing** Eidos Montreal has laid off 124 staff members across production and support teams as part of a strategic realignment towards supporting external projects like Grounded 2 and the Fable reboot. Longtime studio head David Anfossi, known for work on Deus Ex and Shadow of the Tomb Raider, has also left the company, though details of his departure are unclear. This follows multiple rounds of layoffs dating back to early 2024 and reported cancellations of in-house projects including a rumored new Deus Ex title. The cuts come amid broader industry-wide reductions, with other major studios like Epic Games and Ubisoft also announcing layoffs in March. *Why this matters:* The layoffs at Eidos Montreal reflect ongoing challenges in the games industry related to project shifts and cost control, underscoring the precarious nature of studio employment and project sustainability.
