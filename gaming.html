Beyerdynamic Releases MMX 150 Wireless Gaming Headset

Beyerdynamic has introduced the MMX 150 Wireless, a wireless gaming headset priced at $230, positioning it as a more affordable option compared to its higher-end wired sibling, the MMX 330 Pro. The MMX 150 features 40mm drivers acclaimed for their warm and detailed sound, plush velour earpads, a well-padded headband, and a sturdy 2.4 GHz wireless connection complemented by Bluetooth 5.3 support. Battery life is solid at around 50 hours.

While comfort and sound quality are highlights, the headset's build quality shows some signs of compromise, with grainy earcup materials and flimsy plastic controls. Additionally, it lacks active noise cancellation‚Äîa feature typically expected at this price point. Its microphone performs well but does not benefit from dedicated software for noise cancellation or sound customization. The MMX 150 Wireless is more focused on delivering core high-quality audio and comfort rather than extensive features, which places it in direct competition with other mid-to-high-end gaming headsets from brands like Razer and Fractal Design.

Arc Raiders Considers Changes to Blueprint System Amid Player Feedback

Embark, the developer behind the third-person shooter Arc Raiders, is evaluating adjustments to how blueprints are handled in the game following persistent community concerns. Blueprints, which unlock key gear and significantly affect player progression, currently reset completely after optional in-game Expeditions, causing frustration due to the reliance on RNG in obtaining them.

Design director Virgil Watkins acknowledged the idea of allowing players to carry some blueprints beyond Expeditions remains under consideration but emphasized the challenge in balancing such a change. Instead, Embark is exploring alternatives focused on providing players with more deterministic ways to acquire and learn blueprints, potentially easing frustration without tying the system closely to Expedition resets. This approach aims to make progression smoother and less dependent on luck, while maintaining game balance.

Internet Archive Director Responds to Site Blocks Amid AI Concerns

Mark Graham, director of the Internet Archive, addressed recent instances where websites like Reddit, The New York Times, and The Guardian have blocked the organization's Wayback Machine from accessing their content. These blocks respond to worries about data being scraped for AI training purposes. Graham described these concerns as "understandable but unfounded," noting that the Internet Archive implements rate limiting, filtering, and monitoring measures to prevent large-scale data scraping.

He stressed that blocking archival efforts risks significant harm to the public record, resulting in loss of transparency and historical data crucial for journalists and researchers. While acknowledging the tension between protecting paywalled content and preserving internet history, Graham argues that restricting archival access could inadvertently fragment the web and impair future accountability.
