Crimson Desert Players Request Permanent Exotic Mounts

Since the release of *Crimson Desert* last week, players have expressed frustration over the game's temporary system for exotic mounts such as bears, dodo-like kuku birds, and dragons. While early marketing suggested these creatures could be domesticated as permanent mounts, in practice any non-horse mounts are only rideable for limited periods and cannot be stabled permanently. For example, players can only ride bears briefly after defeating them in combat before they turn hostile again. Dragon mounts are limited to a short summoning once per hour.

Players on forums and Reddit have been petitioning developer Pearl Abyss to reconsider this design choice, arguing that since *Crimson Desert* is a single-player game, allowing permanent exotic mounts would not unbalance multiplayer competition. The ongoing dissatisfaction highlights the tension between player expectations shaped by marketing and final game design decisions. It remains to be seen if future updates will expand mount options beyond horses.

Capcom Clarifies AI Asset Use in Development

In a recent investor briefing, Capcom stated it will not incorporate AI-generated assets directly into its game content. However, the company plans to actively use generative AI tools to improve efficiency and productivity within its development workflows, covering areas such as graphics, sound, and programming.

This clarification addresses concerns raised by the use of AI in gaming, especially following controversial examples such as Nvidia’s DLSS 5 altering character appearances in *Resident Evil Requiem*. Capcom’s approach reflects a cautious stance: AI may support early-stage ideation and process optimization but won’t replace handcrafted game assets. How exactly the company balances AI-assisted work versus final asset creation remains an open question.

Elon Musk Found Liable for Misleading Twitter Investors

A Californian jury has ruled that Elon Musk misled investors with false or misleading tweets during his 2022 acquisition of Twitter. While the jury found Musk did not intentionally defraud shareholders, two tweets from May 2022 were found to have materially affected Twitter's stock price, causing some investors to sell shares at undervalued prices.

The class action lawsuit argued Musk aimed to lower the share price for renegotiating his $44 billion buyout offer. Musk plans to appeal, with potential damages estimated as high as $2.6 billion. This ruling is significant for investor protection and highlights the legal risks of high-profile figures making public statements that influence financial markets.

Baldur’s Gate 3 Romance Stories Emerge From Shared Narrative Arcs

Writers for *Baldur’s Gate 3* have revealed that the romantic storyline between characters Shadowheart and Lae’zel developed organically because both scripts explored similar themes from different perspectives. Both characters grapple with their backgrounds and question the systems that shaped them, which allowed their initial antagonism to evolve naturally into romance.

The development of these characters and their story arcs relied heavily on player feedback during early access to refine personalities and interactions. This insight illustrates the challenges of crafting nuanced relationships in RPGs where player choice and character depth must align to feel authentic.

Nintendo Switch 2 Will Support Replaceable Batteries in EU, Highlighting Repairability Trends

Nintendo announced it will re-release the Switch 2 in the European Union with replaceable batteries for both the console and Joy-Con controllers. This change responds to new EU regulations mandating that portable batteries must be easy for consumers to replace without specialized tools.

Currently, the Switch 2 batteries are glued inside, making replacements difficult. Nintendo will continue selling the glued-in battery model in other regions. The European move reflects broader "right to repair" laws aimed at reducing electronic waste and promoting longer device lifespans. Industry observers expect other handheld gaming PC manufacturers to follow Nintendo’s lead to comply with similar legislation.
