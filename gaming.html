Peter Molyneux Reflects on the Decline of God Games

As the early access launch of *Masters of Albion* approaches, legendary game designer Peter Molyneux shared his thoughts on the god game genre, which he helped pioneer with *Populous*. Molyneux expressed sadness that god games have largely fallen out of favor in recent years, noting they feel stuck in the late 1990s and early 2000s. While other genres like shooters and RPGs have evolved significantly, god games have not kept pace, reflected even in their user interfaces. He attributed this decline to a cycle where publishers' lack of interest discourages developers, and vice versa. In response, Molyneux’s studio 22cans is self-publishing *Masters of Albion* as both a swansong and a potential attempt to revive interest in the genre.

Windows Task Manager Creator Accidentally Embedded Phone Number in Legacy Code

Dave Plummer, who developed the original Windows Task Manager during his time at Microsoft in the 1990s, recently revealed that he unintentionally left his home phone number in the app’s code. This came about when he was troubleshooting an elusive bug where CPU usage readings sometimes exceeded 100%. To facilitate debugging, he added an "assert" that would trigger a fault if CPU usage was too high, including his contact details so others could inform him directly. Although this version was shipped in a beta release used by thousands or more, Plummer never received a call. The incident highlights the informal engineering practices common in early software development at Microsoft and serves as an intriguing piece of computing history.

*Crimson Desert* Players Embrace Superhero-Themed Cosplay

Players of the action RPG *Crimson Desert* have been creatively using the game’s dye and gear customization to transform the protagonist Kliff into various iconic superheroes. Community members have fashioned Kliff to resemble characters such as Superman, Spider-Man, Captain America, Doctor Strange, Doctor Doom, and others using in-game items and dye colors. This trend showcases the flexibility of *Crimson Desert*’s customization system and the enthusiasm of its player base, enriching the gameplay experience through imaginative roleplay, despite some of the system’s complexity.

*Titanium Court* Review: A Complex Blend of Match-Three and Roguelite

*Titanium Court* is a new indie game releasing April 23 that combines match-three puzzles with elements of roguelites, deckbuilding, autobattlers, and visual novels. Players are transported to a mysterious world torn by an endless war between two factions, with gameplay revolving around resource management via match-three tile mechanics and strategic card-based battles. The writing is noted for its humor and surreal tone. However, the review points out that the match-three component can feel cumbersome, as its inherent randomness sometimes undermines strategic planning. Overall, *Titanium Court* is praised for its originality and narrative but may not appeal to those who dislike match-three puzzle gameplay.

Nvidia RTX 60-Series GPUs: Speculation Based on Past Trends

Ahead of Nvidia’s next generation of RTX 60-series graphics cards, analysis of the last decade’s GPU launches suggests these cards will see modest improvements rather than dramatic leaps. Nvidia currently uses TSMC’s N3 node for chip production and is expected to continue with a similar process, offering better transistor density but limited improvements in cache size due to SRAM scaling challenges. Predictions estimate a 30-50% increase in shader core count and floating-point performance compared to the RTX 50-series, with no major increases in VRAM beyond the use of faster 3 GB GDDR7 modules. Tensor and ray tracing cores will likely be enhanced in capability but constrained by die size and architecture considerations. The RTX 6090 will probably be the high-end focus due to AI computing demands, while other models may prioritize cost-efficiency.
