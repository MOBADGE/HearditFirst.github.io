AI Companies Clone Journalists Without Permission

An AI company affiliated with Grammarly launched a tool that mimicked the writing and editing style of various experts, including journalists, without their consent. The software, released seven months ago under Grammarly’s AI brand Superhuman, produced generic and often poor editing advice by scraping and regurgitating the experts’ published work. This sparked backlash and led to a class-action lawsuit against Grammarly. Following this, another AI firm, GPTZero, approached journalists offering to create similar AI-based “expert” reviewing tools but for a fee of around $2,000. The ethical and legal implications are notable, as these AI models rely heavily on scraping copyrighted material, a practice currently under multiple legal challenges. This situation highlights the growing tension between AI companies and content creators over unauthorized use of human intellectual labor.

Silksong Player Reveals Why a Cut Boss Fight Was Removed

A player of *Silksong* discovered a hidden, unfinished variant of The Last Judge boss fight intended for the game’s secret third act. By modding leftover code, the player revealed that the “voided” version of The Last Judge, though visually altered with blackened armor and void-themed attacks, was largely underdeveloped. Some new moves were either ineffective or poorly positioned, making the encounter less challenging than expected for a late-game fight. This discovery provides insight into Team Cherry’s development process and explains why the boss was likely removed: the content was incomplete and not suitably polished for release.

Slay the Spire 2 Faces Review Bombing Over Beta Patch Changes

Following the release of a significant balance patch to the beta branch of *Slay the Spire 2*, over 9,000 negative reviews appeared on Steam within a day, despite the patch not being live on the main version. The nerf to a key card, Prepared (renamed Prepare), and increased difficulty from enemy buffs were the main points of contention. Interestingly, many negative reviews came from players using Simplified Chinese. Restrictions in China block Steam’s community features and Discord, limiting avenues for feedback. Consequently, some players may resort to negative reviews as their primary form of protest. The review bombing skews the game's overall Steam ratings in China, contrasting with the generally positive reception elsewhere.

OpenTTD Development Team Responds to Atari’s Intervention

*OpenTTD*, the free and open-source remake of *Transport Tycoon Deluxe*, was recently pulled from Steam and GOG due to Atari’s commercial re-release of the original 1995 game. The OpenTTD team clarified that the project remains fully independent and continues to be available for free on its own website. They emphasized that Atari has cooperated on the transition and even agreed to contribute to server maintenance costs. OpenTTD remains accessible outside major storefronts, and the developers ask fans to engage respectfully despite mixed reactions among the community. The situation underscores the complexities of open source projects interacting with official IP holders.

Subnautica 2 CEO Criticizes Krafton's Early Access Announcement

Ted Gill, recently reinstated CEO of Unknown Worlds (developer of *Subnautica 2*), expressed frustration over Krafton’s announcement of the game’s early access release date in May. The announcement, made without Gill's prior knowledge, has complicated community coordination and marketing plans. Krafton argued the announcement was planned before the court reinstated Gill, but legal disputes continue over leadership and development direction. The timing is sensitive because of a $250 million earnout tied to meeting release targets. Gill’s team highlights concerns about the development status and gaining control over launch decisions, signaling ongoing tension between Krafton and Unknown Worlds.

Industry Survey Finds Support for More AI Use Disclosure on Steam

A survey of 826 game industry professionals revealed that nearly 90% support requiring developers to disclose generative AI usage in games on Steam. Launched after Valve's January update focusing on AI-generated content visible to players, the survey found many developers want stricter disclosure policies including detailed checklists of AI applications. Most respondents reported no AI use within their studios, but for those who did, common uses were brainstorming and coding assistance rather than creating assets or audio. The findings indicate a push within the industry for greater transparency around AI in games, which may influence future platform policies.
