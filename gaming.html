Resident Evil Requiem: In-Game Detail and Sales Milestone

An out-of-bounds camera view in Resident Evil Requiem has revealed that a character named Grace Ashcroft appears to be browsing a fictional Reddit-style forum called r/amateurjournalists during a flashback scene. Posts seen on the laptop screen reference key events and entities from Resident Evil lore, such as the Baker family and government experiments, connecting the game’s story to previous installments. This small detail contributes to the narrative depth and fan engagement by incorporating real-world internet culture into the game world.

Meanwhile, Resident Evil Requiem has achieved significant commercial success shortly after its release. Within just four days of launch, the game has sold over five million copies, placing it among Capcom’s top 20 best-selling titles of all time. Its Steam launch set records with 270,000 concurrent players, surpassing other notable releases in the series. This early sales performance underscores the strong player interest, particularly for single-player horror games, and solidifies the franchise’s ongoing viability in the market.

Copyright Dispute Clears Way for Preservation of Cookie’s Bustle

Cookie’s Bustle, a little-known Japanese point-and-click adventure game from 1999, has finally become available online after years of suppression due to copyright claims. The Video Game History Foundation (VGHF) successfully defended its right to archive and share materials related to the game, pushing back against Graceware, a company that had issued multiple DMCA takedown notices. Cookie’s Bustle had been effectively erased from online communities because of aggressive copyright enforcement despite the absence of the original developers and ambiguous ownership status.

The game itself is noted for its unusual story involving a girl transformed into a teddy bear competing in a surreal sporting event. Thanks to this preservation effort, new Let’s Play videos and archival information are now accessible to players and scholars. This case illustrates ongoing challenges in video game preservation when legal uncertainties and copyright enforcement threaten access to historically significant titles.

Highguard Adds Final Content Before Shutdown

Though Highguard’s servers will shut down on March 12, the game recently received a sizable update introducing new gameplay elements. The update features a new healer character named Koldo, who provides defensive buffs and healing abilities to teams, as well as a versatile new firearm that switches between shotgun and marksman functions. Additionally, a new skill tree system and account progression were added, allowing players to allocate points across four specialty branches.

This final content push by developer Wildlight shows an effort to provide closure and additional value for the game’s remaining player base. It also reflects the team’s commitment to completing unfinished features even as the community prepares for the game's imminent closure. The departure of Highguard serves as a reminder of the difficulties multiplayer games face in maintaining sustainable player populations.

Hunt: Showdown to Trial Hidden Extraction Points

Crytek continues to experiment with the extraction shooter Hunt: Showdown by testing a new event starting March 18 called The Devil’s Trail. In this event, extraction and supply points will be hidden at mission start and must be discovered either by locating them in-game or through interactive scouting maps found at new tower locations. The current system where extraction points are visible and predictable will be altered to increase tension in endgame situations.

Additionally, new gameplay elements include expanded environmental storytelling, such as lasting footprints and opened items, enhancing immersion. By removing extraction points from the map, Crytek aims to make the extraction phase less formulaic and increase strategic discovery. How this change affects player behavior and match dynamics will determine if it becomes a permanent feature.

US Government Considers Forcing Tencent to Divest Game Holdings

According to recent reports, the US government is debating whether Tencent’s stakes in major US and European game companies pose national security risks, particularly concerning access to user data. Tencent holds significant ownership in companies such as Riot Games, Epic Games, and Ubisoft. This review was scheduled ahead of diplomatic talks with China but has been delayed by scheduling conflicts.

If forced to divest, Tencent's shares could transfer to private equity firms, introducing further uncertainty into the industry. Past actions have already seen Tencent designated as a Chinese military company by the US Department of Defense. The complex geopolitical situation creates unpredictability for affected studios and their development pipelines, making this a critical issue to watch.
