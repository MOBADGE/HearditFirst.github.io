Death Stranding 2 Adds Hardest Difficulty Mode for PC Players

With the recent PC release of *Death Stranding 2*, players have raised concerns about the game being too easy, especially given the availability of vehicles and shared infrastructure that simplify deliveries. In response, Kojima Productions introduced a new difficulty setting named "To the Wilder." This mode is designed to push the game to its challenging limits by intensifying environmental hazards like the damaging Timefall rain.

Lead level designer Hiroaki Yoshiike explained that "To the Wilder" requires careful resource management and strategic planning, encouraging players to use tools and tactics that may have been overlooked on lower difficulties. The developers played through this mode fully to ensure it offers a meaningful challenge without detracting from player enjoyment. This mode aims to offer experienced players a more realistic and demanding way to explore the game's world. Additionally, the in-game social systems will help ease progression, emphasizing cooperative play.

Crimson Desert’s Control Scheme Under Scrutiny Amid Player Frustration

*Crimson Desert* has seen its control scheme come under widespread criticism since launch. Many players and reviewers have described the controls as complicated and unintuitive. Despite these complaints, Pearl Abyss’ PR director, Will Powers, defended the system, likening mastering the controls to learning to ride a bike—it may feel awkward at first but becomes natural over time.

This stance has found some support among industry figures who value novel control schemes, but many players continue to express frustration, noting issues such as frequent accidental actions. The game has achieved commercial success, selling over two million copies in 24 hours, yet it holds a mixed Steam user rating largely due to control concerns. Pearl Abyss has committed to listening to feedback and plans to release updates, which may include improvements to the control experience.

Kirsten Dunst Cast as Alex in Minecraft Movie Sequel

The upcoming sequel to the *Minecraft* movie has cast actress Kirsten Dunst as Alex, one of the game's default avatars. Dunst’s involvement is partly motivated by her children’s fondness for the first movie and the reliable commercial prospects given the original film’s substantial box office gross exceeding $1 billion.

The sequel, scheduled for release in July 2027, will see the return of several cast members including Jack Black and Jennifer Coolidge, with Jared Hess returning as director. Dunst’s casting adds a familiar name to the project, aligning with the film's aim to appeal to the broad Minecraft fan base. The sequel continues efforts to visually and thematically capture the game’s essence.

Silksong Player Uncovers Cut Boss Version Demonstrating Why It Was Removed

A modder recently discovered and restored a cut version of the Last Judge boss from *Silksong*’s act 1, intended to reappear in a secret third act. This "voided" variant features altered visuals and some new attacks infused with void energy that drains a key player resource. However, upon testing, the remade fight was found to be underwhelming, with several moves ineffective or misplaced, resulting in a relatively tame encounter.

This discovery sheds light on *Silksong*’s development process and provides insight into why Team Cherry likely removed this boss version. Rather than risking a lackluster or unbalanced late-game challenge, the team opted to focus on more polished content for the final release.

Slay the Spire 2 Faces Review Bombing over Upcoming Card Nerf, Chinese Market Restrictions Possibly Involved

Developers of *Slay the Spire 2* introduced a beta patch featuring significant changes to cards, enemy balance, and artifact effects. A notable change involved reworking the Silent character’s Prepared card into Prepare, altering its cost and functionality. This shift has sparked a backlash resulting in over 9,000 negative Steam reviews within a day, despite the patch not yet being live on the main branch.

Analysis suggests part of the review surge stems from players in China, where Steam’s community features and Discord servers are restricted, limiting feedback options. Consequently, Chinese players may resort to review bombs as a primary means of voicing dissatisfaction. While the overall English-speaking player base still rates the game positively, the situation highlights how regional restrictions can impact player communication and community response.
