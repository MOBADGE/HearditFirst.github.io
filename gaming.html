Slay the Spire 2 Introduces Multiplayer Co-op Mode

Mega Crit's Slay the Spire 2 has added a multiplayer co-op feature, allowing up to four players to play together online. One player hosts a save file, and up to three friends can join via Steam friends lists—there is no matchmaking or password-protected lobbies, and local co-op is not supported at this early access stage.

Multiplayer retains the core gameplay of the singleplayer deckbuilding roguelite but adapts several systems to suit cooperative play. Each player controls their own character with an independent deck, gold, and relic inventory. Battles feature enemies with stats scaled to party size, attacking the whole group, while energy resources remain individual. Players spend energy on their turns before all must end their turn for the battle to proceed. If a player’s character dies, they revive after victory.

The map phase also supports cooperation and disagreement: players can vote on routes or let random chance decide when choices conflict. Events and "Ancients" offer different options per player, and special campfires allow healing allies. Loot and rewards, including relics and cards, are personal and not shared across the party. Ascension levels, the game's difficulty modifiers, are tracked separately for multiplayer and require all party members to have unlocked the chosen level.

This multiplayer mode is notable as it marks a departure from the original game’s solo focus, providing a team-based experience without overhauling core mechanics. It offers a new way for players to enjoy the roguelike card game collaboratively while preserving individual playstyles and progression.

Nova Roma Puts Players in the Shoes of Roman City Builders Pleasing Jealous Gods

The early access city builder Nova Roma places players in a post-Empire Roman setting where they establish a new civilization on a pristine island. The game challenges players not only with typical city planning and citizen needs but also by requiring them to manage the demands of various Roman gods.

Players build temples devoted to gods like Ceres or Vulcan, who issue quests and bonuses related to the city's development. However, unchosen gods frequently manifest to express displeasure and threaten natural disasters or attacks if ignored. This pushes players to build multiple temples and balance resources carefully to appease these divine beings.

The interaction between citizen demands and rival gods introduces a dynamic and sometimes punishing layer to city management. Players must adapt to the gods’ mandates, such as maintaining farms, offerings, and defenses, lest their cities suffer setbacks like floods or invasions. This combination of mythological oversight and practical city-building tasks creates a distinct challenge compared to more straightforward simulation games.

Nova Roma's gameplay pace is relatively brisk, with continual demands keeping players engaged without the need for accelerated game speeds. The game entered early access this week on Steam, offering a different take on managing both mortal and divine expectations in city creation.

Death Stranding 2 Designed for Higher Completion Rates

Hideo Kojima and his team aimed to address player feedback from the original Death Stranding that the pacing felt slow. According to lead level designer Hiroaki Yoshiike, the sequel was specifically developed to encourage more players to finish the game.

By simplifying worldbuilding elements and making story details accessible through supplemental content rather than embedded exposition, Kojima Productions made the second game more approachable while retaining depth for completionists. Vehicles and tools appear earlier, facilitating faster travel and easier progression.

Completion data supports these design choices; whereas fewer than half of players finished the first game, over 62% reached the end of Death Stranding 2 on PlayStation platforms. This suggests that making the experience more digestible led to increased player retention without sacrificing the narrative's core.

The designers did include higher difficulty modes for players seeking more challenge, indicating a thoughtful balance between accessibility and depth.

OpenAI Ends Sora Video Generation App and Disney Withdraws Investment

OpenAI announced it will discontinue its AI video generation app, Sora, less than six months after the release of its second version. The decision reflects a shift away from AI video tools toward productivity-focused software, such as integrated desktop superapps combining ChatGPT and other AI services.

Sora had garnered attention for generating unauthorized videos featuring copyrighted characters, causing concern in entertainment industries and prompting demands from Japanese studios to cease use of their content. Disney had planned to invest $1 billion in OpenAI with licensing agreements for its IP in Sora but recently pulled out following Sora's end.
