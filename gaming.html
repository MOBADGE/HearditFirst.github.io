Forza Horizon 6 Returns with a Focus on Japan’s Landscape

Forza Horizon 6 previewed recently, offering players a detailed glimpse of its new setting: Japan. The map centers on a densely recreated Tokyo, capturing distinct urban layouts ranging from cramped central lanes to sprawling suburban highways. While gameplay mechanics remain consistent with Forza Horizon 5, the environment introduces a fresh aesthetic with a careful balance of cityscape and rural countryside. Regions such as Hokubu feature a mix of farmland and mountains, and the northern Sotoyama area provides dramatic, snowy descents likely tied to major races. Despite little change in core gameplay elements, this setting stands out as the series’ best map yet, especially for players interested in virtual exploration paired with high-speed driving. The build previewed emphasized the experience of virtual tourism alongside racing, with over 550 vehicles expected in the full game.

No Man’s Sky’s Xeno Arena Adds Creature Collection and Battle Gameplay

No Man’s Sky has released a substantial update called Xeno Arena, introducing a new creature-collection and battle system inspired by Pokémon and similar games. Players can now find, train, and battle alien creatures in designated holo-arenas, adding a multiplayer component centered on pet battles. These creatures have elemental types linked to their planetary biomes and can be collected, bred, and used in combats that offer progression rewards and daily challenges. This new feature represents a significant gameplay expansion, effectively embedding a standalone multiplayer game within No Man’s Sky. It aims to engage players who enjoy collecting and battling creatures alongside traditional exploration.

Anthropic’s Claude Mythos AI Uncovers Thousands of Software Vulnerabilities

Anthropic has unveiled Claude Mythos, an AI model that has automatically identified thousands of high-severity security vulnerabilities across major operating systems, web browsers, and other critical software. Notably, 99% of these issues remain unpatched, highlighting gaps in current security practices. The AI can also develop functional exploits rapidly—tasks that previously took human experts weeks can now be done in hours. Anthropic has initiated Project Glasswing in collaboration with major tech companies to address these vulnerabilities proactively. This development stresses both the risks posed by undiscovered security holes and the potential for AI-assisted tools to improve software safety by accelerating detection and patching.

Intel to Maintain Raptor Lake CPU Supply and DDR4 Support

Intel has confirmed it will continue producing its Raptor Lake generation of CPUs, ensuring their availability for the foreseeable future. This announcement reassures users who value the balance of performance and cost offered by these older chips, especially as newer models like Arrow Lake and Nova Lake enter the market. Intel is also sustaining support for DDR4 memory on Raptor Lake platforms, providing broader compatibility and potentially reducing costs amid current memory shortages driven by AI demand. This strategy suggests Intel aims to serve budget-conscious gamers and system builders by keeping accessible yet capable hardware options available alongside newer, more expensive technologies.

Death Stranding 2 Performance on Steam Deck Improves after Patch

Following its PC release last month, Death Stranding 2 initially struggled with performance issues on the Steam Deck. However, patch 1.2 has addressed many of these problems, improving frame rates and stability on the handheld device. With the patch applied, players can expect frame rates generally between 30-35 fps in open-world areas and higher performance indoors, though battery life remains limited to around 90 minutes per charge. The update includes various fixes beyond performance, such as user interface improvements and input tweaks. This progress demonstrates ongoing optimization efforts to make graphically demanding games more playable on portable hardware.

Astronaut Demonstrates D20 Dice Rolling in Zero Gravity

Rabea Rogge, a German astronaut aboard a recent SpaceX mission, has shown that tabletop gaming elements like rolling a 20-sided die (D20) are feasible in space’s zero-gravity environment. In a shared video, she demonstrates a method of flipping and catching the die by hand to determine results without relying on tables or trays. This lighthearted moment confirms that roleplaying games such as Dungeons & Dragons can be played during space missions, though certain gameplay elements requiring many dice rolls may be less practical. This incident reflects the broader cultural interest in blending gaming with space exploration activities.
