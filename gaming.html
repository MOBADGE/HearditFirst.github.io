Fortune's Run Development Resumes After Creator's Prison Release

After more than a year-long hiatus due to legal troubles, Dizzie, the sole developer of the immersive sim shooter *Fortune's Run*, has resumed development following release from prison. Dizzie's time in custody, related to events not fully disclosed, halted the game's progress despite ongoing legal proceedings during its early development. Upon early parole, Dizzie returned to a halfway house and is working to complete critical story levels and finalize version 1.0 within the next 6 to 12 months. The developer’s post also revealed unexpected productivity during incarceration, including designing a roguelike game on paper. *Fortune’s Run* has drawn comparisons to classic immersive sims and offers a unique fast-paced approach to stealth and combat within early access.

**Why it matters:** The continuation of *Fortune’s Run* highlights how independent projects can struggle with unforeseen personal challenges affecting development but remain poised to reach completion. Players invested in immersive sims may find promise in its revival, though patience will be necessary as the team stabilizes post-incarceration.

Paul Barnett’s 2009 Critique on Design Trends Remains Relevant

A 2009 interview with Paul Barnett, former creative director of *Warhammer Online*, has resurfaced for its critical perspective on industry trends. Barnett cautioned against games development becoming overly self-referential, recycling popular formulas rather than fostering new ideas. He described this phenomenon as “design memes” that can limit creativity, funnel funding, and marginalize genuinely innovative pitches. While specifics about MMOs have since shifted, the concern about consensus-driven design persists, especially as live service models dominate the market today. Barnett also emphasized the balance of artistic ambition and business pragmatism in game creation, valuing longevity and financial sustainability alongside creativity.

**Why it matters:** Barnett's observations from over a decade ago continue to resonate, illustrating persistent tensions between creative risk-taking and financial pressures. Reflecting on these ideas may inform how developers and publishers approach innovation in a crowded market.

Starship Troopers: Ultimate Bug War Falls Short of Potential

*Auroch Digital's* *Starship Troopers: Ultimate Bug War*, a licensed first-person shooter inspired by the Verhoeven film, offers an uneven experience. Positive aspects include solid gunplay and faithful visual nods to the source, but sparse enemy encounters and repetitive mission design weaken engagement. The game’s highlight is its opening Klendathu level, featuring dense combat scenarios, yet much of the gameplay involves traversing empty maps with unremarkable objectives. Attempts at satire and narrative context do not match the original film’s tone, and the addition of an alien assassin campaign feels underdeveloped. Overall, the title struggles to capture the film’s iconic atmosphere and lacks depth in combat intensity and pacing.

**Why it matters:** Licensed games often face challenges balancing fan expectations and gameplay quality. *Ultimate Bug War* exemplifies how faithful aesthetics alone cannot compensate for design shortcomings, an important consideration for future adaptations.

Moves of the Diamond Hand Launches in Early Access as an Ambitious RPG

*Moves of the Diamond Hand*, by developer Cosmo D, entered early access offering a complex, story-rich RPG set in the surreal Off-Peak City. Building on previous work like *Betrayal at Club Low*, it blends narrative depth, skill-based dice mechanics, and a jazz-infused atmosphere. Players engage in quests tied to various skills and political subplots within a sprawling urban environment. The game features tactile dice-roll systems reminiscent of tabletop RPGs and incorporates stealth challenges without significant frustration. Early access status means some content is unfinished or buggy, which may affect pacing and player experience. A full release is planned for spring 2027.

**Why it matters:** This title continues an indie tradition of blending narrative innovation with RPG mechanics. While early access may deter some, the game shows promise for those interested in emergent storytelling and experimental gameplay systems.

Pickmos Removed From Steam Following Publisher Intervention Over Copyright Issues

Pickmos, a survival crafting game widely criticized for copying elements from *Pokémon*, *Palworld*, *Zelda*, and *Overwatch*, has been removed from Steam. Its publisher, NetworkGo, announced direct intervention in development to address controversy, clarifying oversight over the developer PocketGame. This move followed negative community feedback over Pickmos’ blatant design imitations and questionable responses
