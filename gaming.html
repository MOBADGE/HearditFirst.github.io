EVE Online Introduces Its First Non-PvP Starter Zone

After 23 years known for its intense player-versus-player combat, EVE Online is adding a new non-PvP region called Exordium. This zone, revealed by CCP in an official blog post, aims to improve the experience for new players by providing a safe space where they cannot be attacked by other players. Unlike the current scattered starter systems—where PvP is discouraged but sometimes occurs—Exordium will be a central hub made up of 13 beginner systems with PvP completely disabled.

This change addresses the "cloud of anxiety" many new players feel due to EVE's ruthless reputation. While Exordium offers less lucrative rewards than dangerous sectors, it introduces new players to a wide range of activities and mechanics in a more controlled environment. CCP believes this will not only help newcomers but benefit veterans by increasing the player base and activity across the universe. Exordium is expected to open for exploration before EVE Fanfest in May, with a full release during the summer.

Capcom’s Pragmata Shines as a Modern Action Game for Speedrunners

Capcom’s latest sci-fi action game, Pragmata, demonstrates the developer’s success in crafting experiences that support speedrunning, though not officially. The game includes features that cater to players aiming for fast completion times, such as clear save file timers, selectable game modes after initial runs, and cutscene skipping options.

Playing Pragmata with a speedrunner mindset changes its pace drastically—encouraging players to avoid cautious approaches and instead master enemy behavior, animation canceling, and efficient use of abilities. This mode of play enhances the game’s replay value, making subsequent runs feel fresh and challenging despite familiarity. The article’s author notes cutting the game’s playtime from over six hours to under three while incorporating higher difficulty runs, emphasizing that Pragmata invites players to repeatedly engage with its mechanics beyond a traditional playthrough.

Linux Gaming and PC Platforms Present Challenges for Family Play

PC Gamer hardware editor Dave James shares his recent experiences trying to game with his young son on Linux and PC systems. Though Linux distributions like PopOS and Nobara have improved greatly in terms of usability and gaming support—thanks largely to Valve’s Proton compatibility layer—compatibility issues remain, especially with peripherals and certain game ports.

Specifically, Dave ran into persistent problems connecting Bluetooth Xbox controllers to Linux, which required firmware updates and terminal commands that were difficult to troubleshoot on the fly. Additionally, problems with Microsoft account logins while trying to launch games from Xbox Studios caused further frustration, interrupting time meant for co-op play. Ultimately, Dave resorts to cloud gaming via GeForce Now on platforms with simpler setup processes to maintain enjoyable gaming sessions with his son.

Free Fan Remake Brings the Original 1999 Medal of Honor to PC

A fan-made remake of the original Medal of Honor, the 1999 World War II first-person shooter initially exclusive to PlayStation, has been released for free on itch.io. Created by developer Elber88, the project rebuilds the game from the ground up using a modern engine while borrowing some enemy models and mechanics from the later Allied Assault installment to improve visuals.

The remake captures the slower, tension-heavy pace and survival-horror feel of the original, with lone missions behind enemy lines and smaller-scale encounters. Although some animation choices borrowed from Allied Assault are less faithful to the original’s style, the remake overall preserves the game’s unique atmosphere and sounds. This project provides an accessible way to experience a historically significant shooter that is otherwise difficult to find.

Multiplayer Shooter Last Flag Faces Challenges Building an Audience

Last Flag, a 5v5 multiplayer capture-the-flag shooter from Night Street Games—founded by the lead singer and manager of Imagine Dragons—has struggled to attract a stable player base since its April 14 launch. Despite being promoted on the band’s substantial social media following, priced reasonably at $15 without microtransactions, the game peaked below 600 concurrent players and currently holds fewer than 400.

Reviews are generally positive, although many players note the presence of bots in matches as a symptom of low player count. Night Street Games has not disclosed sales figures but expressed a focus on gradually nurturing a sustainable community.
