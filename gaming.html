TSMC Plans Sub-1 Nanometer Chip Trials by 2029

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) reportedly aims to start trial production of chips using sub-1 nanometer (nm) process technology in 2029. This follows their ongoing cadence of introducing a new chip manufacturing node roughly every two years, with mass production of their A14 (angstrom-level) node expected by 2028. While consumer GPUs currently rely on older nodes like 4nm or 5nm, this development signals a roadmap toward much smaller, potentially more efficient chips over the next decade.

This progression is important for gaming hardware, as it suggests future CPUs and GPUs—especially from TSMC's major clients like AMD, Nvidia, Intel, and Apple—will continue to receive advances in processing technology. However, the economic impact of the AI-driven chip market boom has made hardware prices unpredictable, meaning that even with new nodes, cost savings might not be as significant or immediate as in the past.

Aion 2 MMO Sequel to Launch Globally Despite Monetization Concerns

NCSoft’s MMO sequel Aion 2, which launched in late 2025 primarily in Asian markets, is set for a global release later this year on Steam. The game had a controversial start due to aggressive microtransaction practices that prompted a public apology from the developer. Despite this, Aion 2 reportedly achieved strong revenue figures and subscriber growth, boosting NCSoft’s financial outlook.

This global launch signifies NCSoft’s confidence in the MMO’s ongoing commercial viability, although it remains to be seen how Western audiences will respond to the contentious monetization model. The trend highlights the ongoing tension in free-to-play MMOs between player dissatisfaction over pay-to-win elements and the profitability of such schemes.

Analysis of Nvidia’s RTX 60-Series GPUs Suggests Moderate Performance Gains

Looking back at a decade of Nvidia’s GPU development, analysts predict the forthcoming RTX 60-series graphics cards will feature around 30-50% more CUDA cores than the RTX 50-series, reflecting a 60-70% increase in transistor density but similar die sizes. Nvidia is expected to continue using TSMC’s N3 process node rather than moving to the more advanced N2 node for cost reasons.

L2 cache sizes are unlikely to increase significantly due to SRAM scaling limitations with smaller nodes, and VRAM capacities may see only modest increases or remain unchanged because of ongoing memory supply constraints and high costs. Improvements in ray tracing and AI tensor core performance are anticipated but likely incremental. Overall, this suggests the RTX 60-series will offer meaningful but not revolutionary gains, balancing performance improvements with manufacturing cost and market positioning considerations.

OpenAI Patent Shows Ambitious Approach to Stacking High Bandwidth Memory

OpenAI recently published a patent revealing a chip architecture designed to stack much larger numbers of high bandwidth memory (HBM) chips around compute units than current standards permit. The design uses embedded logic bridges to extend communication beyond typical limits, enabling what one analyst described as “HBM stacked up like rice cakes.”

This approach aims to alleviate memory bandwidth bottlenecks critical for AI workloads. While the diagrams have drawn amusement for their simplicity and apparent excess, the patent underscores industry efforts to integrate vast memory resources closely with processing units to meet growing data demands.

South Korean Court Sentences Ex-Samsung Engineer for DRAM Tech Leak

A former Samsung Electronics researcher was sentenced to seven years in prison for leaking core DRAM technology to Chinese competitor CXMT. The technology involved advanced 18nm DRAM process tech valued at over $1 billion in development costs. The court underscored the national importance of such semiconductor intellectual property, citing the damage inflicted on both the company and South Korea.

This case highlights the ongoing tensions around semiconductor technology security amid a global memory supply shortage and geopolitical competition. Protecting trade secrets remains a high priority, given the crucial role of DRAM in gaming and broader computing markets.

Firefox CTO Highlights AI’s Role in Discovering Security Vulnerabilities

Mozilla’s Firefox team collaborated with Anthropic’s AI research group using an early version of the Claude Mythos AI model to detect security vulnerabilities. The AI reportedly found 271 bugs, which helped the team address issues in Firefox 150. Firefox’s CTO noted that while elite researchers could find these bugs eventually, AI accelerates the process significantly, potentially shifting the security landscape in favor of defenders.
