James Bond Rights Owners Oppose James Pond Trademark Application

Gameware and System 3, co-owners of the cult 1990s platformer series James Pond, filed for a UK trademark on the name last year, covering games, toys, and apparel. However, Danjaq LLC, the holder of the James Bond trademarks and copyrights, has formally opposed this application. This opposition follows a similar challenge by Danjaq in 2012 that resulted in a European trademark rejection. Since UK law offers less protection for parody trademarks compared to US law, the opposition significantly reduces the chance of James Pond securing the trademark in the UK. Meanwhile, Gameware and System 3 are preparing a James Pond sequel titled *James Pond and the Rogue AI*, despite criticism from the series’ original creator about its AI-focused promotion.

**Why it matters:** Trademark disputes like this can affect the ability of developers to release or market legacy properties. The case highlights ongoing tensions between intellectual property owners of original works and those reviving parody or derivative content. It also puts a spotlight on the challenges of reviving classic games when rights issues intervene.

Infocom's Failed Business Software Cornerstone Runs on Modern PCs

Once celebrated for its text adventures such as *Zork*, Infocom’s attempt to pivot to business software with its 1985 database program Cornerstone was unsuccessful, contributing to financial difficulties that eventually led to the studio’s merger with Activision and closure. Designed to run on a virtual machine inspired by Infocom’s text game engine, Cornerstone suffered from poor performance and failed to gain traction in a market dominated by IBM-compatible machines.

Thanks to developer TaradinoC, enthusiasts can now run Cornerstone on modern PCs through a new interpreter called Linchpin. The effort revives a piece of gaming history often overlooked compared to Infocom’s adventure games. This development comes amid broader interest in classic gaming preservation, including last year’s open-sourcing of *Zork*.

**Why it matters:** The project preserves an overlooked chapter of gaming history and demonstrates efforts to maintain access to vintage software beyond entertainment titles. It also serves as a case study in how business missteps can impact game studios’ trajectories.

Nexon Reorganizes Development Teams at Neople, Wraps Up The First Berserker: Khazan

Nexon confirmed it has reorganized teams at developer Neople responsible for *The First Berserker: Khazan*, clarifying that the move is a strategic reallocation of talent rather than due to poor performance or layoffs. While the game received positive reviews and a strong launch, sales reportedly declined below expectations. Nexon states the decision relates to the game entering its final development phase, with staff reassigned to other projects within the Dungeon & Fighter franchise.

*The First Berserker: Khazan* is the first of three planned games to introduce the Dungeon & Fighter universe to global audiences. Despite the downsizing of this chapter’s development, Nexon intends to support live operations and future initiatives, leaving open the possibility for sequels.

**Why it matters:** The statement underscores how companies manage resources and personnel in response to market performance. It also indicates Nexon’s continued investment in the Dungeon & Fighter IP, while tempering expectations for further content in this particular game.

Darkstone, a 1999 Action RPG, Is Being Remastered by Original Creator

*Darkstone*, a lesser-known Diablo-style action RPG released in 1999, is getting a remastered edition led by the game’s original creator, Paul Cuisset. The game featured a solo play option with an AI-controlled sidekick, a hunger mechanic requiring players to feed characters, and an aging system that affected gameplay over time.

The remaster, called *Darkstone Restoration*, aims to improve stability, compatibility, controls, and quality of life. It also considers recreating elements like modernized multiplayer and a Quest Editor, depending on early access reception. The project highlights an attempt to modernize a niche classic that has suffered performance and usability issues on contemporary systems.

**Why it matters:** This remaster revives an obscure title from the late 1990s RPG landscape, illustrating ongoing interest in preserving and updating older games from industry veterans. It also broadens available options for fans of classic action RPGs seeking improved versions.
