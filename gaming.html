Rockstar Hackers Leak Data, Confirming GTA Online’s Dominance

Hackers known as ShinyHunters released stolen internal data from Rockstar Games ahead of their April 14 ransom deadline. The leaked files, examined by the GTA Forums community, mainly consist of company financials rather than significant game development secrets, supporting Rockstar’s claim that only "non-material" company information was accessed. The data reveals that GTA Online generates nearly $500 million annually, vastly out-earning Red Dead Online, which pulls in about $26 million per year. Platform-wise, PlayStation 5 leads in user numbers and revenue, while PC accounts for a surprisingly small fraction, even behind Xbox One. Notably, three-quarters of GTA Online’s revenue comes from in-game microtransactions known as Shark Cards. This financial snapshot reinforces Rockstar’s strategic focus on GTA Online, while offering little insight into the upcoming GTA 6. For now, Rockstar has declined to comment on the leak.

Xbox Game Pass Faces Scrutiny Over Pricing and Model

New Xbox head Asha Sharma has acknowledged in an internal memo that the Xbox Game Pass subscription service has become "too expensive" for many players. Following a recent significant price increase — with Game Pass Ultimate jumping 50% to $29.99 monthly — Sharma indicates that Microsoft plans to revise the subscription model to offer "a better value equation" in the short term and evolve towards a more flexible system over time. This comes amid growing debate within the industry about the sustainability and impact of subscription models on game sales and developer revenue. Some developers have expressed concern that Game Pass's popularity can cannibalize retail sales and create tension regarding financial returns. Under Sharma’s leadership, Xbox also seems poised to refocus on its console hardware identity, moving away from previous marketing campaigns and preparing for new hardware projects.

Generation Exile’s Struggles Highlight Challenges of Niche Games in Early Access

Nels Anderson, known for Firewatch and Mark of the Ninja, has reflected publicly on the underwhelming early access launch of his new city builder, Generation Exile. Despite strong early interest—over 35,000 wishlists—the game sold fewer than 300 copies initially. Anderson suggests factors such as early access fatigue and the game’s unique but somewhat "fuzzy" identity contributed to the poor sales. Despite this, his studio has continued development, finishing major story chapters and improving gameplay to fulfill promises made to players. Anderson criticizes industry trends that prioritize quick commercial success and encourages continuous player engagement, fearing they may undermine creative and longer-term game design ambitions. Generation Exile is set for full release on April 17 and is available on Steam.

New Experimental Mode Launches in Marathon FPS

Marathon, the online multiplayer shooter from Bungie, has introduced a limited-time experimental playlist called "Sponsored Queue" or "free kit frenzy." Unlike other competitive modes that require minimal gear loadouts, this playlist allows players to enter with a free standard kit and then build up using scavenged items on the Dire Marsh map. Running from April 15 to 29, this "zero to hero" style format aims to provide insights into player behavior and balance in low-gear conditions. Bungie plans to observe how these constraints affect gameplay and whether this approach enhances early-game progression. This experiment coincides with an upcoming balance patch that will buff the Recon loadout, a currently underpowered option.

91-Year-Old Gamer’s Missed Check-In Reveals Growing Age Diversity in Gaming

A 91-year-old woman in Westlake, Ohio, prompted a welfare check by local police after missing her daily automated safety calls. When officers entered her home, they found her fully fine—engrossed in a videogame and attempting to beat her personal record. The incident highlights the increasing diversity of gamers by age and challenges stereotypes about elderly people and gaming. The story gained attention for its unexpected outcome and lighthearted nature, though media coverage notably did not specify the game involved. Westlake Police confirmed everyone involved found the situation amusing. This anecdote serves as a reminder of gaming’s broad appeal across generations.
