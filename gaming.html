**Last Flag Faces Challenges in a Crowded Multiplayer Shooter Market** Night Street Games, the indie studio founded by Imagine Dragons’ lead singer Dan Reynolds and his brother Mac, launched their debut multiplayer shooter Last Flag on April 14. This 5v5 capture-the-flag style shooter was well-marketed, including promotion to the band's 10 million Instagram followers, a reasonable price point of $15 with no microtransactions, and generally positive reviews. Despite these advantages and a gameplay style that some testers found fun, player numbers on Steam peaked under 600 concurrent players and quickly declined to below 400. Reviewers noted that many matches contain bots due to the low player base, signaling difficulties in attracting and maintaining an audience. The studio expressed satisfaction with the critical reception but did not comment on sales, emphasizing hopes to grow a sustainable community over time. Last Flag joins several recent multiplayer shooters in struggling to carve out a stable player base in a competitive market.

**Windrose Survival Crafting: Detailed Item Discovery Guide for Early Biomes** Windrose, an early access pirate-themed survival crafting game, features multiple biomes filled with materials and recipes that players need to discover to progress. A comprehensive discovery list highlights all key, major, and additional resources obtainable in the three currently available biomes: Coastal Jungle, Foothills, and Cursed Swamps. Each biome has unique quests and bosses that unlock progression and crafting capabilities, such as the fight against Thomas Richards to access the Foothills. The guide notes that some items, like the Crab Shell, Palm Seeds, and Ficus Seeds, can be rare and require persistence. Later biomes introduce more hostile enemies and demand upgraded tools and tactics. Players can use this detailed resource to ensure no materials or recipes are missed, aiding in efficient exploration and crafting advancement.

**Slay the Spire 2 Developer Prioritizes Quality Over Deadlines** Since its early access launch in March, Slay the Spire 2 has seen substantial player engagement and content expansion beyond its predecessor. Developer Mega Crit outlined upcoming updates including alternate versions of Acts 2 and 3, Steam Workshop support, new experimental game modes, additional cards, events, relics, potions, and a new character. The studio, however, notably eschews setting release dates for these features. Co-founder Casey Yano explained that applying strict deadlines tends to result in rushed, low-quality work, which the team aims to avoid. Instead, the small team works at a sustainable pace, prioritizing impactful development and room for experimentation. Players interested in early access to new content can opt into beta branches, though they should expect ongoing balance adjustments. The commitment to creative freedom and quality over a fixed schedule marks a deliberate approach to game development.

**Locating the People of Tortuga Buyer in Windrose Requires Extra Exploration** Players of Windrose encountering difficulties finding the buyer for the People of Tortuga faction should note that he is not located on the island of Tortuga itself, but rather on a nearby island in the Brethren of the Coast Waters. Finding him involves progressing through the “Glorious Hunters” and “Buccaneers” questlines, including delivering gunpowder to Henri and then tanned hides to Charlie Sharpe, who is the buyer. This quest sequence updates the player’s map with the buyer’s location. Once reached, players can sell medicine and provisions to this buyer, raise faction reputation, and hire NPCs like Black Axel, who can craft potions at the player’s base. This information clarifies a common point of confusion due to the game's procedural world generation and adds important context for efficient trading and progression.

**Stop Killing Games Receives Positive Reception in European Parliament Hearing** The Stop Killing Games campaign, which advocates against shutting down online game servers after profitability declines, presented to the European Parliament’s Committees on the Internal Market and Consumer Protection. Organizer Moritz Katzner and founder Ross Scott reported that the reception from Members of European Parliament (MEPs) was overwhelmingly supportive across political groups. The hearing detailed the challenges of game shutdowns and argued that including end-of-life planning in development budgets is feasible and not cost-prohibitive. The campaign clarified that it does not seek eternal online support for all games, but rather policies to prevent premature shutdowns. This hearing marks a significant legislative engagement for the campaign following recent lawsuits and NGO initiatives, indicating growing political awareness of online game preservation issues in Europe.
