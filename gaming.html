**Indie Pass Subscription Service Faces Criticism**

A new subscription service called Indie Pass, launching April 13, offers access to a curated selection of around 70 indie games for $6.99 per month. Developed by indie.io, the service compensates developers based on how much time players spend with their games. While the concept aims to provide discoverability for lesser-known indie titles, it has met with a largely negative response from the gaming community. Critics argue the time-based compensation model incentivizes game design that revolves around keeping players in repetitive loops and question why players shouldn’t just purchase indie games directly since they tend to be affordable. Concerns were also raised about the proliferation of yet another game launcher and the ongoing issue of players not owning their games outright. Some industry figures, like 3D Realms co-founder George Broussard, warned it could be detrimental for developers. Indie Pass hopes to improve discoverability for indie titles in a crowded market, but its future remains uncertain amid skepticism.

**ScummVM Expands Compatibility with More Niche Titles**

ScummVM, a tool that enables old PC games to run on modern platforms, has recently added support for eight additional classic games, including the FMV (full-motion video) shooters Crime Patrol and Crime Patrol 2: Drug Wars, known for their campy, over-the-top acting. It also now supports the Lovecraftian point-and-click adventure Necronomicon: The Dawning of Darkness, which received a notably poor rating from a YouTuber reviewer. ScummVM has evolved beyond its initial focus on adventure games, allowing players to explore many obscure or forgotten titles across various systems. The project maintains resources to help users find where these supported games can be purchased or downloaded, preserving gaming history and offering a means to experience quirky or overlooked classics.

**AI Startup Tesana Aims to Democratize Game Development**

Tesana is a company developing AI-driven game creation tools that transform text prompts describing game elements into playable game code. By combining its engine with third-party AI services, it lets users generate prototype or experimental games through natural language. Though current outputs are rough and sometimes incoherent, the startup recently reported attracting 10,000 paying users within weeks of launch. Tesana’s co-founder, Johannes Vermandois, envisions this technology enabling millions of new creators to make games without prior coding skills, potentially raising the overall quality of indie games by increasing diversity and quantity. However, some question whether AI-generated games can have unique creative visions or stand out amid mass production. Tesana positions its tool as complementary to traditional game development, focusing on prototyping and amateur creation, but acknowledges the challenges ahead.

**Slay the Spire 2 Introduces End-of-Run Badges in Beta Update**

The deckbuilder Slay the Spire 2 has added a new feature called badges in its latest beta patch. These function like achievements that highlight notable accomplishments or events during individual playthroughs, such as defeating bosses without taking damage or uncovering easter eggs. Mega Crit, the developer, intends badges to make each run feel distinct and aid in scoring. The update also includes balance changes to characters, enemies, and events, bug fixes, improved visuals, and UI enhancements. The patch comes after mixed community reactions to previous balance adjustments. Though negative reviews have appeared alongside the latest update, the developer reports the response is less severe than with earlier contentious patches. Players can access the new content via the game’s public beta branch.

**The Gaming Historian Ends YouTube Career with Historical Nintendo Documents**

Norman Caruso, known as The Gaming Historian on YouTube, announced his retirement from producing video essays after more than 15 years and over one million subscribers. He cites burnout following his most ambitious project—a documentary on The Oregon Trail—and a waning desire to continue making videos. Moving forward, Caruso will focus on a history podcast hosted with his wife. As a final contribution to his fans and gaming history preservation, he uploaded a collection of scanned court documents from an early 1980s legal case where Universal Studios sued Nintendo over Donkey Kong’s alleged trademark infringement. These documents include interesting development details, such as alternate names considered for Donkey Kong. The files are accessible via the Internet Archive and have also been shared with the Video Game History Foundation. Caruso reflects on his career as a life-changing experience.
