Resident Evil Requiem’s Hidden Detail Connects Grace to Reddit

An out-of-bounds camera cam reveal in Resident Evil Requiem shows young Grace Ashcroft using a laptop accessing a fictional subreddit called r/amateurjournalists during a flashback cutscene. The posts she reads reference events and characters from previous Resident Evil games, such as the Baker family from Resident Evil 7. This subtle inclusion, discovered by fans and posted on Reddit, adds a new layer of narrative detail suggesting Grace follows conspiracy discussions online. While the in-game subreddit is fictional, fans have created a real Reddit community inspired by it. This discovery highlights the developers’ attention to immersive storytelling and interconnected Resident Evil lore.

Historical Japanese Game Cookie’s Bustle Preserved After Legal Battles

The Video Game History Foundation (VGHF) has successfully preserved the obscure 1999 Japanese point-and-click game Cookie’s Bustle, which had been largely erased from online platforms due to repeated copyright claims by a company called Graceware. Cookie’s Bustle’s unique story about a girl transformed into a teddy bear traveling to a sports competition island includes sci-fi and surreal elements. Graceware’s takedown notices affected gameplay videos, screenshots, and fan content, but VGHF acquired a physical copy and archived the game, pushing back against questionable copyright claims. As a result, platforms like Ukie have ceased automated takedowns for Graceware. This case underlines challenges in preserving obscure games when confronted by aggressive copyright enforcement and the importance of archival efforts.

Highguard Receives Final Update Before Shutdown

Highguard, a multiplayer shooter scheduled to go offline on March 12, has received a substantial final update introducing a new playable Warden character named Koldo, skill trees, account progression, and a versatile weapon called Switchback. Koldo offers support abilities including protective shields and health regeneration. Players can now earn skill points to specialize in roles such as Raider or Protector, reaching a level cap of 100. The update, completed rapidly by the development team, represents a final polish and expansion of gameplay features despite the game’s impending closure. The shutdown reflects the difficulties multiplayer titles face in sustaining player bases, even after attracting millions of initial users.

Hunt: Showdown Hides Extraction Points to Enhance Gameplay

Crytek’s extraction shooter Hunt: Showdown is experimenting with gameplay by hiding extraction points and supply locations at match start during the upcoming Devil’s Trail event starting March 18. Players will need to discover extraction points via scouting maps or by completing objectives, rather than having them visible on the map initially. This change introduces uncertainty and strategic choice to the extraction phase, where players must decide routes after acquiring bounty tokens. Additional features include new scout towers offering valuable items and expanded passive world tracking that leaves permanent marks from player actions. These system tweaks aim to reinforce Hunt’s focus on tactical hunting and player-driven storytelling within its matches.

US Government Considers Forcing Tencent to Divest US Game Holdings

Reports indicate the US government is debating whether Tencent, a major Chinese gaming conglomerate with extensive stakes in Western companies, should be forced to divest its US and Finnish game holdings over national security concerns. Tencent owns or invests in companies including Riot Games, Epic Games, and Ubisoft. The concern centers on potential access to personal data of US citizens. This follows previous Trump administration efforts scrutinizing Tencent and raises questions about future ownership and investment in key industry players. Forcing divestment could disrupt funding and ownership structures, potentially leading to increased private equity involvement. The issue reflects ongoing geopolitical tensions affecting the gaming industry’s corporate landscape.

Minimal Pass Rate in First Adventure Game Aptitude Test

Over 800 gamers took part in the inaugural Adventure Game Aptitude Test, a monitored exam requiring completion of the 1987 graphic adventure Maniac Mansion within four hours without consulting walkthroughs. Only two participants passed, demonstrating the test’s difficulty under strict conditions. The exam utilized online proctoring to prevent cheating and did not reveal the game beforehand. Many contestants expressed frustration as the game’s puzzles are known to be challenging. This experiment highlights the enduring complexity of classic adventure games and the potential for standardized tests in gaming skill assessment. The small number of passes may reflect both the game’s difficulty and the constraints of test conditions.
