1440p Remains Top Choice Among PC Gamers, But 4K Popularity Matches 1080p

A recent poll by PC Gamer confirms that 2560 x 1440 resolution continues to be the most preferred gaming resolution for PC gamers, accounting for 36% of respondents. Surprisingly, the number of gamers opting for native 4K (3840 x 2160) resolution is nearly equal to those still gaming at 1080p (1920 x 1080), with both around 22%. This parity is notable given the assumption that native 4K gaming is less common due to hardware demands. However, advancements in upscaling technologies like DLSS and FSR may make high-quality 4K output easier to achieve without native resolution rendering, possibly inflating 4K usage figures. Ultrawide resolutions, particularly 3440 x 1440, also hold a significant share at 14%. The data provides insight into current PC gaming hardware trends, highlighting the strong interest in higher resolution displays balanced with performance considerations.

Intel’s Market Cap Surges Amid Foundry and AI Collaborations

Intel's market capitalization has reached its highest level in over 25 years, surpassing $300 billion. This financial growth follows years of internal challenges including job cuts and problematic CPU launches. Key contributors to this turnaround are Intel’s foundry agreements with major tech and AI companies. Collaborations such as the partnership with Elon Musk’s Terafab project to manufacture AI chips for SpaceX and Tesla demonstrate a strategic shift toward leveraging Intel’s fabrication facilities beyond its own CPU production. Additionally, contracts with Google to supply custom IPUs further strengthen Intel’s position. While still trailing AMD and Nvidia in overall valuation, Intel’s increased market cap signals investor confidence in its foundry and AI-related initiatives. For the gaming community, a revitalized Intel could mean more competition and innovation in processor technologies.

Microsoft Reduces Copilot Branding in Windows 11 Apps Following User Feedback

Microsoft is scaling back the presence of Copilot branding in several built-in Windows 11 applications including Photos, Notepad, Snipping Tool, and Widgets. The company’s recent preview update to Notepad removed the prominent Copilot button, replacing it with a more neutral "writing tools" pen icon that retains the underlying AI features without explicit branding. This shift aligns with Microsoft’s earlier commitment to reduce “unnecessary Copilot entry points” to address user concerns about intrusive AI branding. Despite the branding reduction, AI functionalities remain intact and accessible. The change reflects Microsoft’s attempt to balance integrating AI tools into its OS while responding to community feedback about overt branding and unexpected AI activations.

Asus Introduces ROG Equalizer Cable to Address GPU Power Connector Issues

Asus has announced a new power cable, the ROG Equalizer, designed to prevent the common problem of melting power connectors on high-end GPUs like the RTX 5090. The cable meets ATX 3.1 specifications but supports higher current (up to 17A compared to the standard 9.2A), and balances current delivery across power pins to avoid overloading any single connector. The cable includes an extra line that interfaces with select Asus ROG power supplies for real-time current monitoring via Asus’s GPU Tweak III software. Compatible with any PSU using standard 12V-2x6 connectors, the ROG Equalizer aims to provide hardware-level protection for demanding graphics cards, reflecting growing concerns around power delivery safety in modern gaming PCs.

Former Bethesda Artist Acknowledges Developers Anticipated Most Player Complaints at Launch

A retired Bethesda artist who worked on Skyrim, Fallout, and Starfield has stated that roughly 95% of player-reported issues at game launch were already identified internally by developers before release. He emphasized that developers are aware of many flaws but face limitations due to time, finances, and management decisions, echoing Todd Howard’s frequent remark that “we can do anything, but we can’t do everything.” The interview highlights the complexities of game development and the reality that not all issues get resolved before launch. Although Bethesda has demonstrated a willingness to improve games post-release, as seen with Fallout 76’s ongoing updates, skepticism remains whether similar efforts will extend to Starfield given prior comments suggesting limited plans for major overhauls.
