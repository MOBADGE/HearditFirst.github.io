Resident Evil Requiem Sales and In-Game Detail Uncovered

Capcom’s recently released Resident Evil Requiem has achieved notable commercial success, selling over five million copies within four days of its February 27 launch. This rapid uptake places the game firmly within Capcom’s top 20 best-selling titles, positioned between the original 1998 Resident Evil 2 and the 2014 Resident Evil Remake. The game also recorded the series’ biggest launch on Steam, surpassing concurrent player counts of previous entries such as the Resident Evil 4 Remake.

In a separate development related to Resident Evil Requiem, players exploring out-of-bounds camera angles have discovered an Easter egg in a flashback scene: the character Grace Ashcroft is shown browsing a fictional subreddit called r/amateurjournalists. The posts she reads reference key series elements such as the Baker family from Resident Evil 7 and other in-universe conspiracies. This detail adds a layer of narrative depth, implying Grace is an active online researcher within the game’s story world.

Additionally, the modding community has quickly created content for Resident Evil Requiem, with the first “sexy Leon” mod appearing on Nexus Mods just under five days after launch. The mod features a shirtless version of Leon S. Kennedy, reflecting ongoing fan interest in character customization and personalization.

Legal Victory Preserves Obscure Japanese Game Cookie’s Bustle

The Video Game History Foundation (VGHF) has successfully countered multiple Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) takedowns aimed at preserving and archiving content related to Cookie’s Bustle, a little-known 1999 Japanese point-and-click game by Rodik. The game had remained inaccessible with no official releases or playable archives for years, largely due to aggressive copyright claims by the company Graceware.

VGHF obtained a physical copy of Cookie’s Bustle and uploaded gameplay footage and documentation to their digital archive, rejecting Graceware’s claim of ownership as unsubstantiated. Graceware's legal claims rely on registrations with an organization called Interoco, which offers only minimal proof of copyright. After VGHF’s legal challenge, Ukie—the agent processing takedown notices for Graceware—ceased providing takedown services for them. This outcome represents a rare example of a nonprofit successfully pushing back on overreaching copyright enforcement and helps ensure the game’s preservation for historical and cultural study.

Highguard Receives Final Content Update Before Shutdown

Highguard, a multiplayer action game developed by Wildlight Entertainment, is set to shut down its servers on March 12, just days from now. Despite this, the developers released a substantial final content update. It introduces a new healer character named Koldo with unique defensive abilities, a new dual-purpose firearm, added skill trees covering several player specializations, and account progression systems.

Creative director Jason McCord emphasized this update was completed voluntarily by the “skeleton crew” to offer last content touches to remaining players. Highguard struggled to maintain a large player base despite two million users having tried it, with the majority on PlayStation 5. The update serves as both a thank-you to fans and a farewell before the game’s closure.

Hunt: Showdown to Hide Extraction Points in Upcoming Event

Crytek’s extraction shooter Hunt: Showdown will experiment with a new gameplay twist starting March 18 in its Devil’s Trail event. Supply and extraction points, previously visible on maps, will be hidden at mission start. Players can reveal these locations by exploring in-game or finding scouting maps at new points of interest called Scout Towers. Extraction points may also be revealed after acquiring bounty tokens or using specific tarot cards.

This change aims to increase uncertainty and tension during the extraction phase of matches, which was previously more predictable since the locations were visible to all. Additionally, the update expands environmental persistence features to leave behind more permanent traces of player activity, reinforcing the game’s focus on tracking and hunting in a dark, immersive world.

US Government Reconsiders Tencent’s Game Holdings Amid Security Concerns

The US government is reportedly debating whether to force Tencent to divest its holdings in several US and Finnish video game companies, due to perceived national security risks. Tencent owns or has significant stakes in major companies such as Riot Games, Epic, and Ubisoft, raising concerns that it could access personal data of US citizens.
