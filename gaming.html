Indie Pass Subscription Service Faces Criticism

A new subscription service called Indie Pass is launching on April 13, offering access to a curated catalog of 70 indie games for $6.99 per month. The service, run by publisher indie.io, compensates developers based on the time subscribers spend playing their games. While this model echoes Microsoft's Game Pass but focused solely on indie titles, reception among gamers and developers has been largely negative. Critics argue that time-based compensation discourages games that don’t encourage prolonged play loops, that indie games are already affordable and best supported through direct purchase, and that the service adds yet another launcher and recurring cost. Industry voices, including 3D Realms co-founder George Broussard, have described such models as harmful to developers. Indie Pass aims to address discoverability problems faced by indie titles, with hopes that the subscription service won’t impact traditional sales. However, initial reactions suggest it may struggle to attract both developers and subscribers.

ScummVM Expands Support for Obscure and Quirky Old PC Games

ScummVM, a popular tool that allows players to run classic PC adventure games on modern systems, has added support for eight more games including the cult FMV shooters Crime Patrol and Crime Patrol 2: Drug Wars. These mid-1990s games are noted for their campy acting and unique full-motion video style. Also newly supported is the Lovecraftian point-and-click adventure Necronomicon: The Dawning of Darkness, which recently gained attention after a YouTuber gave it a scathing 2/10 rating. ScummVM's ongoing expansion into supporting a wider range of older titles helps preserve games that might otherwise be inaccessible, allowing players to explore lesser-known or critically panned games as historical curiosities or for unique entertainment value. The tool works across platforms such as Windows, Mac, and even the Nintendo Wii.

AI Startup Tesana Seeks to Democratize Game Development

Tesana is a new AI-driven platform that allows users to create videogames by typing natural language prompts. Using generative AI combined with a proprietary game engine, Tesana translates descriptions of game elements into playable code. The service, which attracted about 10,000 paying customers shortly after launch, markets itself as a way to prototype ideas quickly and potentially enable new types of games. Co-founder Johannes Vermandois envisions millions of new creators using such tools to expand the gaming landscape. However, some experts question whether ease of game creation through AI can replace the detailed creative control traditionally required in game development. While the technology is still in early stages and current user-generated games are rudimentary, Tesana reflects an ongoing trend of incorporating AI into creative tools.

Slay the Spire 2 Beta Adds End-of-Run Badges and Other Updates

The latest beta update for Slay the Spire 2 introduces a new badge system that highlights unique accomplishments at the end of each run, such as defeating a boss without losing health or discovering easter eggs. These badges aim to make individual runs feel more distinct and to enhance scoring. The patch also includes balance adjustments to card mechanics, enemy behavior, map generation, as well as new art and visual effects. While earlier balance updates provoked a sharp backlash from players, this patch has seen a more mixed but less hostile reception. The game remains in active development with further improvements expected as it continues its beta phase, available to players opting into the Steam beta branch.

Longtime YouTuber The Gaming Historian Ends Video Production

Norman Caruso, known as The Gaming Historian, announced that he will stop making his long-form documentary videos after more than 15 years on YouTube. Citing burnout following his most ambitious video on The Oregon Trail, Caruso said his interest in producing new videos waned and he wanted to maintain quality rather than release lower-effort content. He plans to focus on his history podcast going forward. As a final gesture, he released a large collection of scanned court documents related to the 1982 Universal vs. Nintendo legal case concerning Donkey Kong. These documents are now publicly accessible on the Internet Archive and offer valuable insights for video game historians and fans.
