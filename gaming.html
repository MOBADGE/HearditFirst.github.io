Five New Steam Games to Notice

This week, several lesser-known titles have appeared on Steam, offering a variety of experiences worth exploring. Among them:

- **Stiff Neck** is a visual novel blending psychological horror with a mysterious narrative set in a vaguely timeless world. It centers on Noele, a small-town cop entangled in strange events involving cursed dolls and an AI dog. - **Which Sausage, Mate?** presents a life simulation focused on Kazio, a former coal miner navigating the challenges of retail work and personal finances in Poland, with multiple endings shaped by tough choices. - **Wasteland Bites** combines food truck management with combat in a post-apocalyptic setting where players fend off mutants while trying to reach a rumored safe haven. - **It Has My Face** is a first-person roguelike mixing elements of social deduction with combat, where players track and eliminate doppelgangers in a procedurally generated landscape. - **Grime 2**, a sequel to the 2021 metroidvania, emphasizes combat with a system that allows players to use enemy molds as weapons, alongside cinematic boss fights.

These new releases reflect a range of genres and creative concepts, illustrating the diversity available in the current Steam catalog.

Pillars of Eternity Adds Turn-Based Mode After 11 Years

Obsidian has updated the classic RPG *Pillars of Eternity*, adding a full turn-based combat mode alongside its original real-time with pause system. This turn-based mode was previously in beta but is now integrated officially.

Unlike many turn-based games, the mode limits players to one action per turn but grants multiple turns depending on stats, restoring dynamics lost in the sequel’s system. This adjusts character build viability, making classes like Ciphers more competitive and giving an advantage to speed and crowd control tactics.

The new mode has been received positively for making combat more deliberate and accessible, offering a fresh way to experience a nearly decade-old game. It represents an example of a developer significantly reworking a game long after its release.

Zelda: Twilight Princess PC Port Progresses with Modding Activity

Development continues on a fan-made PC port of *The Legend of Zelda: Twilight Princess* that is based on a code decompilation of the original game. Recent showcase videos demonstrate stable gameplay of the early sections, with native 60fps support and expanded platform compatibility (Windows, Mac, Linux).

The project does not include official game assets, requiring owners to supply them from their own copies. Despite not being officially released, modders have already begun adding new content, including a mod that replaces Link with Linkle, a character from *Hyrule Warriors*.

This port follows in the footsteps of similar projects for other Nintendo titles and highlights the ongoing interest in bringing classic games to PC with modern improvements.

Linux to Drop Support for Intel 486 Architecture

After more than three decades, Linux kernel developers are starting to phase out support for the Intel 486 CPU architecture. The 486 processor debuted in 1989 and was discontinued for desktop use in 1998, although some embedded variants lasted until 2007.

Linus Torvalds has stated that this hardware is no longer relevant for modern kernel development. The removal of compatibility code in an upcoming Linux 7.1 release aims to simplify maintenance and avoid allocating resources to supporting outdated and scarcely used hardware.

For users running Linux on very old machines, this means continued use will rely on older kernels, but for most, it will have no noticeable impact. Support for later processors, such as early Pentium models, will remain in place.

Reflections on the First Video Game TV Show: The Pac-Man Cartoon

A retrospective look at the *Pac-Man* animated series from the early 1980s highlights just how far video game adaptations have come. The show featured Pac-Man and family in Pac-Land, with minimal plot coherence and a heavy reliance on redundant "Pac-" naming conventions.

Despite its low production quality, odd storytelling choices, and repetitive scenarios—such as repeatedly chasing ghosts with power pellets—it set a precedent for adapting games to television. Comparing it to modern adaptations like *Arcane* or *The Witcher* illustrates the significant evolution in writing, production values, and narrative ambition.

This perspective underscores how current adaptations benefit from decades of development and audience expectation changes.
