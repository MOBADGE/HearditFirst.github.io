Overwatch’s Latest Update Influenced by Marvel Rivals’ Hero Drops

Blizzard’s Overwatch recently saw its biggest update, adding five new heroes simultaneously—a move partly inspired by the launch strategy of competitor Marvel Rivals. Overwatch 2 director Aaron Keller revealed at the 2026 Game Developers Conference (GDC) that seeing the excitement around Marvel Rivals dropping dozens of characters at once sparked the idea of bundling multiple heroes in one update. While Blizzard did not match Marvel Rivals’ rapid expansion of nearly 50 characters since late 2024, Overwatch’s new season—which has dropped the “2” from its title—has seen steady player count growth for the first time since its 2022 launch. Blizzard has plans to add another five heroes later this year, signaling an ongoing effort to revitalize the game’s multiplayer experience.

Baby Steps Embraces Unconventional Level Design by Challenging Player Expectations

At GDC, Gabe Cuzzillo, co-creator of the climbing game Baby Steps, discussed how the game’s design diverges from traditional polished singleplayer experiences. Instead of carefully curated challenges, Baby Steps intentionally includes “indifference to the player,” placing stacks of cans on climbable structures that serve no reward other than the challenge itself. Remarkably, players managed to climb nearly all these can stacks, except one, which still sparked creativity and trial among the community. This approach highlights a design philosophy that invites players to explore and engage with emergent gameplay rather than following explicit guidance, standing in contrast to typical linear level design focused on directing player behavior.

Generative AI Divides Games Industry Amid Investor Optimism and Developer Concerns

A rift over generative AI technology was evident at GDC, where Moritz Baier-Lentz of Lightspeed Venture Partners expressed surprise that many game developers are critical of AI’s role in game creation. Despite growing pushback—52% of developers surveyed recently said AI is bad for the industry—investors and big tech firms maintain that generative AI will augment development processes rather than replace human creativity. Baier-Lentz attributed much of the negativity to job security fears following industry layoffs. Other concerns from developers include ethical issues like unauthorized use of artists’ work, environmental impact, and the current quality limitations of AI-generated content. Panels at GDC emphasized that AI tools are still evolving and that meaningful human input remains essential.

Bungie Acknowledges Audio Changes in Marathon Went Too Far; Plans Adjustment

Bungie has admitted that a recent update to Marathon, which increased how far gunfire and explosions can be heard, was an “overcorrection” negatively affecting gameplay. Players reported that the louder sounds made stealth approaches and strategic play more difficult, pushing matches toward constant combat dominance. In response, Bungie stated they will reduce the audio range to a more balanced level that preserves player awareness without feeling excessive. No exact timeline for this fix has been given, but the developer is monitoring player feedback and plans to implement changes in an upcoming update to improve the overall experience.

Arc Raiders Director Highlights Challenge of Launching Live Service Games

Caio Braga, production director of Arc Raiders, reflected at GDC on the difficulties of live service game development, emphasizing the importance of having time and trust to pivot creatively. Arc Raiders underwent multiple major directional changes during development but was allowed to adapt without cancellation. Braga expressed hope that other studios facing high-profile live service failures might be afforded similar opportunities instead of being pressured into launching unfinished products that may fail. He noted that treating game development as a formula leads to problems and that creative freedom—though inefficient at scale—is crucial to a game’s eventual success.

Obsidian on the Difficulty Dilemma in RPGs with The Outer Worlds 2

Obsidian senior systems designer Robert Donovan presented insights into balancing RPG difficulty, focusing on The Outer Worlds 2’s development. He explained the team aimed to maintain a consistent challenge level throughout the game, avoiding the common RPG issue of early difficulty spikes followed by easy progression. The developers settled on a system measuring enemy toughness by the number of hits to kill rather than raw hit points, allowing players to feel meaningful improvement through skills and upgrades. Despite extensive playtesting, difficulty preferences varied widely, with some players finding the game too easy and others too hard. Donovan acknowledged that no single difficulty setting pleases everyone and that accommodating both casual and hardcore players requires trade-offs.
