Valve Responds to New York Loot Box Lawsuit

Valve has issued a strong public rebuttal to the recent lawsuit filed by New York's attorney general, which alleges the company enabled illegal gambling through loot boxes accessible to children and adults. Valve denies that its loot boxes legally constitute gambling in New York, noting that all items acquired are purely cosmetic and do not affect gameplay advantage. The company compared loot boxes to collectible physical items like baseball cards and trading cards, arguing that players are not pressured to spend money.

Valve also emphasized its efforts to combat unauthorized gambling activities external to Steam, including locking over one million accounts involved in fraud or gambling misuse. The firm rejected the New York Attorney General's proposed changes such as banning item trading, stating these would harm consumer rights and developer innovation. Valve further criticized the investigation for sidestepping legislative processes and warned against intrusive age and location data collection that could breach user privacy.

This statement reflects Valve’s rare decision to publicly engage with legal challenges amid mounting pressures, including class-action lawsuits over loot boxes and antitrust claims in the UK. The dispute highlights ongoing tensions around regulation of virtual item economies and age verification in gaming platforms.

Minecraft Shifts from Large Annual Updates to Frequent Smaller Drops

At GDC 2026, Minecraft general manager Ryan Cooper explained why Mojang moved away from its previous model of major annual updates featuring wide-ranging new content. The ambitious 2021 Caves & Cliffs update required extensive changes to world generation technology, which proved challenging due to Mojang updating foundational game and development tools simultaneously. This experience was likened to “trying to build a skyscraper with hand tools,” prompting the studio to reconsider its workflow.

Following Caves & Cliffs, Mojang opted for a more frequent, smaller-scale update cadence, rolling out targeted content drops. The first for 2026, called the “Tiny Takeover” update, focuses on adding baby mobs and other compact features. This approach aims to deliver more regular content driven by player feedback without overloading development resources as happened with previous monumental updates.

The shift signifies how Mojang adapts to the complexities of maintaining a massive live game while balancing community engagement and technical constraints.

Steam Sees Nearly 6,000 Games Earn Over $100,000 in 2025

Valve shared data from a recent Game Developers Conference talk indicating that 5,863 games earned more than $100,000 in revenue on Steam during 2025, a significant increase from around 3,000 titles in 2020. Despite concerns about over-saturation on the platform, Valve aims to improve discovery by matching games to suitable audiences, illustrated by its successful daily deals program, which attracted over 8 million buyers.

The data suggests Steam remains a viable marketplace for both large publishers and smaller developers, supporting a broad diversity of successful titles. Valve also highlighted the platform’s vast global reach and continuing growth despite industry challenges.

Microsoft Details Project Helix Xbox-PC Hybrid, Releases Years Away

At GDC, Microsoft revealed more about Project Helix, its upcoming console blending Xbox and PC gaming experiences. The device features a custom AMD system on chip designed for next-generation DirectX, including raytracing, machine learning-based upscaling, and neural rendering technologies. The machine will support both native Xbox console and PC games.

Offering a unified experience across platforms reflects Microsoft’s acknowledgment that traditional distinctions between console and PC gamers are fading. Developer kits for Helix are expected in 2027, with consumer release possibly in 2028, indicating the product remains years from market. Supply chain issues, such as the ongoing global RAM shortage, could impact availability.

At Fate’s End Impresses with Detailed Hand-Illustrated Art

The upcoming game At Fate’s End attracted attention at GDC for its high-quality hand-illustrated 2D art and animation. Developed by the team behind Spiritfarer, the game blends action and narrative focusing on family dynamics among demigods. Its combat integrates tarot card-based powers accessible through a time-slow mechanic.

While some interface elements, such as menu navigation during combat, were noted as interruptive, the overall presentation and animation quality were praised as exceptional. At Fate’s End aims for release later in 2026 but has no confirmed date.
