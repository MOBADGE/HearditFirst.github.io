Gaming News Briefing – April 13, 2026

1. Pickmon Rebrands to Pickmos Amidst Copyright Concerns The creature-collecting survival game initially known as Pickmon has altered its name slightly to Pickmos. Publisher NetworkGo stated the change was to better align with the game’s brand identity and lore. This follows criticism over Pickmon’s marketing, which featured imagery heavily resembling characters and creatures from Pokémon, Final Fantasy, Zelda, Overwatch, and Palworld. The obvious parallels, particularly to Pokémon, may have risked trademark infringement claims, as Nintendo has shown a readiness to pursue legal action against perceived imitators. Although only a single letter was changed, the rebranding attempts to reduce possible confusion and distance the game from existing franchises. The creatures within the game will continue to be called Pickmon. The move highlights ongoing challenges games face regarding intellectual property when drawing inspiration from popular titles.

2. Fable Studio Maintains Autumn 2026 Release Window Despite rumors suggesting the Fable reboot might delay its launch to avoid coinciding with the November 19 release of Grand Theft Auto 6, developer Playground Games and Microsoft have reiterated their commitment to an autumn 2026 release for Fable. Industry insider Jeff Grubb had speculated about an internal pushback influenced by GTA 6’s launch, potentially shifting Fable's release into late 2026 or early 2027. However, current official statements confirm no such delay has occurred. The situation reflects a common industry dilemma where publishers attempt to avoid competing with major releases, but also the risks involved in chasing release windows, as delays can compound if other large titles adjust their schedules. For now, Fable remains on track for fall 2026, awaiting further updates from the studio.

3. Modder Introduces Enemy Size Customization to Resident Evil: Requiem A new mod for Resident Evil: Requiem allows players to adjust the size of enemies in-game, shrinking or enlarging them between 10% and 300% of their original scale. Designed to mitigate the game’s intense difficulty and horror elements, particularly on higher difficulty settings, the mod can instantly toggle enemy sizes using in-game controls. While it adds a humorous angle—such as tiny zombies that are both less scary yet harder to hit—there are some noted glitches, including physics oddities when enemies die. The mod requires the RE Framework to function and offers an interesting way for players to tailor the experience if the standard enemy presence is too daunting. This highlights the vibrant modding community’s role in expanding and customizing gameplay options.

4. Voice Actor for GTA 5’s Lester Comments on GTA 6’s Satire Impact Jay Klaitz, who voiced the character Lester in Grand Theft Auto 5, expressed the view that GTA 6 might face challenges replicating the impact of previous entries in the series due to the increasingly “weird” nature of the real world. He noted that much of the satire that once felt far-fetched now aligns closely with actual events and public behavior, especially given GTA 6’s setting of a fictional Florida-like region known as Leonida. This changing context might make the game’s social commentary less striking but Klaitz remains confident the game will still deliver significant content and satire. His remarks underscore how shifts in reality can influence the reception and relevance of cultural satire in games.

5. Valve Developer Releases Linux VRAM Fix for 8 GB Graphics Cards Linux users with 8 GB VRAM GPUs, particularly on Arch-based distributions, may benefit from a recent fix introduced by Valve developer Natalie Vock. The fix involves kernel patches and utilities that improve memory management during gaming, preventing excessive VRAM usage that previously led to performance degradation. By using cgroups to prioritize GPU memory allocations more effectively, games can run more stably on systems without requiring larger VRAM capacities. This update is currently easiest to implement on Arch-based distros like CachyOS, with plans for broader distribution support in the future. Given current memory price increases and hardware supply issues, this work can help Linux gamers extend the usable life of GPUs with moderate VRAM.
