Project Zomboid Bans Malicious Steam Workshop Mods

Developer The Indie Stone has removed 14 Steam Workshop mods from Project Zomboid after discovering they contained heavily obfuscated code creating malicious files outside the game’s directory. The affected mods, mainly soundtracks for various games and series, were installed on between 500 and 2200 devices. The exploit impacted only the game's Build 42 (unstable) branches, so users on Build 41 were unaffected. While the exact purpose of the malicious files remains unclear, the developer advises anyone who installed these mods to take security precautions beyond simply uninstalling them. The offending mods were uploaded by a single user who has since been banned from the Workshop.

This action highlights the ongoing risk of third-party mods compromising game security, emphasizing the importance of monitoring unofficial content in popular mod-supported games.

Hazelight Studios Surpasses 50 Million Game Sales

Hazelight Studios, known for co-op action adventures like *A Way Out*, *It Takes Two*, and *Split Fiction*, announced it has sold over 50 million copies across these three titles in under ten years. Sales breakdown shows *It Takes Two* leading with 30 million copies, followed by *A Way Out* at 13 million, and *Split Fiction* at 7 million since its release last year.

The milestone reflects the sustained popularity of narrative-driven co-op games. Hazelight is currently working on a new project, details of which remain under wraps. Their consistent sales suggest a steady audience for their focused genre approach.

Creative Player Rebuilds Dust 2 Map in World of Warcraft Housing

A World of Warcraft player known as Edengonedark has recreated the iconic *Counter-Strike* map Dust 2 using WoW’s player housing tools. The build includes details such as a car crafted from wooden shelves, doormats, and fruit plates. The project drew attention for its surprising fidelity given the limited and fantasy-themed assets available in WoW housing.

This creation showcases the versatility of WoW’s housing system and the creativity of its community. It also illustrates how highly recognizable game environments can be reconstructed in unexpected platforms, underlining player-driven content as an enduring aspect of gaming culture.

New Overwatch Hero Sierra Introduces Controversial Ability

Blizzard revealed details about *Overwatch*’s upcoming hero Sierra, who appears to have an ability that marks targets with a dart causing her shots to curve and home in on them. This mechanic resembles an auto-aim feature, which is often contentious in shooter communities due to concerns about fairness.

Sierra also has mobility options, including grappling onto her drone and using a carpet bomb ultimate. She is set to release on April 14. Early reactions suggest that her dart ability might make her heavily banned in competitive play, echoing past controversial heroes known for circumventing player aim.

Ethically Themed Wizard School Sim Songs of Glimmerwick Launches Demo

*Songs of Glimmerwick*, a life simulation game set in a magical school, has released its first playable demo ahead of a planned 2026 launch. Developed by Eastshade Studios, the game features music-based magic, potion brewing, and gardening mechanics within a story focused on inclusivity and resistance to oppressive politics in its world.

The game aims to offer a more thoughtful and hopeful approach to the wizard school genre, avoiding problematic themes found in recent franchises. While the demo shows some rough edges, it has been praised for its charm, voice acting, and unique setting. Eastshade Studios funded development through sales of their previous title and intends for demo saves to carry over to the full release.

Insight: Excel’s Persistent 1900 Leap Year Bug

While not gaming related, an interesting technical note surfaced regarding Microsoft Excel’s treatment of the year 1900 as a leap year, which it was not. This date error originated from Lotus 1-2-3 for compatibility reasons and has been perpetuated by Microsoft for decades. Correcting the bug now would cause widespread issues with existing spreadsheets and software interoperability.

This longstanding quirk serves as a reminder of how legacy design decisions can lead to unexpected complications in modern software systems, something that could be relevant when developing or maintaining game development tools or community platforms.
