The Sims Launches Creator Marketplace and Maker Program

Electronic Arts has announced a new feature for The Sims 4 called the Sims 4 Marketplace, an in-game platform that allows players to discover and purchase user-created content, termed "Maker Packs." Alongside this, the Sims Maker Program will enable approved creators to sell their custom content directly within the Marketplace, earning 30% of sales revenue while standard base game and official expansions remain separate purchases. The Marketplace uses a new virtual currency called Moola. Players will be able to access the Marketplace on PC and Mac starting March 17, with a console release planned in the following months.

This move formalizes a paid custom content market within The Sims ecosystem, a concept reminiscent of Bethesda’s Creation Club model. For creators, it offers a clearer, EA-sanctioned channel for monetization, while for players, it introduces officially supported custom content on consoles for the first time. The program permits creators to continue offering free content on other platforms but restricts selling previously available content through the Marketplace. Applications for the Maker Program open March 5, requiring creators to meet regional, age, and quality criteria.

Bungie Details Marathon’s First Season and Seasonal Progression Resets

Bungie has revealed the first season of its upcoming game Marathon, which launches March 5. Season 1, titled "Death is the First Step," runs through June and includes new story elements such as unlocking access to the derelict UESC Marathon ship and an end-game zone called Cryo Archive. The season will also introduce Ranked mode in late March, events, new weapons, and other content.

A notable aspect is Bungie's commitment to mandatory server wipes every three months, resetting players’ gear, contracts, faction progression, and levels. Players retain earned or purchased cosmetics, titles, codex progress, and liaison contracts across seasons. Bungie argues these resets maintain game balance, keep the experience challenging, and allow new or returning players to compete without disadvantage. This design differs from other games offering optional or no wipes, and it may affect player sentiment regarding lost progress.

Tripwire Interactive Lays Off 23 Employees Amid Industry Challenges

Tripwire Interactive, known for Killing Floor and Rising Storm, has laid off 23 staff members across various departments. The studio cited "business realities" and ongoing changes within the gaming industry as reasons for the workforce reduction. The layoffs were reportedly unexpected by many team members, as described by a principal QA tester who expressed shock and disappointment.

This event is part of a broader trend of layoffs and restructuring in the games sector over the past years, influenced by market contractions, shifts in business models, and economic pressures. Other studios have faced similar workforce adjustments recently, highlighting continuing instability in game development employment despite ongoing demand for games.

Apple Increases Base Storage on MacBook Air M5 Amid Memory Market Constraints

Apple has doubled the base storage of its new MacBook Air with the M5 chip to 512GB, up from 256GB in the previous generation. Although the base price has increased by $100 to $1,099, this configuration offers more storage at a price point that, adjusted for inflation and previous models, remains competitive. The upgrade comes despite an ongoing global memory and storage "crisis" driven by increased demand from AI applications and supply constraints.

Apple's scale, purchasing power, and profit margins likely enable it to absorb higher memory costs and provide better base configurations than many competitors. However, the MacBook Air remains suboptimal for gaming due to software and hardware limitations. This development may slightly reshape the affordability landscape for basic PCs amidst rising hardware costs.

OpenAI Revises Controversial Pentagon Deal Terms Following Backlash

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has publicly acknowledged that the company rushed its agreement with the US Department of War regarding the deployment of its AI tools, leading to significant public criticism. OpenAI is amending the contract language to clarify prohibitions on using its technology for domestic surveillance, autonomous weapons control, and high-stakes automated decisions like social credit systems.

Altman emphasized the complexity of the issues and stated OpenAI’s commitment to civil liberties, adding protections consistent with the Fourth Amendment and the National Security Act. The updated terms explicitly forbid intentional use of OpenAI AI systems for surveillance of US persons. The Department of War reportedly affirmed that OpenAI’s services would not be used by intelligence agencies such as the NSA.
