**Sony Enters Competitive OLED Gaming Monitor Market with Fnatic Collaboration**

Sony has launched its new Inzone M10S II OLED gaming monitor, designed to compete directly with established dual-mode esports OLED monitors from Asus and LG. Similar to its competitors, Sony's 27-inch display offers a native resolution of 2560 x 1440 pixels with a high refresh rate up to 540 Hz. A notable feature is its dual-mode capability, allowing users to switch to a 1080p resolution at 720 Hz for competitive play, appealing to esports enthusiasts who prefer smaller screen sizes in high-refresh environments.

Sony's collaboration with Fnatic, a well-known esports brand, aims to refine the product based on pro feedback. However, the monitor’s pricing at $1,099.99 positions it as the most expensive of the trio, with LG’s 27GX790B-B currently available at $750. The market now offers multiple options for gamers seeking high-performance OLED monitors, though Sony’s premium price may impact its competitiveness until independent reviews clarify the value of the Fnatic partnership.

**Microsoft Surface Laptops See Significant Price Increases Due to Memory Shortage**

Microsoft has raised the prices of all Surface laptop models, with some configurations now costing up to $500 more than at launch. The base 13-inch Surface Pro with 16 GB RAM and 512 GB SSD now starts at around $1,500, reflecting the ongoing global shortage and increased cost of DRAM and flash memory components driven by high demand from the AI industry.

This price adjustment poses a challenge for consumers, especially considering competitors like Apple offer devices, such as the MacBook Neo, at lower prices despite having fewer resources. Microsoft's own investments in AI and data center infrastructure partly contribute to this supply pressure, illustrating wider industry struggles. The memory shortage is expected to persist for several months, likely causing further pricing challenges across the PC hardware market.

**New Graphene-Based Memristor Chip Tolerates Extreme Heat**

Researchers at USC Viterbi School of Engineering have developed a graphene tungsten memristor capable of reliable operation at temperatures up to 700°C. This exceeds the heat tolerance of conventional silicon-based memory chips, which typically fail around 200°C, making the new memristor suitable for extreme environments such as the surface of Venus, where temperatures reach over 400°C.

The device's layered composition—tungsten, hafnium oxide, and graphene—enhances thermal resilience and durability, maintaining data retention and switching performance over one billion cycles under high heat. This breakthrough has potential applications in space missions and AI workloads, where energy-efficient, high-speed memory is critical. The development follows earlier advances in high-temperature flash memory, representing ongoing progress in robust semiconductor technologies.

**Overwatch Stadium Adds Jetpack Cat and Ramattra to Roster**

Blizzard's Overwatch Stadium mode will introduce two new characters for the upcoming season: Jetpack Cat and Ramattra. Jetpack Cat is a flying hero capable of rescuing teammates mid-battle, adding a new dynamic to the mode’s gameplay. While the full range of Jetpack Cat's abilities remains undisclosed, players anticipate counter-strategies involving snipers and anti-flyer items.

Ramattra, arriving earlier in the season, also features crowd control abilities effective against airborne opponents. The additions directly influence current meta discussions, especially concerning hitscan heroes, as players prepare tactics to address these aerial threats. Community response has been mixed, with some players expressing preference for other heroes in future content updates.

**NZXT Reaches Class Action Settlement Over Controversial Flex PC Rental Program**

NZXT has reached a preliminary $3.45 million settlement in a class action lawsuit related to its Flex PC rental program. As part of the agreement, certain users who participated before the end of 2023 and meet specific criteria will be allowed to keep the rented PCs without additional payments. Others may receive debt forgiveness or cash claims.

The lawsuit addressed allegations of misleading advertising, unfair contracts, and inconsistency in the hardware provided to renters. NZXT has acknowledged issues with past influencer promotions and spec changes. The settlement highlights ongoing scrutiny of PC rental schemes amid increasing hardware costs due to component shortages, affecting consumers’ confidence and industry rental practices.
