**World of Warcraft: Players Attempt Early Raid Boss Kill Thwarted by Blizzard**

A group of inventive World of Warcraft players recently tried to defeat an upcoming raid boss, L’ura, ahead of its official release in the Midnight’s March of Quel’Danas raid, which opens next Tuesday. They exploited game mechanics and bugs, including using low-level creatures (“rats”) and a damaged monk attack to inflict massive damage while mitigating lethal environmental damage with a resurrection sickness debuff. Despite managing to reduce the boss’s health significantly, Blizzard had preemptively coded L’ura to be unkillable before the event, blocking the players’ efforts. This incident highlights Blizzard's proactive measures to control raid progression and maintain planned content schedules, reinforcing developer control over content rollout in MMOs.

**Reflections on "Eurojank": The Ambitious but Imperfect European Game Development Scene**

An in-depth article revisits the term “eurojank,” once a somewhat derogatory label for European games known for ambitious scopes but rough edges and bugs. The term now evokes a nostalgic appreciation for these titles’ creativity and willingness to experiment with open worlds and complex systems despite technical limitations. Examples like Gothic, Boiling Point, and early Witcher titles illustrate the charm and challenges of these games. However, recent years have seen many mid-size European studios fold due to financial pressures, consolidation, and evolving industry demands, including Spiders, Cyanide, and Piranha Bytes. Meanwhile, improvements in accessible game engines and industry knowledge have led to more polished European games, reducing “jank” but also changing the creative risk profile. The piece serves as both a eulogy and celebration, noting that while “eurojank” as a concept may be fading, its spirit influences current and future development.

**Inzoi Studio Head Discusses Challenges of Life Simulation Games and Early Access**

Hyungjun “Kjun” Kim, head of Inzoi Studio, shared insights about the challenges faced developing Inzoi, a Sims-inspired life sim backed by Krafton. Despite 29 years in game development, he acknowledges that Inzoi’s ambition to create an open-world life sim was difficult to fulfill fully. To manage expectations, the studio released a demo before launching in early access. While initial sales exceeded a million copies, concurrent player counts have dropped, reflecting mixed reception and the typical early access struggles, such as missing simulation content and awkward social interactions. Kim expressed sympathy for players acting as testers in early access but emphasized it was necessary since developers can't observe all results internally. The studio plans ongoing updates, including improved mod support and user-generated content tools, underlining early access as part of a longer-term development process rather than a finished product.

**Peak’s Latest Update Adds Extensive Customization Options for Players**

The climbing adventure game Peak has rolled out its "Play It Your Way Update," focusing on player control over difficulty and game features. Players can now toggle various hazards and conditions, such as fall damage, spider encounters, hunger, and even remove specific items like blowguns from the game. A notably relaxed mode labeled “Grapple Mode (Stupid)” grants unlimited-use rescue claws to all players. Moreover, the update introduces “mini runs” allowing players to attempt single biomes rather than a full expedition, appealing to those with limited time. Progress-saving campfires have been added, enhancing convenience. The update is designed to encourage accessibility and personalization, helping broaden Peak’s appeal while maintaining the core challenge for those who want it.

**FTC Raises Concerns Over Payment Companies’ “Debanking” Practices**

The U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has issued warning letters to major payment companies, including Mastercard, Visa, PayPal, and Stripe, regarding the denial of services to customers based on political or religious views. This comes in the aftermath of reports that Mastercard’s policies indirectly led to Steam removing several adult-themed games under pressure from payment processors concerned about "brand-damaging transactions." The FTC emphasized that access to payment infrastructure is essential for participation in commerce and core freedoms. However, the letters primarily reinforce a 2025 executive order addressing politically motivated “debanking.” While the move signals regulatory attention, it remains uncertain whether it will curb the opaque practices payment processors use when restricting certain content creators. The issue is especially relevant to adult and indie developers navigating payment access challenges.
