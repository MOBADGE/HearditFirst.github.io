**Marathon: New Guides for Order/Chaos and Protect/Destroy Contracts**

Players of the FPS game *Marathon* have new detailed walkthroughs available for two of its complex faction contracts. The *Order/Chaos* contract involves triggering alarms and hacking terminals across Dire Marsh. Its key steps include accessing a red terminal in a NuCaloric lab within the Complex region, defeating a spawned Security Commander to collect credentials, and downloading data from a terminal in the Maintenance hangar. Completing all objectives in one run is required, with recommended safe routes provided to avoid combat.

Following *Order/Chaos*, the *Protect/Destroy* contract tasks players with marking locations with propaganda. The trickiest step, Protect/Destroy 3, requires players to install a UV Protocol in a locked building in Intersection by shooting power boxes to gain entry. Then, players must tag three specific marked locations in the Maintenance area—on a roof wall, a northern entrance pillar, and a hangar bay pillar—all within one run. These guides clarify vague in-game instructions and help players navigate the challenging and hostile areas efficiently.

**Market Prediction: Half of Gaming Mice to Use Superstrike-like Switches in Three Years**

A marketing specialist from the mouse company Pulsar, known as FrostyZK, has shared industry insights regarding the impact of Logitech's recent G Pro X2 Superstrike. This mouse features analogue haptic switches enabling ultra-light, rapid actuation. FrostyZK predicts that in about three years, roughly 50% of gaming mice will adopt similar switch technology.

He notes the market remains divided between enthusiasts eager for new tech and pro gamers preferring established designs and heavier mice. Adoption may be gradual as users wait for their favorite brands and shapes to implement this technology. While Logitech was the first to implement haptic switches, other companies have reportedly considered but hesitated to introduce similar solutions. The prediction underlines a possible shift in gaming mouse design, though some users may stick to traditional mechanical switches for a longer time.

**Cloudflare’s Latest Report Highlights Increasing Complexity of Cyber Threats**

Cloudflare’s 2026 yearly threat report outlines a more diverse and sophisticated cyberthreat landscape. The report emphasizes a shift away from brute force attacks towards more subtle, trust-based exploitations, often leveraging legitimate tools like Google Calendar, Dropbox, and GitHub to bypass security.

Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly used by attackers to automate operations such as network mapping, exploit development, and generating deepfakes. The report cites recent scams involving deepfaked CEOs in spoofed Zoom calls as examples. AI-assisted phishing attempts have a significantly higher success rate than human-generated ones. Additionally, state-sponsored groups are targeting telecommunications infrastructure, and hyper-volumetric DDoS attacks have escalated in scale and intensity.

Cloudflare’s findings stress the need for heightened cybersecurity awareness and measures to counter evolving tactics employed by attackers.

**Graphics Card Sales Decline Continues; Nvidia Further Dominates Market**

Research from Jon Peddie shows a 4.4% drop in graphics card (add-in board) shipments in Q4 2025 compared to the prior quarter, despite a 36% year-on-year increase. Nvidia’s market share has risen to approximately 94%, while AMD’s share has decreased to about 5%, continuing a trend of Nvidia’s growing dominance following the launch of its RTX 50-series cards.

The drop in sales is linked to rising hardware costs, influenced by high memory prices and supply constraints related to AI industry demand. Fewer desktop PCs are shipping with discrete graphics cards, indicated by a decline in AIB attach rates to 55%. The report forecasts a compound annual decline rate of 5.9% for discrete graphics cards through 2028. Factors include competition from integrated graphics, high hardware prices, and economic uncertainties, suggesting a challenging environment for future GPU sales.
