**Project Zomboid Removes Malicious Steam Workshop Mods**

Developer The Indie Stone has identified and removed 14 Steam Workshop mods for Project Zomboid that contained heavily obfuscated code creating malicious files outside the game directory. The mods, all related to soundtracks from various games and media, were installed on between 500 and 2200 devices, primarily affecting the game's Build 42 testing branch. The Indie Stone has banned the user responsible and recommends affected players take thorough security measures beyond just uninstalling the mods. This incident highlights potential risks of third-party content in mod-supported games and the importance of vetting community contributions for player safety.

**World of Warcraft Player Recreates Counter-Strike’s Dust 2 Map**

A creative World of Warcraft player has remarkably reconstructed the iconic Dust 2 map from Counter-Strike within WoW’s player housing system. Using a combination of wooden shelves, doormats, and fruit plates, the map’s recognizable features and even a car were recreated despite WoW’s limited building tools. The build has attracted attention from the official World of Warcraft social accounts, underscoring the flexibility and creativity afforded by player housing features. This example illustrates how in-game creation tools can foster unique cross-genre fan projects that engage communities beyond the original game’s scope.

**Hazelight Studios Surpasses 50 Million Game Sales**

Hazelight Studios, known for co-op action games like A Way Out, It Takes Two, and the recent Split Fiction, announced it has sold over 50 million copies of its three titles combined in under a decade. Sales figures show It Takes Two at 30 million, A Way Out at 13 million, and Split Fiction at 7 million, despite Split Fiction being on sale for just one year. The studio emphasizes ongoing community engagement and has hinted at a new game in development. This milestone confirms Hazelight’s strong position in the cooperative adventure genre and suggests continued interest in narrative-driven multiplayer experiences.

**ChainStaff Offers a Different Take on Retro Shooters**

Indie developer Mommy’s Best Games released ChainStaff, a psychedelic alien shooter that draws on lesser-known vintage inspirations rather than the usual retro classics. Featuring surreal art reminiscent of Psygnosis titles and classic run-and-gun designs, ChainStaff combines unusual enemies and landscapes with modern gameplay twists like a versatile grappling weapon. It stands out by embracing a more obscure side of gaming history, demonstrating that the retro genre can still innovate by exploring new artistic and design influences beyond familiar archetypes.

**Microsoft Explains Why Excel’s 1900 Leap Year Bug Will Remain**

Microsoft confirmed that Excel’s long-standing mistake—treating 1900 as a leap year, which it was not—will not be fixed due to compatibility issues. This behavior originated from Lotus 1-2-3’s design for simplifying date calculations and was retained by Excel to maintain compatibility with existing spreadsheets. Fixing the error would cause dates to shift, break formulas, and create incompatibility with other programs. The bug only affects dates before March 1, 1900, a range rarely used by most Excel users. This example underscores the practical trade-offs in software maintenance between correctness and backward compatibility in widely adopted tools.

**New Overwatch Hero Sierra’s Aim-Assisting Ability Raises Debate**

Blizzard revealed Sierra, an upcoming Overwatch hero whose secondary fire appears to auto-aim by marking targets with a dart that curves bullets toward them. This mechanic resembles an aim-assist or homing effect, a feature often unpopular among competitive shooter players due to concerns over fairness. Sierra also has abilities including grappling and area bombarding with ultimate. Her debut on April 14 will reveal how her kit balances power and skill requirements. Early impressions suggest Sierra may provoke discussion about acceptable target-assisting mechanics in competitive play, similar to past debates around heroes with aim assistance.
