Five New Steam Games You Might Have Missed

A batch of lesser-known games recently launched on Steam, offering a variety of gameplay styles and themes:

- **Stiff Neck** is a visual novel mixing psychological horror with a small-town cop story. It features a unique setting that's hard to pin down in time, and complex relationship dynamics. - **Which Sausage, Mate?** is a life simulation about a Polish man who loses his mining job and must survive by working retail while managing tight finances. - **Wasteland Bites** combines food truck management with shooting action in a post-apocalyptic world, blending resource gathering and combat. - **It Has My Face** introduces a roguelike first-person experience where players hunt doppelgangers in a procedurally generated hellscape. It includes an online multiplayer mode for up to eight players. - **Grime 2** is a sequel to a Souls-like metroidvania with combat-focused gameplay. The game adds mechanics such as enemy-based mold shaping for abilities.

These releases show the diversity still present in Steam's new game influx, despite many titles flying under the radar.

Pillars of Eternity Gains New Turn-Based Mode

Eleven years after its release, *Pillars of Eternity* received a significant update introducing a full turn-based combat mode. Originally known for its real-time with pause (RTWP) system, the new mode changes how actions and movements are handled, allowing multiple turns per round and emphasizing tactical decision-making.

This approach improves upon the earlier turn-based implementation seen in *Pillars of Eternity 2: Deadfire*, addressing balance issues between character builds and making certain classes, like Ciphers, more viable. The developers have brought back the recovery mechanic affecting action order and turn frequency.

For both newcomers and series veterans, this new mode offers a different way to experience the game’s complex combat, potentially broadening its appeal.

Zelda: Twilight Princess PC Port Nears Completion

Fans of Nintendo’s *The Legend of Zelda: Twilight Princess* are getting closer to an official native PC port thanks to a volunteer decompilation project called *Twilight Princess: Courage Reborn*. Using reverse-engineered code from the original GameCube version, the project adds features like 60 fps support, multi-platform compatibility (Windows, Mac, Linux), and customizable graphics options.

The port is still in development but shows promising stability and gameplay smoothness. In anticipation of the release, modders have already started adding content such as the alternative character Linkle.

Players wanting to try this port will need to supply their own game assets from a legitimate copy, as the project does not include proprietary Nintendo content.

OpenAI’s Reported Internal Plan to Pit World Leaders

A recent New Yorker feature revealed controversial internal discussions at OpenAI involving a proposed strategy to position the company as a tool to encourage competition among world powers like China and Russia. The idea was to create a scenario where governments would compete to fund OpenAI to avoid falling behind in AI capabilities, described metaphorically as a “prisoner’s dilemma.”

OpenAI denies that this plan was seriously considered, calling such claims “ridiculous.” However, former employees maintain there was genuine support for the idea at high levels before employee backlash caused it to be abandoned.

This insight adds context to ongoing debates about AI development ethics, corporate power, and geopolitical dynamics around emerging technologies.

Linux to Drop Support for Intel 486 CPUs After Nearly 30 Years

After almost three decades since Intel’s 486 processors ceased production, Linux maintainers are beginning to remove support for this platform in the upcoming Linux 7.1 kernel. The Intel 486 debuted in 1989 and marked a milestone in PC hardware history, though its performance and capabilities are now considered obsolete.

Linux creator Linus Torvalds supports this move, noting that such old hardware is primarily of historical interest, and its support complicates modern kernel development. Reportedly, no active users rely on recent Linux versions on 486-class machines anymore.

Support for newer processors such as Pentium models will remain for the foreseeable future, allowing continued use of somewhat older but still functional hardware.
