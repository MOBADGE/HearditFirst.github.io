Overwatch’s Big Hero Drop Influenced by Marvel Rivals

At this year's Game Developers Conference, Overwatch 2 director Aaron Keller revealed that the multiplayer shooter’s recent introduction of five new heroes was partly inspired by the launch strategy of NetEase’s Marvel Rivals. Marvel Rivals debuted with an unusually large and fast-paced roster expansion, reaching nearly the same number of characters as Overwatch 2—47 compared to Overwatch’s 50 after a decade. Keller noted how the excitement among new players for a big hero drop in Rivals led Blizzard to consider how Overwatch fans might react to several new heroes simultaneously. While Blizzard didn’t add 30 heroes at once, as Marvel Rivals effectively did, it did introduce five characters in the latest season, including the notably unique Jetpack Cat. Early signs suggest this approach has helped sustain rising player counts over consecutive weeks, a trend not seen since Overwatch 2’s 2022 launch. Blizzard plans to add another five heroes later this year.

Baby Steps’ Level Design Challenges Player Expectations

Baby Steps, recognized as one of 2025’s notable games, embraces a level design philosophy that eschews traditional playtesting and player-friendly polish. Co-creator Gabe Cuzzillo discussed at GDC how the game intentionally incorporates “indifference to the player,” challenging players to tackle climbing obstacles not guaranteed to be fully navigable. The developers placed stacks of cans atop various plausible climbing spots without confirming their climbability. Surprisingly, players managed to climb all but one, turning that unreachable can pile into an open-ended puzzle inviting creativity and exploration. This approach contrasts with typical singleplayer designs focused on guidance and minimized frustration, instead fostering an environment where players’ curiosity and problem-solving define the experience.

Industry Investor Expresses Concern Over AI Backlash

Generative AI remains a contentious topic at this year’s Game Developers Conference. Moritz Baier-Lentz, head of gaming at venture fund Lightspeed Venture Partners, expressed disappointment at the gaming industry’s largely negative stance on generative AI. While a recent GDC survey found over half of developers view AI as harmful to games, Baier-Lentz described this as “demonizing” a promising technology. He suggested much of the apprehension stems from job security fears following recent layoffs, alongside concerns about artistic consent, environmental impact, and content quality. Representatives from AI-focused companies at the conference emphasized that, despite current imperfections, generative AI tools have potential to augment creativity and improve development workflows. The divide was visible on the expo floor, with union advocacy booths positioned near startups promoting AI-driven game creation, highlighting ongoing tensions in the industry.

Bungie Reverses Marathon Audio Range Increase After Player Feedback

Bungie acknowledged that a recent update to its fast-paced shooter Marathon increased the audible range of gunfire and explosions too much, negatively affecting gameplay balance. The change led to players finding it more difficult to complete objectives without constant harassment from opponents, as noise now attracted attention from farther away, encouraging near-constant PvP combat. In response to widespread criticism, Bungie stated they had “overcorrected” and plan to reduce the audio range again in an upcoming patch to restore a more intuitive and comfortable experience. The developer’s goal was originally to provide players with more situational awareness, but the new settings had the unintended effect of making stealth and strategic play less viable.

Development Insights: The Outer Worlds 2 Difficulty Balancing

Obsidian senior systems designer Robert Donovan shared insights on balancing the difficulty curve in The Outer Worlds 2 during a GDC session. The game sought to maintain a design philosophy where “weak things are weak, and strong things are strong,” inspired by the first game’s opening area. Initial attempts to flatten player power progression led to gains feeling insignificant, so the team shifted to more impactful percentage-based improvements. To balance challenge, the team focused on a “hits to kill” model rather than abstract health scaling, aiming for a fair but noticeable difficulty on Normal mode. Difficulty settings above and below Normal apply straightforward multipliers. Donovan acknowledged the challenge of satisfying the wide variety of player skill levels, noting that some players find the game too easy while others find it too hard. The approach reflects ongoing difficulties in tuning RPG difficulty to balance accessibility with depth.
