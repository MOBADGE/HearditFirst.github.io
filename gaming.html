**Reminiscences from 1989 Reveal Early Industry Challenges and Changes** Newly uncovered recordings from the 1989 Computer Game Developers Conference provide a window into the formative years of the PC gaming industry. Developers such as Robert Garriott and Steve Cartwright recalled the tight-knit nature of early game design, where experience was scarce and salaries could be easily doubled to attract talent—an era now long past. Discussions highlighted concerns about market saturation even at that time, and a sense that publishers had become more market-driven compared to a decade prior, when experimental and product-focused approaches prevailed. These reflections underscore how specialization and marketing have evolved into major facets of game production, while also reminding readers that some foundational challenges have persisted.

**Blizzard Recruiting Lead Designer for Unnamed Open-World Shooter** Blizzard Entertainment recently posted a job listing seeking a lead designer with experience in Unreal Engine to helm development on a new "AAA open-world shooter." This represents a departure from Blizzard’s traditional use of proprietary engines and suggests the studio is exploring new genres and technologies. Speculation links the project to a previously reported Starcraft-themed shooter under incubation. However, given Blizzard’s history of canceled projects and recent layoffs, the game's future remains uncertain. The announcement is notable as Blizzard prepares for its upcoming BlizzCon event, where further details might emerge.

**Valve Tests Updated Steam Storefront Design** Valve is currently beta-testing a refreshed Steam store homepage aimed at improving content discoverability and visual cohesion. Key changes include enhanced "Featured and Recommended" sections with more detailed explanations and user review summaries, larger artwork for discounts and events, and new wishlist and DLC sections. The Discovery Queue now appears as an overlay for easier browsing, and infinite scrolling has been introduced. While the updates are subtle rather than transformative, they leverage wider, higher-resolution displays more effectively. Steam users in the beta program can access the new layout now; wider rollout depends on testing results.

**Diablo 4 Introduces April Fowl’s Day Event Featuring Unique Chicken Boss** For April Fool’s Day, Diablo 4 added "Chik’han, The Fowl Beast," a large chicken world boss that drops unique, themed loot items such as the "Cluckonomicon" staff and the "Eggcecutioner" scythe. Unlike typical Diablo 4 events that focus on progression boosts, this event offers a playful twist with unusual weapon effects tied to poultry. The event’s achievement title implies it may return in future years. This addition stands out as a more creative and engaging seasonal event, contrasting with the game’s generally scaled-back approach to special activities since its launch.

**US Patent Office Moves to Revoke Nintendo’s 'Summoned Character Battle' Patent** The United States Patent and Trademark Office issued a nonfinal decision rejecting all claims of Nintendo’s controversial US patent 12,403,397, which covers a specific system for battling summoned sub-characters. This comes after criticism of the patent’s initial approval as overly broad and problematic for game development. The patent described detailed mechanics of summoning and controlling sub-characters in battle, but the USPTO examiner cited prior art from multiple sources—including other game patents and community discussions—to conclude the design was not sufficiently novel. Nintendo may respond and appeal, but this move reflects ongoing scrutiny of patents in game mechanics.

**Garry’s Mod Successor s&box Scheduled for Steam Release on April 28** Facepunch Studios announced a Steam release date of April 28 for s&box, the spiritual successor to Garry’s Mod. The announcement was low-key, appearing in a small update rather than a formal reveal. Notably, Facepunch recently secured a licensing deal with Valve allowing s&box users to create and sell standalone games on Steam without revenue sharing with Facepunch, potentially encouraging a new wave of Source 2 engine content. Pricing has not been confirmed but is expected to be between $10 and $20. The release marks a significant moment two decades after Garry’s Mod helped shape sandbox multiplayer experiences.
