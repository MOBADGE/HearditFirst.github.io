Microsoft Ends 'This is an Xbox' Campaign Amid Shift in Strategy

Microsoft is quietly removing its 'This is an Xbox' marketing campaign, which had promoted the idea that nearly any device could act as an Xbox through cloud gaming. Launched in late 2024, the campaign emphasized Xbox’s move away from traditional consoles towards a cloud and PC hybrid model, allowing gameplay on phones, smart TVs, and even car screens. However, following the recent announcement of the next Xbox console, codenamed Project Helix—a hybrid Xbox-PC running Windows 11—Microsoft appears to be retiring this broad messaging. The company has taken down the original campaign’s announcement and related website tags, though some ads remain on Xbox’s YouTube channel.

This change signals a shift back to highlighting the tangible hardware of the new Xbox rather than abstract cloud-based identity. While it doesn’t imply abandoning cloud gaming, Microsoft seems to want to better define the upcoming Xbox device as a distinct product, rather than a concept spread across many platforms.

Copper Foil Price Increase Could Raise PC Component Costs

Mitsui Kinzoku, a major supplier of copper foil used in printed circuit boards, announced a 12% price increase effective April 2026. This follows record-high copper prices, driven by a complex mix of global supply challenges, tariff-induced market uncertainty, and continued strong demand, including significant consumption increases from the AI industry. Copper foil is crucial in the manufacturing of many electronics components, including those found in PCs.

The price hike is likely to amplify an existing trend of rising costs for PC components, a situation already strained by memory supply issues and previous metal scams affecting manufacturers like Thermal Grizzly. Additionally, Mitsubishi Gas Chemical announced a 30% price rise for some related materials. These developments suggest that higher PC hardware prices may persist or worsen as foundational material costs increase.

Fortnite Players Push Back Against V-Bucks Price Hike

Epic Games revealed that the in-game currency V-Bucks will become more expensive due to rising operational costs for Fortnite. This change has provoked significant dissatisfaction within the player base, with some cancelling their Fortnite Crew subscriptions and organizing a potential boycott on March 19. The backlash centers on feeling like paying the same amount yields fewer V-Bucks, impacting the value proposition for players.

Epic developers have responded publicly, emphasizing ongoing commitment to Fortnite’s gameplay and explaining that increased revenue supports the development of new content and features. Despite these appeals, the community remains unsettled, reflecting broader tensions around game monetization and player expectations.

Nearly Half of Unreleased Games Have Official Discord Servers

Data presented at the 2026 Game Developers Conference shows that 48% of unreleased games already maintain official Discord servers. These platforms are often used for community engagement and as news distribution channels, sometimes replacing traditional forums. Developers reportedly start community-building efforts around 18 months before release.

While Discord integration aids developer-player communication, it also raises concerns about reliance on a single tech platform with recent controversies around privacy and data partnerships. Despite misgivings, Discord remains a cornerstone of game community management in the current industry landscape.

New Society Simulation MMO by Former EVE Online Developers

Klang Games, founded by two former EVE Online developers, showcased their ambitious MMO called Seed. Set during GDC, the game emphasizes a persistent, society-driven world where player-controlled characters (Seedlings) continue operating even when the player is offline. Players participate in managing societies that can range from small villages to large cyberpunk cities, with player-driven economies, laws, and politics shaping the world.

Seed mixes deep simulation elements resembling RimWorld, The Sims, and Dwarf Fortress and aims to appeal to both strategy and “caretaker” style players through detailed character management and social interactions powered partly by AI. The game is currently in closed beta, with a wider release timeline yet to be announced.
