World of Warcraft Add-On Changes Continue Following substantial restrictions on what add-ons can access during combat introduced in the Midnight expansion pre-patch, Blizzard plans further adjustments via patch 12.0.1. The changes aim to reduce dependency on add-ons that automate or simplify raid mechanics, instead encouraging core game changes to improve accessibility. While initial restrictions broke many UI and accessibility mods unintentionally, Blizzard is working with community developers to ease issues and improve built-in tools such as damage meters and cooldown managers. The company anticipates ongoing balancing and tuning as players and developers adapt, acknowledging that some player workarounds will arise and will be managed accordingly.

4. Enshrouded Roadmap Updated: 1.0 Scheduled for Fall 2026 Survival crafting game Enshrouded has shifted its official 1.0 release window to fall 2026, according to a developer roadmap update. The change is a refinement from the earlier spring target and reflects ongoing feature work and polish. Upcoming updates in spring will include community content sharing features, general improvements, and bug fixes. Post-1.0 plans involve expanded areas, deeper combat depth, optimization for lower-end systems, and quality-of-life improvements. The developers indicate continued support and content updates for years following 1.0, including both free updates and paid expansions.

Ubisoft Orders Full Return to Office and Announces Further Studio Closures In a new corporate directive, Ubisoft has mandated a full return to on-site work for all employees, eliminating remote work options. The company attributes this to team collaboration needs but faces criticism due to the disruptive nature of sudden return-to-office (RTO) policies. This directive follows recent layoffs and restructuring efforts, including studio closures and project cancellations, notably the cancellation of Prince of Persia: The Sand of Time remake. Critics see the return-to-office mandate as a strategy that may push employees to resign, effectively serving as "soft layoffs." Labor unions have condemned the policy and called for strikes, highlighting concerns over working conditions and the impact on workers.
