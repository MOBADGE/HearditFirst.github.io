Resident Evil Requiem Details Reveal Character as a Reddit User

An out-of-bounds camera exploration in Resident Evil Requiem has uncovered a small in-game detail: during a flashback scene, the young character Grace Ashcroft is shown browsing a subreddit called r/amateurjournalists. The posts on the subreddit reference events from Resident Evil 7 and tie into the game's story elements, including mentions of the Baker family and government experiments. This Easter egg adds a modern, meta layer to the game’s narrative by portraying Grace as a fan of internet conspiracy theories and discussions related to the Resident Evil universe. This kind of detail is notable for players who enjoy delving deeper into games’ lore and appreciating subtle world-building touches.

Rare Japanese Game Cookie’s Bustle Comes to Light After Copyright Conflict

The Video Game History Foundation (VGHF) has succeeded in preserving and sharing content related to Cookie’s Bustle, an obscure point-and-click adventure from 1999 by the now-defunct Rodik studio. Previously, copyright claims by a company named Graceware suppressed gameplay videos, screenshots, and fan material, effectively erasing public traces of the game online. VGHF, holding a physical copy, pushed back against takedown notices, arguing that Graceware’s DMCA claims lack sufficient proof of ownership and overreach by targeting even articles discussing the game. This development is significant for game preservationists and historians as it prevents the loss of niche and culturally specific games from the public record.

Highguard Releases Final Update Ahead of Shutdown

Highguard, a multiplayer online shooter, is set to shut down on March 12, but has received a substantial final update adding new content. This includes a new playable character, Koldo, featuring supportive abilities such as a protective "Soul Shield," and a weapon called the Switchback that functions as both a shotgun and a rifle. The update also introduces skill trees and account progression systems, which were unfinished at launch. The decision to release new features so close to closure reflects the developers’ effort to support the remaining player base. Highguard’s closure follows modest engagement metrics, marking the end of its lifecycle after attracting over two million players primarily on PS5.

Hunt: Showdown to Hide Extraction Points in Upcoming Event

Crytek’s extraction shooter Hunt: Showdown will experiment with hidden extraction and supply points during its March 18 “Devil’s Trail” event. Players will need to discover these points by exploring the environment or using in-game Scouting Maps rather than having them appear on the map at the match’s start. This change aims to add unpredictability and tension to the extraction phase, forcing players to adapt on the fly rather than heading directly to known safe zones. Additional features include new scout towers containing resources and tarot cards, as well as expanded environmental persistence to enhance immersion. This approach underlines Crytek’s ongoing efforts to refine extraction gameplay through iterative testing.

US Government Considers Compelling Tencent to Divest US Gaming Assets

Reports indicate that the US government is actively debating whether to require Chinese conglomerate Tencent to divest its holdings in major US and Finnish game companies over national security concerns. Tencent owns or holds stakes in numerous major studios, including Riot Games, Epic Games, and Ubisoft. The concerns revolve around data privacy and potential foreign access to personal user data. Past efforts to restrict Tencent’s involvement in the US gaming market began under the previous Trump administration but have yet to result in forced sales. This debate occurs amid a broader context of geopolitical tensions and recent corporate ownership shifts influenced by political affiliations. The outcome could significantly affect the industry’s investment landscape and control.

Standardized Test Highlights Difficulty of Completing 1980s Adventure Game

A recent first-of-its-kind exam, the Adventure Game Aptitude Test (AGAT), challenged over 800 participants to complete the 1987 graphic adventure Maniac Mansion without using any walkthroughs or external help while being monitored via webcam. Only two people passed within the four-hour time limit, illustrating the difficulty and complexity of classic adventure games. The test’s strict monitoring aimed to uphold fairness and prevent cheating, adding to the challenge. The low pass rate underscores both the demanding design of older games and players’ reliance on online guides today, offering insight into gaming skills and behaviors in modern contexts. The event also gathered community interest, with streams and post-test discussions shedding light on player experiences.
