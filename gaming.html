91-Year-Old Gamer Causes Police Welfare Check by Missing Calls

In Westlake, Ohio, police responded to a welfare check for a 91-year-old woman who had missed several daily "Are You Okay?" program calls. Instead of finding an emergency, officers discovered she was simply engrossed in playing video games, trying to beat her record level. The incident highlights how gaming spans all ages, though media coverage often treats elderly gamers as novelty. The story also raises questions about assumptions around missed wellness calls and the absence of details like the specific game she was playing. This event underscores the growing presence of older adults in gaming communities.

Pickmon Rebrands to Pickmos Amid Controversy Over Imitation

The creature-collecting survival game formerly known as Pickmon has changed its name to Pickmos, altering just one letter. Publisher NetworkGo framed this as a move to “better align with our brand identity,” though it comes amid criticism for heavily borrowing designs from established franchises such as Pokémon, Final Fantasy, and even Palworld. Legal pressure from Nintendo against similar imitators may have influenced the rebranding. Despite the name change, public sentiment remains skeptical about Pickmos’s creative originality and potential trademark confusion, especially given its visual similarities to popular IPs.

Fable Studio Affirms Autumn 2026 Release Despite Delay Rumors

Playground Games and Microsoft have reiterated that the Fable reboot is scheduled for fall 2026, dismissing rumors of a delay linked to Grand Theft Auto 6’s launch. GTA 6 is set for November 19, 2026, and industry speculation suggested Fable might postpone to avoid clashing with such a major release. However, Playground Games expressed confidence in their timeline on social media. The discussion reflects common industry challenges in timing releases amidst blockbuster competition. Ultimately, only an official launch or delay announcement will confirm the final date.

Resident Evil: Requiem Mod Lets Players Resize Enemies

Modder lucablight83 has released a mod for Resident Evil: Requiem that allows players to scale enemy sizes from tiny (10% of normal) to large (up to 300%). This provides an alternative way to adjust difficulty and atmosphere by turning usually intimidating foes into miniature versions or towering threats. The mod requires the RE Framework to work and introduces some physics quirks when enemies are shrunk. This mod adds a humorous and customizable layer to the game’s horror experience, offering players new options in engaging with its challenges.

Valve Developer Improves Linux Gaming on 8 GB VRAM Cards

Valve Linux developer Natalie Vock introduced a kernel patch and utilities that optimize VRAM management on Linux systems with 8 GB graphics cards, notably benefiting Arch-based distributions like CachyOS. The patch prevents performance degradation caused by the kernel overcommitting VRAM and swapping memory to system RAM, which previously affected some games. Though currently Arch-focused, the fix may expand to other distributions over time. This improvement is significant for Linux gamers holding mid-range hardware, especially amid rising memory costs and ongoing adoption of Linux for gaming.

Minisforum AtomMan G1 Pro Review: Compact Performance with Thermal Trade-Offs

The Minisforum AtomMan G1 Pro is a compact 3.8-liter mini gaming PC that combines a Ryzen 9 8945HX laptop CPU with a Gigabyte RTX 5060 low-profile GPU. Priced around $1,440, it blurs the lines between gaming laptops and entry-level ITX desktops. Performance tests show solid gaming results at 1440p with titles like Cyberpunk 2077 and Metro Exodus, aided by DLSS support, and capable CPU multi-threaded benchmarks. However, its small form factor leads to high CPU temperatures, occasionally reaching 91°C in gaming sessions, indicating thermal limitations. The device offers portability and good build quality but provides limited I/O options, which may require peripherals. Overall, it marks a practical choice for space-conscious users seeking capable gaming in a very small package.

Linux Kernel 7.0 Released with Incremental Improvements

Linux kernel version 7.0 has been released, continuing the tradition of quieter launches focused on numerous small enhancements rather than dramatic changes. Highlights include a self-healing system for XFS drives, improved drive health monitoring, memory management optimizations, and support for new CPU and GPU architectures such as Intel’s Nova Lake.
