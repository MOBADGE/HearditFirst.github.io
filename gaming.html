The Sims Launches New Marketplace and Maker Program for Paid Custom Content

Electronic Arts announced the upcoming launch of the Sims 4 Marketplace on March 17 for PC and Mac, with a later release planned for consoles. This in-game platform allows players to buy curated "Maker Packs" of custom content, created by approved community creators, using a new virtual currency called Moola. Content creators will set their own prices and earn 30% of sales revenue.

Alongside the Marketplace, EA introduced the Sims Maker Program, enabling verified creators to sell their work directly through the Marketplace. Applications open on March 5 with eligibility criteria including age, location, and portfolio review. The program is designed to bring official paid custom content to consoles for the first time, while free content can still be offered on external sites but not sold via the Marketplace.

This move resembles Bethesda’s Creation Club in structure, but comes at a time when the Sims community has shown more willingness to pay for quality content. It also aims to clarify EA's previous inconsistent stance on paid mods. Bringing official monetization to custom content may stabilize the ecosystem and widen access to paid creations.

Bungie Details Marathon’s First Season and Its Mandatory Loot Resets

Bungie revealed the start of the first seasonal content update for its upcoming game Marathon, launching on March 5. Season 1, titled “Death is the First Step,” runs through June and includes new story elements, events, and a community-unlockable zone called Cryo Archive. Ranked multiplayer mode and additional weapons will arrive in late March.

Significantly, Bungie confirmed that every three months, at the start of a new season, all player progress, gear, and faction standings will be reset. The intention is to maintain a challenging and balanced environment where returning and new players can compete on a level playing field. Players will retain cosmetics, titles, and certain system progressions through resets.

While seasonal wipes are common in looter shooters, this strict approach contrasts with some titles that offer optional resets. It remains to be seen how Marathon’s player base will respond to losing character progress regularly.

Tripwire Interactive Announces Layoffs Amid Industry Challenges

Tripwire Interactive, known for Killing Floor and Rising Storm, announced layoffs affecting 23 employees across various departments. The studio cited changing “business realities” and the need to align long-term strategy with industry conditions as reasons for the cuts.

The layoffs surprised many at the company and reflect broader industry trends marked by contraction and workforce reductions seen across studios in recent years. Tripwire emphasized continued dedication to high-quality games and player support despite downsizing.

This development adds to an ongoing pattern of layoffs reported in early 2026, highlighting ongoing volatility in the games sector despite some companies’ growth.

Apple Doubles Base Storage on New MacBook Air M5 Despite Memory Supply Tightness

Apple has introduced a new MacBook Air model powered by the M5 chip with 512 GB of storage in its base configuration, doubling the previous 256 GB baseline. The updated model costs $1,099, $100 more than the prior generation, but represents a substantial storage increase at a still-competitive price.

This comes amid an ongoing global memory supply crisis driven by surging AI demand, which has generally caused price increases across PC components. Apple’s scale, purchasing volume, and profit margins likely allow it to absorb these pressures more effectively than other manufacturers.

While this is a positive development for storage capacity in affordable laptops, Mac hardware remains less suitable for gaming due to software compatibility limitations.

OpenAI Revises Pentagon AI Deal Following Public Backlash

Following significant criticism over its AI contract with the US Department of War, OpenAI’s CEO Sam Altman announced amendments to the agreement to clarify restrictions against using AI tools for domestic surveillance, autonomous weapons, and high-stakes automated decisions.

Altman acknowledged the initial announcement was rushed, leading to confusion and backlash including a surge in ChatGPT uninstalls. The updated terms explicitly prohibit AI use for surveilling US persons, aligning with constitutional and legal safeguards.

OpenAI stated the agreement contains more guardrails than previous classified AI contracts and emphasized ongoing collaboration with government authorities to address safety and ethical issues. Despite efforts to reassure the public, negative sentiment remains high.
