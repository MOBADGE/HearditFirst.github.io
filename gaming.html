Nvidia CEO Highlights Company's Role in Shaping Modern Video Games

At the Morgan Stanley Media and Telecom Conference, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang emphasized Nvidia’s critical role in the development of the modern video game industry. Huang stated that Nvidia helped create the industry by pioneering computer graphics algorithms and technologies that underpin today’s 3D gaming experiences. He cited Nvidia’s contributions to graphics libraries, ray tracing (RTX), and game engine integrations as foundational to how games look and perform on current hardware.

While Huang’s claim that Nvidia "created the modern videogame industry" drew some skepticism—given that video games predate Nvidia by decades—there is broad acknowledgment of Nvidia's influence, particularly through its GPU architectures and software technologies like CUDA. Huang also noted Nvidia’s dominant position as the world’s largest game platform, thanks in part to its hardware’s popularity among developers and gamers. Nonetheless, the company’s relationship with gamers is complex, as issues like high graphics card prices and certain technology features have caused some friction.

Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella Credits Intel and Apple for Foundational Success

Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, in a candid discussion at the Morgan Stanley conference, reflected on the company’s historic dependence on contributions from Intel and Apple. Nadella noted that without Intel's processors, Windows might not have succeeded, and without Apple’s Macintosh, Office software might not have developed as it did.

This acknowledgment highlights how early collaborations and platform availability shaped Microsoft's flagship products—Windows was closely tied to Intel-based PCs, while initial versions of Office applications were developed for the Mac’s more advanced graphical interface. Nadella framed this history as an example of "non-zero sum" relationships in tech, emphasizing cooperation rather than competition when it benefits customers.

Intel Repositions 18A Chip Node for External Customers, But Nvidia Manufacturing Unlikely

Intel’s CFO Dave Zinsner revealed at the Morgan Stanley Conference that the company’s advanced 18A chip manufacturing node, initially designated solely for internal use, is now being positioned for use by external customers. The 18A node, which debuted with Intel’s new Panther Lake CPUs, is now in full production and reportedly attracting customer interest.

However, Zinsner clarified that Intel’s recent collaboration with Nvidia is focused on product integration rather than Intel manufacturing Nvidia GPUs. While Intel is expanding its foundry business, Nvidia GPUs are not expected to be produced on Intel’s 18A node in the near term. This update reflects Intel’s ongoing efforts to strengthen its foundry services after prior challenges with delays and production issues.

Deus Ex: Invisible War’s Console Limitations Traced to Publisher’s Market Assumptions

In a retrospective shared by Deus Ex: Invisible War director Harvey Smith, some of the game’s well-known technical limitations on the original Xbox were linked to publisher Eidos’s beliefs that first-person shooters (FPS) and RPG games did not sell well on consoles. This led to a strategic focus on console development, forcing design compromises due to limited hardware resources.

Smith highlighted that levels were smaller and more confined to accommodate memory restrictions, which disappointed fans expecting the open, expansive environments of the original Deus Ex. The decision to prioritize consoles was also influenced by the relative success of a prior Deus Ex port to PlayStation 2. These factors contributed to a game that angered some of the franchise’s core followers due to its constrained scope and design.

Elgato’s Free Wave Link Audio Mixer Now Supports Any Microphone

Elgato updated its Wave Link audio mixing software to support any microphone recognized by Windows or Mac, removing the previous limitation of requiring Elgato hardware. Wave Link allows users to mix multiple audio sources with individualized effects such as compression and equalization, all within an intuitive interface.

This expansion broadens Wave Link’s appeal beyond Elgato device owners, offering streamers, content creators, and gamers a flexible and free audio mixing tool. While there are some limitations—such as a maximum of eight input sources and effects applying uniformly across mixes—the software is praised for its ease of use and potential to simplify audio management during streaming, recording, or gaming sessions.
