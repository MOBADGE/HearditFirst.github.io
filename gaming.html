Slay the Spire Sets New Steam Player Record During Holiday Sale

Nearly seven years after its initial release, the deckbuilding roguelike Slay the Spire has reached a new all-time peak for concurrent players on Steam. The record of over 57,000 players eclipses the previous high of about 33,000 from its early access days in 2018. This surge coincides with the game's unprecedented low price of $2.49 during the 2025 winter Steam sale, making it an accessible entry point for new players and returning fans.

Interest is also likely fueled by anticipation for Slay the Spire 2, a sequel highly awaited by the community. Additionally, the release of a cooperative board game adaptation may have drawn in players curious about the franchise. This renewed interest demonstrates the longevity of a well-crafted game and hints at robust engagement ahead of the sequel's launch.

Arm Chips and PC Gaming: Progress Delayed but Momentum Builds

The anticipated mainstream adoption of Arm-based chips in PC gaming did not materialize substantially in 2025 despite early optimism around Qualcomm‚Äôs Snapdragon X CPUs. Performance challenges remain, mainly due to the scarcity of PC games compiled natively for Arm architectures and the reliance on Microsoft's Windows on Arm Prism emulation layer. This emulation adds overhead and inconsistency in game performance.

Qualcomm's follow-up Snapdragon X2 continues to emphasize gaming capability, but as a low-power mobile SoC with integrated graphics, it falls short of replacing traditional x86 platforms for demanding games. Nvidia is entering with its own Arm chip featuring powerful graphics, yet it faces similar software compatibility hurdles.

On a positive note, Valve has developed SteamOS for Arm with its own x86 emulation, highlighting industry interest in this architecture. Overall, significant Arm progress in PC gaming is expected further out, possibly by 2028, as hardware matures and software support improves.

2025 Marks a Shift in First-Person Shooter Trends

The first-person shooter (FPS) genre saw noticeable changes in 2025, with a move away from extreme competitiveness and skill-based ranking toward more casual, enjoyable experiences. Battlefield 6 emerged as the year's leading FPS, notable for its lack of ranked modes and appeal to players seeking fun without high emotional investment.

This period also saw backlash against over-the-top cosmetic items, prompting developers like EA and Activision to adjust their approach toward more grounded visual designs. Additionally, traditionally competitive shooters, including Rainbow Six Siege and Splitgate 2, introduced more casual and cooperative modes to broaden their audience.

Purely cooperative shooters like Helldivers 2, Warhammer 40,000: Darktide, and Deep Rock Galactic maintained strong communities, underscoring demand for social and less stressful multiplayer experiences. Furthermore, players demonstrated willingness to purchase complete, content-rich shooters rather than rely solely on free-to-play offerings.
