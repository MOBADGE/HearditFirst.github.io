Nightdive Studios Remasters PO'ed, an Early 3DO Console FPS

Nightdive Studios has released a remastered version of *PO'ed*, a 1995 first-person shooter originally for the 3DO console. The game is now available on PC for the first time. *PO'ed* attempted to bring FPS action to consoles before the genre became popular there with titles like *GoldenEye 007* and *Halo*. It featured unusual weapons like a frying pan, a jetpack with six-degrees-of-freedom movement, and a lighthearted tone with offbeat humor. However, despite these ambitions, *PO'ed* was widely regarded as clunky and poorly designed, with confusing level layouts, unsettling enemy designs, and frustrating controls. The remaster preserves the original experience without attempting to fix its many flaws, aligning with Nightdive’s goal of historical preservation rather than improvement. This release provides a unique look at early console FPS attempts that struggled to compete with the PC's dominance at the time.

New Indie Pass Subscription Service Launches Amid Skepticism

A new subscription service called Indie Pass will launch on April 13, offering access to a curated collection of around 70 indie games for $6.99 per month. The service is backed by publisher indie.io and pays developers based on how long subscribers play their games. While clearly inspired by Microsoft's Game Pass model, Indie Pass has received a mostly skeptical reception from the gaming community. Critics point out that many indie games are already inexpensive, questioning why players would prefer subscription access over direct purchases that better support developers. The time-played revenue model may encourage design choices that prioritize player retention over quality. Additionally, concerns over yet another launcher and the issue of not owning games continue to affect sentiment. Indie Pass aims to help developers tackle discoverability challenges in a saturated market, but convincing both players and creators remains a significant hurdle.

ScummVM Expands Support for Obscure Old PC Games

ScummVM, a popular tool that allows playing older PC games on modern systems, has added support for eight more games, including two FMV shooters: *Crime Patrol* and *Crime Patrol 2: Drug Wars*. These mid-1990s full-motion video shooters are known for their cheesy production values and over-the-top acting. The update also includes support for *Necronomicon: The Dawning of Darkness*, a Lovecraftian point-and-click adventure game that one YouTuber rated very poorly. ScummVM’s ongoing expansion of supported titles helps preserve otherwise obscure or forgotten games and encourages players to explore or re-experience them despite their dated or flawed design.

Skyblivion Lead Remains Optimistic Amid Development Challenges

The development team behind *Skyblivion*, a massive mod that recreates *The Elder Scrolls IV: Oblivion* using the *Skyrim Special Edition* engine, recently issued a new call for experienced developers to assist with polishing the project. Originally planned for release in 2025 and delayed into 2026, the mod still has significant work to do, but project lead Rebelzize expressed optimism about the current progress. Key milestones, such as completing all quest locations and polishing the Imperial City, have been reached. Despite ongoing challenges and risks of setbacks, the team aims to deliver a functional, high-quality experience. Rebelzize also urged the community to maintain patience and appreciate the developers' efforts rather than speculate negatively.

Slay the Spire 2 Adds End-of-Run Badges in Latest Beta

The recent beta patch for *Slay the Spire 2* introduces "badges," a new feature highlighting notable achievements or events from each run. These badges appear at run completion and are designed to provide players with clear reminders of unique aspects like defeating bosses without damage or finding Easter eggs. The update also includes various balance changes, new artwork, improved visual effects, and interface refinements. While the previous beta balance patch was met with significant backlash, the response to this patch has been more mixed yet generally calmer. The badges aim to make runs feel more distinct and add depth to scorekeeping and player feedback.
