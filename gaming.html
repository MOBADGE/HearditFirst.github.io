Aether & Iron: A Lean Steampunk RPG with Tactical Combat

The newly released Aether & Iron is a steampunk-inspired RPG featuring turn-based tactical combat and visual novel storytelling. Players navigate an alternate 1930s New York, now a network of floating boroughs ruled by barons, as a cynical smuggler embroiled in a revolution plot. The game combines vehicle combat reminiscent of Twisted Metal with narrative elements including skill checks and full voice acting. Exploration is handled through 2D locations with limited interaction rather than traditional isometric movement, offering a streamlined pace.

Combat difficulty escalates later in the game, requiring strategic vehicle upgrades and resource management. While the story is largely linear, player choices and sidequests offer some variation and consequence. The game runs on Windows and is compatible with the Steam Deck. Aether & Iron illustrates a blend of RPG and tactical combat that prioritizes narrative efficiency and atmospheric setting over sprawling open worlds.

The Call of Duty Schism: Divided Studios, Divided Fans

Call of Duty's ongoing creative divide is rooted in contrasting visions between its two main studios: Infinity Ward and Treyarch. Infinity Ward is credited with pioneering the series’ flagship Modern Warfare sub-series, known for grounded military settings, superior audiovisuals, and technical polish. Treyarch’s Black Ops series embraces faster-paced, more arcade-style gameplay and features unique modes like Zombies co-op.

This schism fuels tribalism among fans, with debates over which approach better embodies the franchise’s spirit. Meanwhile, Activision’s aggressive annual release schedule increasingly strains its development teams. Treyarch, Raven, and Sledgehammer have picked up much of the workload, while Infinity Ward has released fewer titles but with longer development. This imbalance contributes to perceptions that Infinity Ward games are special events, while Treyarch games maintain ongoing community engagement.

This year marks Infinity Ward’s return to lead development after a Black Ops sequel that underperformed. The industry will be watching to see if Infinity Ward can revitalize the series or if Treyarch’s style remains dominant.

Nightdive’s Larry Kuperman Retires After 25 Years in Games Preservation

Larry Kuperman, Vice President of Business Development at Nightdive Studios, has retired after a career spanning 25 years in the games industry. Known for his work in game preservation and remastering classics like System Shock and Sin, Kuperman was instrumental in acquiring and managing complex intellectual property rights that obstructed re-releases.

At Nightdive, he emphasized treating games as art, focused on preserving gameplay alongside supplementary materials such as concept art and documentary footage. His retirement follows a challenging year marked by industry losses. Although stepping back from full-time work, Kuperman intends to remain involved with the International Game Developers Association, aiming to support new industry entrants.

French Consumer Group Sues Ubisoft Over The Crew Shutdown

France's UFC-Que Choisir, supported by the Stop Killing Games initiative, has filed a lawsuit against Ubisoft concerning the 2024 server shutdown of The Crew. The lawsuit alleges that Ubisoft violated consumer rights by failing to clearly inform players about the temporary nature of their game access and by revoking their licenses post-shutdown.

This legal action follows Ubisoft’s controversial handling of the shutdown, which included stripping player licenses, stirring a significant backlash and spawning the larger Stop Killing Games campaign advocating for players’ digital rights. The lawsuit seeks to prevent similar practices by clarifying publishers’ obligations, removing clauses allowing unilateral game closures, and acknowledging harm to consumers. Meanwhile, modders have revived The Crew, highlighting issues around digital ownership and game preservation.

World of Warcraft Players Complete Year-Long Puzzle with Coordinated 40-Player Dance

A dedicated group of World of Warcraft players recently solved the final steps of a year-old puzzle related to the Felcycle motorcycle mount. The penultimate phase required coordinating a synchronized dance involving 40 players—a raid size no longer standard in WoW—demonstrating significant player commitment to unlock unique cosmetic rewards.

The final step involves a challenging escort quest avoiding enemies. Completing the puzzle grants an outfit matching the Felcycle mount, reflecting the Legion expansion’s demonic themes. This event exemplifies the ongoing player investment in complex, community-driven challenges in WoW, often driven by the desire to collect rare appearances regardless of practical use.
