Former Bethesda Executive Calls for More Respect for Studio’s Worldbuilding

Pete Hines, Bethesda’s longtime marketing and PR executive who retired in 2023, recently defended the studio’s approach to creating large, complex open-world games. In an interview, Hines highlighted that Bethesda allows players significant freedom to pursue multiple quests simultaneously and explore vast worlds without restrictions. He compared this design to titles like Red Dead Redemption 2, where players must often choose one quest at a time.

Hines acknowledged that Bethesda’s games can be technically rough or buggy, but argued that the level of freedom and systemic interactivity in their game worlds is unmatched. He suggested the studio – led by Todd Howard – deserves more credit for embracing complexity where others avoid it. While Bethesda has softened this approach over time, notably after Morrowind, Hines’s comments underscore the distinct priorities in Bethesda’s design philosophy, focusing on player agency rather than tightly scripted narratives.

Overwatch Adjusts Map Voting to Favor Majority Choice in Upcoming Season

Ahead of Overwatch’s Season 2 launch on April 14, Blizzard announced changes to the map voting system to better reflect player preferences. Now, if a map earns the majority of votes in quick play or competitive modes, it will automatically be selected rather than using a weighted random system. This change is expected to make popular classic maps like King’s Row even more frequent, reducing the chances of playing less favored or newer maps.

Blizzard also introduced a “random” map option for those seeking variety, but player voting patterns suggest familiar maps will dominate. Additionally, losses on new or revamped maps will have less impact on competitive ranks, aiming to encourage players to try them without fear of rank penalties. These amendments address long-standing community feedback on map selection fairness and could influence player experience by further entrenching preferences for established maps.

Amazon Luna Ends Support for Third-Party Game Purchases and Streaming

Amazon’s Luna cloud gaming service will no longer support the purchase of games from third-party stores such as EA, Ubisoft, and GOG starting immediately. Subscriptions to third-party content like Ubisoft+ and Jackbox Games will also be discontinued. Users will retain streaming access to previously purchased third-party games only until June 10, after which these titles will be removed from the platform.

This move follows Amazon’s significant gaming division layoffs in late 2025 and signals a further scaling back of Luna’s ambitions. Amazon stated that the change responds to player demand for easier access to content from familiar developers and more social features. However, users reliant on Luna for streaming third-party games will need to transition to those games' original storefronts. Luna Premium subscribers can still stream many third-party titles available in that subscription tier, but overall, the changes limit Luna’s game library offerings and purchasing options.

PC Gamer-Favored Cozy Game ‘Starsand Island’ Temporarily Delisted Due to Copyright Issues

The cozy farming sim demoed by PC Gamer and praised as a standout game of 2026, Starsand Island, has been temporarily removed from Steam. Developer Seed Sparkle Lab revealed the delisting was caused by unauthorized use of core visual elements from a classic title within a mini-game segment. While the specific title was not officially named, fans suspect it to be Tetris based on gameplay footage.

The studio has submitted an updated version without the copyrighted content and plans to relist the game once approved. Owners will receive a free in-game outfit set, and no saved progress will be lost during this temporary removal. This incident follows earlier complications for Starsand Island, including a wave of suspicious positive user reviews. The developer said it is improving internal review processes to prevent similar issues in the future.

French Government Moves Away from Windows in Favor of Linux for Digital Sovereignty

In an effort to reduce dependency on foreign technology providers, France’s government announced plans to transition its computer workstations from Microsoft Windows to Linux. This initiative involves various government bodies and aims to achieve "digital sovereignty" by the end of 2026, especially for around 80,000 agents within the National Health Insurance Fund.

The shift to Linux – a free and open-source operating system – is intended to give France greater control over its digital infrastructure and avoid reliance on American tech companies. Officials stressed the importance of controlling data, pricing, and software evolution to protect strategic interests. This move reflects broader geopolitical concerns about digital independence and the role of foreign software in government systems.
