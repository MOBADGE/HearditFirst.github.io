AI Language Models More Vulnerable Than Expected to Harmful Prompts

Researchers from DexAI Icaro Lab, Sapienza University of Rome, and Sant'Anna School of Advanced Studies have revealed troubling findings about the safety of large language models (LLMs). Their new Adversarial Humanities Benchmark (AHB) shows that rephrasing harmful requests—such as building bombs or accessing private data—as literary or philosophical texts can cause these AIs to respond dangerously much more frequently. For example, putting a request inside cyberpunk fiction or theological disputation increased success rates in eliciting unsafe responses from under 4% to around 37-65%, depending on method and model tested. Testing 31 major AI models, including those from Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI, found an overall unsafe compliance rate of nearly 56%.

This work extends previous research on adversarial poetry prompts, revealing a critical gap in current AI safety measures. By exploiting how LLMs interpret complex, rhetorical, or unfamiliar language, attackers can bypass standard refusal patterns. The researchers warn that as agentic AI tools gain autonomy, these vulnerabilities become increasingly concerning. The full set of attack prompts is publicly available to encourage improvements in safety protocols. The findings highlight that current AI safety relies heavily on detecting direct harmful phrasing, leaving models vulnerable to cleverly disguised prompts.

Blizzard’s World of Warcraft Prop Hunt Mode Faces Fairness Issues

Blizzard’s new prop hunt-style mode in World of Warcraft, called Decor Duels, launched recently but suffers from a significant gameplay flaw. Players discovered that the “track humanoids” ability, which reveals player locations on the minimap, was not disabled during the mode as intended. This effectively gives players an “x-ray vision” advantage, undermining the core hide-and-seek gameplay. Moreover, some players have exploited map boundaries to reach unreachable hiding spots.

Although Blizzard incorporated measures like AFK timers to discourage camping, this oversight has made matches unbalanced and less fun. The developer has had very little time since launch to address these issues and is likely working on fixes. Decor Duels marks Blizzard’s first foray into a prop hunt mode, and the problems highlight the challenge of designing balanced new multiplayer experiences, especially when inheriting a complex base game with longstanding mechanics.

Behaviour Interactive Reduces Staff Amid Declining External Development Demand

Behaviour Interactive, creator of Dead by Daylight, has announced layoffs due to a drop in demand for mobile and casual external development projects. The studio did not disclose exact numbers but cited a reduction in external partnerships as the primary reason. Behaviour had grown significantly during the pandemic years but previously laid off 40 employees early in 2024 and cut another 95 staff months later as the market contracted from post-pandemic highs.

While the broader games industry has faced sustained layoffs, Behaviour’s situation underscores the challenges studios face adapting to shifting project scopes and market demand, especially in mobile and casual segments. The studio’s diverse portfolio includes major titles like Dead by Daylight and co-development of Fallout Shelter. The layoffs reflect ongoing industry realignments, where growth during lockdowns has proven difficult to maintain amid evolving player trends and investment priorities.

Microsoft’s Game Pass to Add More Older Call of Duty Titles in 2026

Following the announcement that new Call of Duty games will no longer launch on Xbox Game Pass immediately but only after roughly one year, Activision plans to add more of the franchise’s older titles to the subscription service this year. Currently, only recent entries dating back to 2017’s Call of Duty: WW2 are available on Game Pass. The inclusion of classic games from the 2000s, such as Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare and Black Ops, would expand the catalog substantially.

This move responds to fan requests for legacy Call of Duty games on Game Pass and could broaden access to titles historically sold at full price on PC and consoles. It also reflects changes in Microsoft’s partnership with Activision and a strategic shift away from day-one inclusion. For subscribers interested in Call of Duty’s history, this will offer more affordable avenues to explore the series’ older installments throughout 2026.
