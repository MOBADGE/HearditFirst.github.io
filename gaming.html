1. Reflecting on PC Gamer’s Text Message Letters from 15 Years Ago Fifteen years ago, readers of the UK edition of PC Gamer magazine could send text messages to the publication at a cost of 25 pence per SMS. The magazine featured a column showcasing these brief messages, ranging from fan commentary to micro reviews. This practice, now obsolete, highlights how player engagement and media interaction have evolved with technology. The quirky and sometimes nonsensical texts give insight into the gamer culture of the early 2010s and underline the contrast with today's free and instant online feedback methods.

*Why it matters:* This retrospective illustrates the changing relationship between gamers and gaming media, and how technological advances have reshaped community engagement and feedback channels.

2. Older Call of Duty Games to Join Xbox Game Pass in 2026 Following recent announcements from Xbox's new leadership, it was confirmed that new Call of Duty titles will no longer be added immediately to Game Pass upon release but will arrive approximately a year later. However, Activision plans to bring more legacy Call of Duty games to the service in 2026. Currently, the oldest available CoD on Game Pass is 2017’s WWII, but classics like Modern Warfare 2 and Black Ops are anticipated to join, potentially increasing the service's value for fans and gamers interested in exploring older entries without high individual costs.

*Why it matters:* This move reflects a strategic shift in how Microsoft manages its subscription offerings and may affect players’ access to popular franchises, while also expanding Game Pass’s library with established classics.

3. Google Exec Proposes AI as a Solution to Game Industry Challenges Jack Buser, Google Cloud's global director for games, addressed ongoing issues in the video game sector such as declining profits, cancellations, and layoffs. He proposes artificial intelligence as a tool to streamline development, reduce costs, and provide smaller studios with competitive advantages. Buser emphasizes that AI can accelerate workflows and marketing strategies, but also acknowledges the industry's structural problems that extend beyond technology. His position suggests AI adoption will be central to the industry's economic adaptation.

*Why it matters:* AI's potential integration into game development could alter workflows and business models significantly. However, while it may address profitability, concerns remain about its impact on employment and creative practices within the industry.

4. Hideo Kojima’s Visit to Valve Sparks Interest and Nostalgia Game creator Hideo Kojima recently visited Valve's headquarters, sharing photos of himself with iconic Half-Life and Portal props, including the notable gravity guns displayed in threes—a subtle nod perceived as playful trolling. Kojima has previously expressed admiration for Half-Life’s design and atmosphere. While no collaboration news emerged from the visit, it rekindles attention to Kojima’s respect for Valve’s work and the enduring influence of classic games on modern developers.

*Why it matters:* Such visits highlight ongoing relationships between influential creators and major studios, offering fans moments of reflection on gaming history and potential, if unconfirmed, future interactions.

5. Fallout Co-Creator Discusses Non-Linear Game Design and Player Behavior Tim Cain, co-creator of Fallout, shared insights on level design emphasizing non-linearity and caution against assuming players will follow intended paths or interactions. Using the example of a hostile NPC guard, Cain explains that players may bypass expected encounters, and designers should accommodate diverse player actions without enforcing rigid sequences. He advocates for open, flexible environments that respect player agency while aligning design goals.

*Why it matters:* Cain’s principles underscore the importance of player freedom in modern game design, reinforcing why non-linear gameplay remains valued and relevant in creating immersive and varied player experiences.
