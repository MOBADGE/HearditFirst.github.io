Overwatch Adds Five New Heroes Inspired by Competitor Marvel Rivals

During the 2026 Game Developers Conference, Overwatch 2 director Aaron Keller revealed that the success of NetEase’s Marvel Rivals influenced Blizzard’s decision to introduce a large batch of new characters in one update. Although Blizzard considered adding dozens of heroes at once, they settled on releasing five new fighters simultaneously in the latest Overwatch season: Domina, Anran, Emre, Mizuki, and the notable Jetpack Cat.

This move marks a significant shift in Blizzard’s approach to live service content. Marvel Rivals launched with a rapid pace of character additions, accumulating 47 heroes since late 2024—almost matching Overwatch 2’s roster with only half the time. Since rebranding to simply Overwatch, Blizzard has seen consistent player growth over consecutive weeks, something not observed since Overwatch 2's original launch in 2022. The developer also plans to release another set of five new heroes before the year’s end, aiming to maintain momentum.

Baby Steps Embraces Minimal Guidance and Player Curiosity

At GDC, Gabe Cuzzillo, co-creator of the climbing game Baby Steps, discussed his design philosophy centered on what he called "indifference to the player." Rather than heavily guiding players or providing polished challenges, Baby Steps intentionally includes climbing puzzles that have not been rigorously playtested, placing piles of cans in hard-to-reach spots to provoke exploration and experimentation.

Remarkably, players managed to climb every can stack except one, pushing developers to appreciate how open-ended challenges can inspire creative problem-solving. The approach contrasts with conventional single-player level design, which typically controls player experience tightly to prevent frustration. Baby Steps' design encourages players to find new solutions and embrace the game world’s ambiguity, highlighting an alternative to polished, guided gameplay.

Industry Divided Over Generative AI Amid Layoffs and Ethical Concerns

A panel featuring venture capital representatives at GDC sparked debate over the games industry's reception of generative AI technology. Moritz Baier-Lentz of Lightspeed Venture Partners expressed frustration that many developers “demonize” AI despite its potential to accelerate development processes. He suggests that pushback largely stems from fears of job loss following recent waves of industry layoffs.

Surveys show over half of game developers view generative AI negatively, citing issues such as unauthorized use of artists’ work, environmental impact, and concerns about quality and creativity erosion. Big tech representatives argue the technology is rapidly improving and still requires human oversight to be effective. The contrasting attitudes were symbolized at the conference expo, where AI startups promoting automated game creation shared space with union organizers advocating for workers’ rights.

Bungie Acknowledges Overcorrection in Marathon’s Audio Update

Bungie responded to player criticism over a recent audio change in their multiplayer shooter Marathon, which increased the distance at which gunfire and explosions could be heard. This unintentionally made stealth and cautious gameplay more difficult, leading to a consensus that combat felt more chaotic and demanding.

The studio admitted the adjustment was “an overcorrection” and pledged to revise it in an upcoming update to balance auditory awareness without overwhelming players. Bungie’s goal remains to provide informative audio cues without sacrificing the tactical feel that defines Marathon’s gameplay.

Arc Raiders’ Director Highlights Challenges of Live Service Game Development

Caio Braga, production director of the extraction shooter Arc Raiders, reflected on the difficulties of launching live service games in the current market. Speaking at GDC, Braga noted that Embark’s ability to pivot its game’s direction during development—avoiding cancellation despite multiple major changes—was a key factor in its success.

He contrasted this flexibility with other studios forced to release underdeveloped games or face shutdown. Braga called for more industry patience, allowing projects time to improve post-launch rather than expecting immediate hits. His comments underscore ongoing challenges in sustaining innovation and quality within high-pressure live service models.
