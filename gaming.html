Owlcat’s Romance Writing Challenge in Pathfinder RPGs

Owlcat Games, known for its CRPGs like Pathfinder: Wrath of the Righteous, recently shared some unusual challenges they faced with romanceable characters in their games. Early in development, the studio found that their three main female romance options were all cannibals, which posed storytelling difficulties. To balance this, they rewrote parts of one character, Arueshalae, a succubus, who was adjusted to be somewhat less “edgy” but still distinct. Another character, Wenduag, remained a cannibal, catering to players interested in unconventional romance arcs.

While Owlcat previously experimented with polyamorous relationships in Pathfinder: Kingmaker, they have since stepped back from that approach due to the complexity and development costs involved. The studio also recently revealed new companion characters for its Warhammer 40,000 RPGs, but it’s unclear if these will include romance options.

This insight highlights the ways narrative design in RPGs can confront unusual constraints, balancing player expectations with creative storytelling.

Australian Government Questions Valve on Extremist Content on Steam

Australia’s eSafety Commissioner has issued a transparency notice to Valve, demanding the company explain how it addresses the presence of extreme-right communities on Steam. Valve faces potential fines of up to AU$825,000 per day if it fails to respond. The notice cites concerns over grooming, radicalisation, and extremist-themed content hosted on Steam, alongside similar notices to Roblox, Microsoft (for Minecraft), and Epic Games (for Fortnite).

The commissioner’s statement referenced media reports about far-right imagery and violent event recreations on these platforms. This action comes amid Australia’s enforcement of Age-Restricted Material Codes requiring age verification for accessing adult content online—a regulation Valve has yet to implement on Steam.

This governmental scrutiny illustrates ongoing tensions between digital platforms and regulators regarding content moderation and protecting users, especially minors, from harmful or extremist material.

Framework Sticks to Upgradable Personal Computers Despite Industry Trends

Framework, a small laptop manufacturer focused on modular, upgradeable PCs, made a rare public statement emphasizing ownership and user freedom in a tech landscape increasingly dominated by cloud computing and AI-centric hardware. Founder Nirav Patel highlighted the company’s commitment to upgradability and compatibility, unveiling the Framework 13 Pro laptop with a chassis compatible with previous models, allowing users to reuse components like motherboards, batteries, and expansion cards.

Unlike many competitors moving toward sealed, non-upgradeable designs, Framework supports easily replacing parts such as RAM and screens, aligning with its vision of personal computing as user-controlled and sustainable. The company also noted its collaboration with Linux distributor Ubuntu, with Microsoft’s Windows playing only a minor role in their strategy.

Framework’s stance contrasts with broader tech industry trends toward cloud reliance and AI specialization, offering an alternative for those valuing modular hardware and user agency.

Masters of Albion Early Access Launch Time Announced

Peter Molyneux’s new god game, Masters of Albion, is scheduled for early access release on April 22 at 1 PM EDT / 6 PM BST. The launch time provides favorable hours for players in the US and Europe, while Australian players will access the game in the early morning hours of the following day. Preloading is not currently available, but the game’s 20 GB install size suggests a relatively quick download.

Masters of Albion aims to revisit classic god game mechanics such as town building and commanding divine powers in a new setting. The launch marks an opportunity to experience Molyneux’s signature style in a modern title, though the lack of preloading could impact launch day convenience.

Meta to Implement Keylogging on Employee PCs for AI Training

Meta has announced plans to monitor US-based employees’ computer use by tracking mouse movements, clicks, keystrokes, and taking screenshots on work-related applications. The data is intended to train AI models that automate routine computer tasks. Meta emphasizes that sensitive content will be excluded and that the data will not be used for employee evaluations.

This initiative follows Meta’s broader investment of $600 billion into AI by 2028 and coincides with upcoming layoffs of nearly 8,000 workers. Internal reports describe a culture of paranoia within the company, and the move to keylog employee activity may further affect morale.

Meta's approach raises questions about employee privacy and the extent of surveillance justified by AI development goals.
