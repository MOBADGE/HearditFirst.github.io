**Resident Evil Remake and Resident Evil 4 Receive Detailed HD Mods**

Fans of the early 2000s GameCube era Resident Evil titles have access to community-created HD remaster mods that enhance the visual quality of the Resident Evil Remake and the original Resident Evil 4 (2005). Although Capcom’s ports on Steam include minor enhancements, modders have improved textures, models, and lighting significantly. The Resident Evil Remake mod, known as REscale, sharpens character models and backgrounds while retaining the game’s original atmosphere. Meanwhile, the Resident Evil 4 HD Mod extensively replaces textures using original source materials and location photographs, adding dynamic lighting that the original hardware could not fully support. This mod makes the classic 2005 version visually comparable to expectations set by modern remakes, without altering gameplay. These mods matter because they preserve and renew classic games for current players, enabling fans to experience these titles with improved visuals while maintaining the integrity of the originals.

**Iron Galaxy Denies Working on Fallout: New Vegas Remaster**

Following speculation prompted by a studio social media post featuring a well-known Fallout: New Vegas loading screen, Iron Galaxy clarified that the image was part of a routine company meeting slideshow and not related to any ongoing Fallout projects. The studio’s statement counters rumors of their involvement in a New Vegas remaster, though the community remains skeptical given the specific choice of image and timing. Despite this, industry context suggests that a Fallout: New Vegas remaster is likely to occur eventually, considering the recent success of Oblivion Remastered and the increasing popularity of Fallout games due to the Amazon TV series. While Iron Galaxy is not currently developing such a remaster, players and fans should expect official news on the matter in the future.

**US Supreme Court Declines to Copyright AI-Generated Images**

The US Supreme Court decided not to review a case regarding copyright protection for AI-generated images. This decision upholds the US Copyright Office's position that images created solely by artificial intelligence cannot be copyrighted due to the lack of human authorship. The case involved Dr. Stephen Thaler’s AI-created image “A Recent Entrance to Paradise,” which was denied copyright registration. However, there remains some ambiguity as AI-assisted images with human modifications may still qualify for copyright. This ruling has implications for content creation in gaming and digital media, where AI tools are increasingly used. It highlights ongoing legal and ethical questions about ownership and protection of AI-generated works.

**Star Citizen Studio Reports Data Breach from January with Delayed Disclosure**

Cloud Imperium, developer of Star Citizen, disclosed a data breach that occurred on January 21, involving unauthorized, read-only access to some backup systems containing user metadata and basic account information such as names, dates of birth, and contact details. The studio affirmed no access to financial data, passwords, or the ability to modify data. The breach was publicly revealed only in early March via a site pop-up notification, without advance individual email alerts or social media announcements. The delay and manner of disclosure have drawn criticism from players concerned about potential phishing risks, as the exact contents of the accessed “metadata” were not clarified. Cloud Imperium advises affected users to update passwords and enable two-factor authentication. The incident underscores the importance of transparent and timely communication following data security breaches.

**Highguard Shuts Down Less Than Two Months After Launch**

Wildlight Entertainment announced that their PvP shooter Highguard will cease operations and go offline on March 12, roughly six weeks after launching. Despite attracting over two million players initially, the game's concurrent user numbers declined sharply, undermining long-term sustainability. The developers expressed gratitude to the community but cited their inability to maintain a viable player base. This closure comes after significant staff layoffs post-launch and follows mixed player reviews. Highguard’s final update will introduce new content, including a playable character and progression systems, but these additions come too late to reverse the player exodus. The game's shutdown illustrates challenges within the competitive hero shooter market and the difficulties new IPs face in retaining players.
