Linux Gaming in 2025: Progress and Persistent Challenges

2025 saw notable advances for Linux as a gaming platform, driven largely by Valve's SteamOS and the Proton compatibility layer that allows many Windows games to run on Linux. Valve‚Äôs SteamOS, originally designed for the Steam Deck handheld, expanded to support other devices like the Lenovo Legion Go S. This broadened accessibility sparked enthusiasm around Linux gaming, with performance and usability improvements attracting new users.

However, the experience is still inconsistent outside the Steam Deck and Legion devices. Many SteamOS installations on other handheld PCs remain buggy or difficult to maintain, making widespread adoption challenging. For users reliant on multiplayer games, kernel-level anti-cheat systems present a significant barrier. These anti-cheats operate at a deep system level incompatible with Linux, restricting the platform's support for popular multiplayer titles.

While Linux gaming continues to improve and offers appealing levels of user control, these technical limitations and the prevalence of Windows-specific anti-cheat measures mean many gamers remain hesitant to switch fully to Linux for their primary gaming environment.

System Shock 2: 25th Anniversary Edition Delivers a Classic Remaster

Nightdive Studios released System Shock 2: 25th Anniversary Edition in 2025, praised for its subtle, respectful approach to remastering the 1999 classic. Instead of overhauling the game with extensive modern graphics or major changes, the remaster polished textures, improved animations, and added widescreen and 4K support, keeping the original gameplay and atmosphere intact.

It stands as a model for how remasters can honor their source material without unnecessary alterations.
