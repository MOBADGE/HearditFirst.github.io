WoW Players Attempt Early Raid Boss Kill Blocked by Blizzard

Ahead of the official Tuesday launch of *World of Warcraft: Midnight's March of Quel'Danas* raid, clever players tried to defeat one of its bosses, L'ura, a week early by exploiting game mechanics and bugs. YouTuber Rextroy and others devised a strategy to survive the boss's high-damage area effect by exploiting resurrection sickness, which reduces damage taken, allowing them to stay alive longer. They then attempted to deal massive damage using a bugged monk attack combined with low-level rats as damage conduits.

Although their attacks appeared to reduce the boss's health below zero, Blizzard had programmed L'ura to be unkillable until the raid's official release, nullifying their effort. This safeguard maintained the intended progression timing and prevented premature world-first kills. While the players had fun testing their methods, Blizzard’s measure preserved game balance and excitement for the wider player base.

Reflection on "Eurojank": European Games’ Rough Edges and Ambition

An in-depth piece explores the term "eurojank," once used to criticize European games for bugs and rough presentation but also seen as a badge of ambitious, system-driven design. The article traces the history through series like *Gothic*, *The Witcher*, and *Boiling Point: Road to Hell*, highlighting the willingness of these games to accept flaws for the sake of creative freedom and complexity.

However, economic pressures and studio closures have eroded some of eurojank’s support pillars. Publishers like Nacon and developers like Piranha Bytes have faced insolvency or shutdowns. Simultaneously, better tools and industry knowledge have made such games less "janky," improving polish but perhaps reducing some of the chaotic charm. The piece concludes with a nuanced view of eurojank as a creative spirit that has both evolved and diminished in the modern gaming landscape.

Inzoi Studio Head on Early Access Challenges and Player Testing

Hyungjun "Kjun" Kim, head of Inzoi Studio, reflected on the challenges of developing *Inzoi*, a life simulation game inspired by *The Sims*. Despite 29 years in the industry, Kim acknowledged the difficulty of matching the depth and success of *The Sims* series, particularly given the technical challenges of an open-world life sim.

Kim expressed mixed feelings about releasing Inzoi in early access, noting the unavoidable reliance on players to test and provide feedback but feeling sympathy for them shouldering part of the development burden. The game sold over a million copies in its first week but faced criticism for lacking depth and awkward social interactions, common early access issues. The studio continues to work on content updates, mod support, and user-generated content features to improve the experience.

Peak’s New Update Adds Extensive Customization Options for Players

The climbing adventure game *Peak* received an update that allows players to tailor their experience with a wide range of custom settings. Players can toggle gameplay elements such as fall damage, hunger, environmental hazards, and even remove or restrict certain items like blowguns. A mode dubbed “Grapple Mode (Stupid)” grants unlimited use of rescue claws, making traversal much easier.

These custom expeditions are separate from the main mode and don’t reward badges, focusing on player fun and experimentation. Additionally, a "mini run" mode lets players tackle just one biome instead of the full six, catering to those seeking shorter sessions. The update aims to accommodate diverse playstyles and increase accessibility without altering the core game.

FTC Raises Concerns Over Payment Companies’ Role in Content Restrictions

The US Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has sent warning letters to Mastercard, Visa, PayPal, and Stripe regarding concerns that these payment companies may be denying services based on customers’ political or religious views. This follows Valve’s revelation last year that Mastercard-related payment processors pressured Steam to remove several adult-oriented games.

While Mastercard denied direct involvement, the policy cited was one aimed at preventing "illegal or brand-damaging transactions," which payment processors used to justify content removal. The FTC’s letter underscores the importance of fair access to financial services as essential for commerce and freedom but also notes that regulatory language remains vague, potentially allowing continued restrictive practices under the guise of business decisions. The impact on adult game creators and other marginalized sectors remains an area to watch.
