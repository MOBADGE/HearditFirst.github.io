**Wizards of the Coast Uploads 1980s Dungeons & Dragons Cartoon**

Wizards of the Coast has started releasing episodes of the original 1980s Dungeons & Dragons animated series on YouTube, sharing two or three episodes every Friday. The show’s 27-episode run is currently 11 episodes in. The cartoon, co-produced by Marvel Productions and animated by Toei, reflects the animation style of that era, featuring voice work by notable actors like Peter Cullen and Frank Welker. Fans of classic D&D may enjoy spotting creatures from the game’s Monster Manual, though the show’s dated style is a reminder of 1980s children’s television norms. This archival release offers both nostalgia and context for the franchise’s long history.

**Indie Developer Embraces FMV for Narrative Style in New Game**

Sunset Visitor, known for the game 1000xResist, revealed that a sudden inclusion of video footage in their last game was born “out of desperation,” using existing documentary footage to fill a narrative gap. Encouraged by this experiment, the studio plans to feature full-motion video (FMV) more prominently in its upcoming title, Prove You’re Human, which revolves around a digital copy interacting with an AI. While FMV has had a mixed reputation historically, recent indie games employing FMV show the format’s potential for storytelling. Sunset Visitor's move reflects a broader indie trend of revisiting FMV to create unique narrative experiences.

**YouTuber Offers $10,000 Reward for Improved Netcode in Ultimate Marvel vs. Capcom 3**

Maximillian Christiansen, a prominent fighting game content creator, has announced a $10,000 bounty for anyone who can develop rollback netcode for Ultimate Marvel vs. Capcom 3. The game currently uses delay-based netcode, which can hamper online play by introducing input lag. Rollback netcode, which predicts player inputs to reduce latency, has become standard in recent fighting games and is well-regarded for improving online responsiveness. Christiansen’s offer follows his previous support of modding efforts that enhance fighting game experiences. An improved netcode could extend the longevity and popularity of this classic fighter in online competitive communities.

**Hasbro’s Star Wars Action Figure Featuring Kreia and Darth Nihilus Sells Out Quickly**

Hasbro released a two-pack action figure set in the Star Wars Black Series featuring Darth Nihilus and Kreia (Darth Traya) from Knights of the Old Republic 2: The Sith Lords. The Kreia figure is notable for being the first official one of the character, known for her complex role as companion and antagonist in the game. The figures include detailed features such as floating lightsabers for Kreia. Despite initial enthusiasm, the Amazon-exclusive preorders sold out rapidly. This indicates continuing fan interest in the Knights of the Old Republic franchise’s characters and memorabilia.

**Rockstar Games Experiences Security Breach as Hackers Demand Ransom**

Rockstar Games has confirmed a security incident involving unauthorized access to some non-material company data following a breach of its outsourced Snowflake cloud storage system. The hacker group ShinyHunters claims to have gained access via credentials from a recent breach of third-party service Anodot, threatening to leak the data if a ransom is not paid by April 14, 2026. Rockstar stated the breach does not affect its players or core operations. This event echoes a similar large-scale GTA 6 leak in 2022 and highlights ongoing risks related to third-party cloud services in game development environments.

**BioWare’s Future and Challenges Explored in Recent Analysis**

A recent in-depth column reflects on BioWare’s current state, acknowledging past successes with franchises like Dragon Age and Mass Effect but also the struggles the studio has faced in recent years. Despite some positive reception to Dragon Age: The Veilguard, sales fell short of expectations, and significant post-launch support was limited. The article argues that BioWare’s reliance on legacy franchises, heavy oversight from parent company Electronic Arts, and missteps like Anthem have constrained creative freedom and innovation. It suggests that to recover, BioWare needs to redefine its identity, experiment with new ideas, and move away from repeated reliance on old brands. However, publisher pressures and financial expectations make such a transformation unlikely in the near future.
