Cherry XTRFY K5 Pro TMR Compact Review: A Niche Gaming Keyboard

The Cherry XTRFY K5 Pro TMR Compact is a 65% wired gaming keyboard featuring Cherry's MK Crystal magnetic switches. Priced at $185/£140, it offers precision actuation down to 0.01 mm and a polling rate up to 8,000 Hz. Its compact design excludes a numpad and dedicated macro or media keys but allows for comfortable key spacing, which suits users with smaller hands.

The keyboard's plastic build and orange stripe aesthetics are modest compared to more premium alternatives, and some users may find the clacky space bar and overall noise less appealing. Still, the included Magcrate software enables extensive customization, from RGB lighting profiles to adjustable key actuation and special functions like SnapKey—a dual-key input feature, though it carries competitive play risks due to bans in some esports titles.

While this keyboard is more affordable than Cherry's wireless MX 8.2 Pro TMR TKL, which costs $250/€230, it still sits at a premium price that may not appeal to budget-conscious gamers. Alternatives like the Keychron K2 HE or the Gamakay x NaughShark NS68 provide magnetic or Hall effect switches at lower prices but lack some of the K5 Pro's technical features. The K5 Pro TMR Compact best fits gamers seeking high responsiveness and compactness, particularly those with small hands.

Project Zomboid Bans Workshop Mods Carrying Malicious Code

Survival game Project Zomboid’s developer, The Indie Stone, removed 14 Steam Workshop mods after discovering they contained heavily obfuscated code that created malicious files outside the game directory. The affected mods, mostly soundtracks from well-known games, had been installed on 500 to 2,200 devices, specifically on the game's Build 42 (unstable) branch.

The user responsible was banned, and all affected mods were removed from Steam. The developer advised players who installed these mods to take security measures beyond simply uninstalling them, as the true nature of the malicious files remains unclear. Players should check their installed mods against the identified list to ensure safety.

This incident highlights ongoing risks with community-made mods and the necessity of caution when using unofficial content, especially on game development branches.

World of Warcraft Player Recreates Counter-Strike’s Dust 2 with Housing Tools

A World of Warcraft player used the game’s newly introduced player housing features to painstakingly recreate Dust 2, the iconic map from Counter-Strike. The player incorporated creative solutions such as constructing a car from wooden shelves, doormats, and fruit plates, overcoming constraints like limited room height and non-flat carpets.

The build has gained attention in the WoW community and even from the official World of Warcraft Twitter account. This demonstrates the flexibility and creative potential unlocked by WoW’s player housing system, allowing players to pay homage to other games within the MMO’s fantasy setting.

Hazelight Studios Hits 50 Million Sales with Co-op Hits

Hazelight Studios announced that its three cooperative action games—A Way Out, It Takes Two, and Split Fiction—have collectively sold over 50 million copies in under 10 years. The latest title, Split Fiction, sold 7 million copies in its first year, while It Takes Two remains the best seller with 30 million copies.

Hazelight is currently developing a new game, although details remain under wraps. The studio’s success underscores the ongoing popularity and market viability of well-crafted co-op experiences, despite some of their earlier games receiving mixed reviews.

Songs of Glimmerwick Offers a New Take on Magical School Life Sims

Songs of Glimmerwick is a witch school life simulation game that recently released a playable demo with plans to launch fully later this year. Players take on the role of a new student tasked with garden care, potion-making, and learning magical spells via playing a flute. The art style mixes storybook charm with soft 2D visuals reminiscent of games like Mirthwood.
