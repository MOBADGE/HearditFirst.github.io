Slay the Spire 2 Introduces Cooperative Multiplayer Mode

Slay the Spire 2, known for its singleplayer deckbuilding roguelite gameplay, has added a multiplayer co-op mode. One player hosts a campaign and up to three friends can join via Steam friends lists; there is no matchmaking or local co-op support as it remains in early access. Each player chooses their own character and maintains independent inventories of gold, relics, items, and cards.

Gameplay-wise, enemy difficulty adjusts for party size, and enemy attacks affect the entire team. Players have individual energy pools and must all end their turn before the battle continues. If a player dies, they revive after battle if the party wins. Loot and rewards are personal, not shared. There are also special multiplayer cards unique to co-op mode.

Party members collaborate on map choices but may disagree, with random selection breaking ties. Temples, events, and shopkeepers offer individual options and rewards. Ascension levels in multiplayer are separate from singleplayer, requiring all party members to unlock them for higher difficulty. This addition aims to preserve Slay the Spire’s core while expanding replayability through cooperative play.

Slay the Spire 2 Sets Records as Best-Performing Deckbuilder

Slay the Spire 2 has had an unprecedented launch in early access, quickly becoming the best-performing deckbuilding game ever. Data from Newzoo and Steam show the game peaking at 575,000 concurrent users shortly after launch—a figure far surpassing notable deckbuilders like Balatro and Monster Train 2. In its first week, it sold over 3 million copies, grossing roughly $75 million before platform fees.

The game averaged over a million daily active users in its first 11 days without signs of declining interest. Newzoo attributes this success to seven years of anticipation, a lack of sequels or spin-offs diluting its audience, and developer Mega Crit’s consumer trust built on transparency and an absence of aggressive monetization.

The sequel’s expanded appeal has attracted new player types, including challenge seekers not typically drawn to roguelike deckbuilders. This strong launch reflects a combination of eager existing fans and new players drawn by the game’s difficulty and reputation.

Kojima Aimed for Higher Completion Rates with Death Stranding 2

Hideo Kojima and his team deliberately designed Death Stranding 2 to encourage more players to reach the game’s conclusion. Feedback from the original game cited pacing as slow, so the sequel features streamlined worldbuilding and faster access to vehicles and tools, facilitating easier travel and quicker progression.

Lead level designer Hiroaki Yoshiike said the team balanced catering to completionists with making the story accessible through supplemental elements. Early data indicates the approach succeeded: over 62% of players using PSNProfiles have finished the final chapter, a considerable increase from less than half for the original Death Stranding.

While some players feel the sequel is easier, the game includes a harder "To the Wilder" difficulty setting for those seeking challenge. Kojima Productions’ focus was on making the experience more playable and enjoyable without diluting the narrative impact.

OpenAI Discontinues Sora Video Generation App; Disney Withdraws Investment

OpenAI announced the shutdown of Sora, its AI-driven video generation app, less than six months after the launch of Sora 2. The company will also end developer access to its video models and remove video functions from ChatGPT, shifting focus to productivity tools combining ChatGPT, coding, and browsing.

Sora gained popularity but encountered copyright issues due to unlicensed use of copyrighted characters, prompting legal pressure from Japanese studios. Disney, which had planned a $1 billion investment in OpenAI tied to Sora’s use of its IP, has now pulled out.

This marks a shift away from consumer-facing AI video products at OpenAI amid ongoing concerns about intellectual property and content control in AI-generated media.

Dark Outlaw Games, New Studio from Call of Duty Veteran, Closes Under Sony

Sony has closed Dark Outlaw Games, a first-party studio founded by Call of Duty veteran Jason Blundell just over a year ago. Around 50 employees were laid off as part of Sony’s broader strategic adjustments aimed at sustaining long-term business health.

The studio was developing an unannounced project but was still in early stages at closure. This follows Sony’s recent shutdown of Bluepoint Games and reflects the ongoing volatility in the industry
