Blockchain Gaming Absence at GDC 2026

For the first time in years, the Game Developers Conference (GDC) held in San Francisco features no talks focused on blockchain gaming or NFTs. Previously common at the event, blockchain topics that ranged from building games to optimizing them for blockchain environments have disappeared from this year's schedule. The convention center also hosts only a single banner advertising a blockchain company, signaling a significant retreat of the technology’s presence. Meanwhile, generative AI has a prominent role at GDC 2026, with major companies like Nvidia and Google hosting sessions on AI applications in games. This shift suggests a change in industry focus, though many developers express skepticism about generative AI’s impact on game development.

Valve Faces Second Class-Action Lawsuit Over Loot Boxes

Valve is confronting a new consumer class-action lawsuit in Washington state, accusing the company of enabling illegal gambling through loot box systems in games such as Counter-Strike 2, Team Fortress 2, and Dota 2. The lawsuit alleges these loot boxes use psychological tactics similar to casino gambling to encourage spending, often targeting children without adequate age verification. It claims that the random chance of valuable items and the design resembling slot machines meet the legal definition of gambling under Washington law. This lawsuit follows a recent New York state suit, highlighting ongoing legal scrutiny of loot box mechanics in popular Valve games. Valve has made some compliance adjustments in specific regions, but broader legal outcomes remain uncertain.

Nagoshi Studio Loses NetEase Funding for Debut Game

Nagoshi Studio, founded by famed Yakuza series creator Toshihiro Nagoshi, will lose funding from Chinese publisher NetEase in May, just three months after revealing its first project, *Gang of Dragon*. Reportedly, NetEase decided to cut support after determining that the game required an additional $44.4 million to reach completion, a financial commitment the publisher was unwilling to make. While Nagoshi Studio is seeking new investors to fill this gap, NetEase’s withdrawal may complicate the game’s future. This funding cut is part of a broader trend where NetEase has recently ended support for multiple international studios, though Nagoshi Studio is based in Japan.

Blizzard to Increase Diablo 4’s Difficulty Levels in Lord of Hatred Expansion

Blizzard plans to address concerns about Diablo 4's difficulty and progression balance with its upcoming *Lord of Hatred* expansion. Currently, the game features four torment difficulty tiers, but the new expansion will introduce up to 12 tiers. This change aims to make incremental increases in gear and power feel more meaningful by offering higher challenges that require skill and strategic play rather than simply inflating damage numbers. The highest difficulty tier will be extremely challenging, designed for players seeking a near-impossible test of their builds. Alongside this, a loot filter and new crafting system will be introduced to handle the increased item drops and customization options.

Marathon Features Multiple Homages to Bungie's Halo Weaponry

Bungie's new extraction shooter *Marathon* includes several weapons that closely resemble those found in the Halo series, reflecting the developer's ongoing creative connection to its legacy. Players can find guns like the "CE Tactical Sidearm," visually and functionally similar to Halo’s iconic pistol, and the "BR33 Volley Rifle," which mimics the Halo 2 Battle Rifle with a special attachment that enhances accuracy. Other weapons evoke elements from Halo and Bungie's *Destiny* series, mixing familiar designs with new mechanics. This blend of inspirations underlines Bungie's house style, merging past signature features with fresh gameplay in *Marathon*.

Steam Usage Grows with Massive Data Downloads in 2025

Valve reported that Steam users downloaded approximately 100 exabytes of data in 2025, an increase from 80 exabytes in 2024. This volume equates to an average of 274 petabytes of installs and updates daily, highlighting continued growth in platform usage. Concurrent users peaked at 42 million, up from 25 million five years ago, demonstrating sustained user expansion. With this growth, Valve also noted increased revenue payouts to developers, maintaining its revenue share model that benefits game creators. These statistics offer insight into the scale and reach of Steam as a digital distribution platform.
