Slay the Spire 2: More Remake Than Sequel

The Early Access release of Slay the Spire 2 has led to mixed feelings among players familiar with the original. The game brings back three of the original characters with starting decks and relics largely unchanged, and the progression, card pools, and map design feel very similar. One player reported beating the game multiple times using decks very similar to those used in the first game from eight years ago. While the core gameplay remains engaging, the sequel appears to make only modest changes beyond new cards, bosses, and a co-op multiplayer mode.

The multiplayer addition is notable as a rare feature for a primarily singleplayer roguelike, but it currently feels limited. The user interface does not facilitate easy collaboration, and the difficulty simply scales enemy health pools rather than introducing fresh challenges. Compared to other recent roguelikes that innovate on storytelling and mechanics, Slay the Spire 2 may feel safe and familiar. Given this is an Early Access version, the developers might plan to build on this solid foundation over time.

Sega Sammy Reports Sales Below Expectations Despite Critical Acclaim

Sega Sammy has published its financial results for 2025 and pointed out a disconnect between positive reception and actual sales. Although a range of their recent titles including Virtua Fighter 5: R.E.V.O., Like a Dragon: Pirate Yakuza in Hawaii, and Metaphor: ReFantazio received high critical appraisal, Sega notes "stagnant" sales figures overall. Sonic Racing: CrossWorlds sold about one million units but fell short of forecasts. Football Manager 26 also faced criticism at launch, prompting Sega to allocate resources toward improvement.

The company’s president highlighted the need to improve marketing and sales strategies to better translate acclaim into unit sales. Sega plans to restructure its global publishing operations to address this. Meanwhile, revenue from amusement machines and character licensing, notably Sonic merchandise, remains strong. Rovio, acquired by Sega in 2023, continues to struggle financially with a large asset write-down reported.

US Court Rules Continued Use Implies Consent to Updated Terms of Service

A recent US court ruling has established that continued use of a service after notification of updated terms of service (ToS) can imply consent to those terms, even if the user did not explicitly review them. This comes from an appeal involving Life360 and its subsidiary Tile, which faced a class-action lawsuit over inadequate anti-stalking protections in their Bluetooth trackers. Notice of changes to arbitration and other terms was sent via email, with continued use after a specified date considered acceptance.

This ruling underscores the challenges of user consent in digital services and has implications for how companies update policies. Even users unaware of changes might be legally bound by them if they continue using the product. This case highlights ongoing concerns around surveillance, privacy, and the opaque nature of consent mechanisms.

LG Offers 27-Inch 1440p OLED Gaming Monitor with Bonus IPS Model

LG has launched a new bundle for PC gamers featuring its latest 27-inch 1440p OLED monitor with an exceptionally high refresh rate of 540 Hz, priced at £899.99 in the UK. This OLED panel offers fast pixel response times and a brightness increase over previous models. The deal includes a free 27-inch 1440p IPS monitor with a 200 Hz refresh rate, which individually would sell for about £199.

This pairing appeals to gamers who prioritize high refresh rates and sharp visuals in a popular size and resolution. While not a deep innovation, the bundle represents a significant value proposition, providing two quality monitors at a competitive price point.

Anthropic Releases Claude Code Review AI Tool

Anthropic has introduced a new AI-powered code review tool called Claude Code Review, currently available in research preview. The tool runs on-site without requiring additional plugins and uses multiple AI agents to identify and verify bugs in codebases. It ranks findings by severity to help developers prioritize fixes. This system reflects Anthropic’s internal review process and aims to reduce bottlenecks in software development.

Some developers note that while convenient, the new tool offers functionality comparable to existing AI review setups but potentially with more compute power leading to better bug detection. The service is billed according to token usage, with costs varying depending on the complexity of code being reviewed. The impact of this tool on development workflows and software quality remains to be seen.
