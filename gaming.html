**Crimson Desert’s Controls Draw Mixed Reactions from Players and Developer**

Crimson Desert’s control scheme has been a frequent point of frustration among players and critics, who describe it as convoluted and awkward, especially on PC. Some reviews advise using a controller over keyboard and mouse, though issues like accidentally hitting the horse persist. Reddit discussions echo these sentiments rather bluntly, criticizing the controls harshly. In response, Will Powers, PR and marketing director at Pearl Abyss, acknowledged the learning curve but compared mastering the controls to riding a bike—difficult at first but natural after practice. Co-creative director of Arkane also commented that learning new control setups can be rewarding despite initial difficulty. While Crimson Desert has sold over two million copies quickly, its mixed user reviews partly reflect these control issues. Pearl Abyss has promised to listen to community feedback and work on improvements, likely including the control scheme.

**Kirsten Dunst Cast as Alex in Minecraft Movie Sequel**

The upcoming Minecraft Movie sequel, scheduled for release in July 2027, has added Kirsten Dunst to its cast in the role of Alex, the female player avatar with red hair. Dunst, known for a range of films from Spider-Man to indie classics, expressed interest in the project partly because of its commercial prospects and her children’s fondness for Minecraft. The sequel will also bring back notable cast members including Jack Black and Jennifer Coolidge. Jared Hess returns as director with Chris Galletta writing the script. The first Minecraft movie grossed over a billion dollars, making the sequel a financially sound bet despite receiving mixed reviews. Attention remains on how well the film captures the feel of the game’s world.

**OpenTTD Clarifies Relationship with Atari After Removal from Steam**

OpenTTD, the free, open-source remake of Transport Tycoon Deluxe, was recently removed from Steam and GOG following Atari’s commercial re-release of the original Transport Tycoon Deluxe. The OpenTTD development team emphasized that the project remains fully independent and continues to be available for free from its official website. Atari approached the developers beforehand to coordinate the transition, aiming to balance their commercial interests while supporting the longstanding fan community. OpenTTD is still playable for free but no longer distributed on major storefronts due to this arrangement. Atari has also committed to contributing to OpenTTD’s server upkeep costs. While some fans have expressed frustration, the developers ask for respectful discussion, noting Atari’s collaborative stance contrasts with typical publisher behavior in such cases.

**Subnautica 2 CEO Expresses Concern Over May Early Access Announcement**

Ted Gill, recently reinstated as CEO of Unknown Worlds, the studio behind Subnautica 2, has raised objections to establishment publisher Krafton’s announcement of a May early access release date, stating it was made without his knowledge or consent. This follows a lengthy legal dispute between Gill, Krafton, and former Unknown Worlds leadership concerning studio control and bonus earnouts tied to the game’s performance. Gill’s legal team argues that Krafton’s premature announcement has disrupted marketing and community coordination efforts and complicated preparations for launch. Krafton claims the date was decided prior to the court order reinstating Gill and that he is free to revise it. The public conflict underscores ongoing tensions as the studio attempts to navigate development and scheduling amidst legal challenges.

**Slay the Spire 2 Faces Review Backlash Over Upcoming Card Changes**

The Slay the Spire 2 developer Mega Crit released a beta patch that made several balance changes, including a notable nerf to the Silent character’s card "Prepared." This sparked significant player outrage, with over 9,000 negative Steam reviews posted in less than a day—despite the patch not yet being live on the main branch. An analysis suggests that many of these negative reviews come from players using Simplified Chinese, where alternative community feedback channels like forums and Discord are restricted, making reviews one of few visible means to express dissatisfaction. The controversy points to challenges in managing player communication and reception across different regions, especially where platform features are limited.
