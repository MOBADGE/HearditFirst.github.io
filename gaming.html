Blockchain Gaming Absent from GDC 2026

For the first time in several years, the Game Developers Conference (GDC) lacks blockchain gaming talks or significant blockchain-related presence. Previous conferences featured multiple sessions and sizable booths dedicated to blockchain and NFT gaming. At GDC 2026, only a single talk mentions "digital wallets and alternative payment methods," and just one blockchain-related banner was visible on the expo floor. This marks a notable retreat from what was once considered an “emerging blockchain future” for games.

Generative AI, however, has taken a prominent role at GDC 2026 with various presentations and companies focusing on AI-powered game design and assistants. Despite this, a recent GDC survey found that over half of game developers view generative AI negatively. The absence of blockchain discussions suggests waning enthusiasm or acceptance among the developer community, though companies like Sony and CCP continue to explore blockchain gaming separately.

Valve Faces Second Class-Action Lawsuit Over Loot Boxes

Less than two weeks after New York State sued Valve over alleged illegal gambling practices with loot boxes, a consumer class-action lawsuit has been filed making similar claims. The suits target loot box systems in popular Valve games such as Counter-Strike 2, Team Fortress 2, and Dota 2, alleging these systems are designed to extract money through psychological tactics akin to gambling.

The lawsuits highlight that loot boxes require users to pay real money for keys to unlock random virtual items, some of which can be sold for high real-world value. The complaint argues this meets the legal definition of gambling under Washington state law. Valve has previously faced regulations over loot boxes overseas and recently introduced features like an "X-ray scanner" to preview loot box contents in some regions. The outcomes of these lawsuits could have implications for loot box mechanics industry-wide.

Yakuza Creator’s Studio Loses NetEase Funding

Nagoshi Studio, founded by Yakuza creator Toshihiro Nagoshi, will lose its funding from Chinese publisher NetEase in May, just three months after unveiling its debut game, Gang of Dragon, at the 2025 Game Awards. The studio's project, a third-person action game set within a Korean crime syndicate, has yet to reveal substantial gameplay details beyond a brief cinematic trailer.

Bloomberg reports that NetEase withdrew funding after concluding the project requires an additional $44.4 million to complete. Nagoshi Studio is seeking new investors but has not secured any yet. While NetEase has cut funding to multiple Western studios recently, this is among the rare cases involving a Japan-based developer. The situation puts the project’s future and ownership of its assets in question.

Bungie’s Marathon Includes Clear Halo Weapon References

Bungie’s new extraction shooter Marathon pays visual and mechanical homage to the studio’s earlier Halo series. Several guns in Marathon closely resemble iconic Halo weapons, such as the CE Tactical Sidearm mirroring the Halo pistol, the Magnum MC echoing the Halo Magnum, and the Plasma Pistol-inspired V11 Punch.

Some weapons even have attachments that further strengthen their resemblance to Halo guns, like the BR33 Volley Rifle’s Lockout Muzzle Brake attachment recalling the Halo 2 Battle Rifle on the Lockout map. While many references are subtle, the marked inclusion of these designs reflects Bungie's legacy and design continuity between its franchises.

Diablo 4 Expansion Adds Near-Impossible Difficulty Tiers

Blizzard plans to address concerns about inflated damage numbers and lack of challenge in Diablo 4 through its upcoming Lord of Hatred expansion. The game’s current four torment difficulty tiers will expand to twelve, allowing players to increase difficulty to near-impossible levels where monsters become more threatening and loot improves correspondingly.

Developers say this system aims to make power gains feel meaningful by matching higher character strength with increased challenge. Unlike current tiers, the new torment levels are intended as gradual progression steps rather than broad difficulty leaps. The hardest tier will be optional and designed as a test for dedicated players, potentially extending the game’s replayability.

Steam Users Downloaded 100 Exabytes of Data in 2025

Valve released its Steam Year in Review for 2025, highlighting significant growth in platform usage and data volumes. Steam users downloaded a total of 100 exabytes of data over the year, up from 80 exabytes in 2024. On average, 274 petabytes of installs and updates happen daily on Steam, illustrating the scale of content distribution
