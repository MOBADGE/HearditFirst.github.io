Resident Evil Requiem: Hidden Details and Strong Sales

Recent discoveries from the new Resident Evil Requiem shine light on some interesting in-game details. Players exploring out-of-bounds areas during a flashback cutscene found that the young character Grace Ashcroft is shown browsing a subreddit called r/amateurjournalists. The posts she reads discuss events connected to Resident Evil 7 and the larger series lore, adding subtle narrative depth to the game’s world-building. This Easter egg also inspired fans to create a real subreddit mirroring the fictional one. Meanwhile, in terms of commercial success, Resident Evil Requiem has sold over five million copies in just four days. This achievement ranked it among Capcom’s top 20 best-selling games of all time, highlighting the franchise’s ongoing popularity and strong market performance.

Copyright Dispute Clears the Way for Japanese Cult Classic Cookie's Bustle

The Video Game History Foundation (VGHF) has succeeded in preserving and publicly sharing Cookie's Bustle, an obscure 1999 Japanese point-and-click adventure, after years of copyright trolling stifled its exposure. The game had been effectively erased from online spaces due to repeated DMCA takedowns by a company called Graceware, which claimed ownership without clear legal standing. VGHF obtained a physical copy of the game and added it to their archive, pushing back against unjust takedown requests. This victory allowed longplay videos and fan content to re-emerge, preventing the loss of this rare title. The case underscores the challenges of preserving video game history when faced with questionable copyright claims.

Highguard Releases Final Update Ahead of Shutdown

Highguard, the multiplayer shooter from Wildlight Entertainment, is scheduled to shut down on March 12, but received a final substantial update shortly before closure. This last patch added a new playable character named Koldo, a healer class with protective abilities, alongside new weapons like the Switchback gun that switches between shotgun and marksman modes. Additionally, skill trees and expanded account progression were introduced, allowing players to specialize their Wardens in various roles. The development team completed content that had been in progress, delivering a more robust experience as a farewell to the remaining player base. Although the game struggled to maintain a large audience, this update provides final enhancements for its community.

Hunt: Showdown to Hide Extraction Points in Upcoming Event

Crytek’s Hunt: Showdown is set to experiment with its core extraction formula in the March 18 “Devil’s Trail” event by hiding supply and extraction points at the start of matches. Instead of spawning visible exits and loot locations on the map, players must discover them by exploring or finding scouting maps. Extraction points will only become visible after certain actions, such as collecting bounty tokens or using specific tarot cards. This change aims to reduce predictability and increase tension during extraction phases, which have traditionally been straightforward escapes to a known location. The update also introduces new scout towers, additional tarot cards, and enhanced environmental storytelling through persistent world changes left behind by players.

US Government Considers Forcing Tencent to Divest US Game Holdings

The US government is reportedly debating whether Tencent should be compelled to divest its stakes in American and Finnish video game companies over national security concerns. Tencent owns or has major investments in many major studios including Riot Games, Epic Games, and FromSoftware. Officials worry about potential access to personal data of US users through these holdings. This ongoing discussion, delayed by scheduling conflicts, reflects a broader scrutiny of Chinese ownership in key tech sectors. Forcing Tencent out could lead to private equity stepping in to fill ownership gaps, an outcome that carries its own risks. The situation is unfolding within a complex geopolitical and corporate context, with implications for the structure of the gaming industry.

Few Pass Rigid '80s Adventure Game Exam Without Walkthrough

An “Adventure Game Aptitude Test” conducted by developer Woe Industries challenged over 800 gamers to complete the 1987 graphic adventure Maniac Mansion without using walkthroughs or external help, monitored via webcam to prevent cheating. The exam saw a notably low pass rate, with only two participants successfully finishing the game within the four-hour limit. The test aimed to simulate a strict, formal assessment of puzzle-solving and gameplay skills typical of classic adventure games.
