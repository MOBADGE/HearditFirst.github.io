Blippo+ Offers a Unique but Flawed FMV Experience from Another World

Blippo+ is an experimental FMV (full-motion video) game that presents viewers with a continuous stream of quirky TV broadcasts from an alien planet called Planet Blip. The broadcasts feature various shows—ranging from a quiz-roleplaying hybrid to teen talk shows—evoking the campy style of 1980s media. Interacting mainly involves channel surfing through these brief episodes to unlock more content. While the concept of receiving alien transmissions is intriguing, the game’s pace and repetitive content limit its appeal. The story hinted at by news and current affairs programs drifts without conclusion, and much of the content cycles redundantly. Originally designed for the Playdate handheld with timed unlocks, the PC version’s unlock system based on watch percentages has made some viewing tedious. Despite its creative premise, Blippo+ ultimately illustrates the challenges of turning a passive viewing experience into an engaging game. It is available on Steam and itch.io.

Deus Ex: Invisible War’s Audio Director Reflects on Work and Legacy

Alexander Brandon, who served as audio director for Deus Ex: Invisible War after composing for the original Deus Ex, recently shared his thoughts on the sequel's soundtrack and overall audio design. He acknowledges "room for improvement" in the work but remains proud of the team’s contributions, especially the main theme featuring vocals by his ex-wife. The soundtrack was designed to reflect the game's divisions between factions and locations, delivering distinct moods for places like Cairo and Trier. Brandon described the music as aiming for a serious and immersive tone rather than the cyberpunk clichés of the '90s. Despite some criticisms of the game’s level design, the audio elements, including contributions from the band Kidneythieves, continue to be appreciated by fans. The discussion highlights the complexities of evolving a game’s atmosphere through sound while balancing technical and creative challenges.

Resident Evil Requiem Patch Adds Photo Mode and Tweaks Cutscene Expressions

A recent patch for Resident Evil Requiem introduces a photo mode allowing players to capture and customize in-game screenshots, though camera movement remains somewhat limited compared to other games. Users can apply filters, adjust lighting, and experiment with character poses. Alongside this new feature, the update addresses several vague bug fixes and notes that character expressions in some cutscenes have been adjusted “to better convey emotion.” While specifics about these adjustments are sparse, the patch overall enhances player engagement by enabling creative use of the game’s visuals. Photo mode can be accessed via the pause menu or keyboard shortcuts, although camera movement is restricted during certain animations.

Pathfinder: Wrath of the Righteous Boasts Extensive Build Options with Over 230 Subclasses

Pathfinder: Wrath of the Righteous stands out as a deeply complex RPG, especially appealing to players who enjoy detailed character customization. The game offers 232 subclasses across 26 base classes and additional prestige classes, granting players vast freedom for buildcrafting. Among these, new options like the "Sable Company Marine" Ranger, who starts with a rideable griffon companion, add unique tactical and role-playing dimensions. The game’s emphasis on mounted combat is notable, as it provides strategic advantages in difficult encounters. However, the abundance of choices can be daunting, leading some players to restart multiple times seeking optimal builds. This depth reflects Pathfinder’s roots in its tabletop origins and differentiates it from more streamlined RPGs. The game is currently on sale until early April on Steam and GOG.

Star Wars Zero Company Director Discusses Revival of Classic PC Strategy Genres

Greg Foertsch, creative director of the upcoming Star Wars Zero Company, shared insights into the recent resurgence of older PC-centric genres such as real-time strategy (RTS) and turn-based tactics. He attributes part of the previous decline in such games during the early 2000s to the challenges of adapting complex, isometric-style tactics games for consoles and television screens, where camera use was limited. Foertsch sees current innovations in user experience and camera technology as factors enabling these genres to succeed on modern platforms, including consoles. He also noted that many newer games in these styles are pushing boundaries by experimenting with gameplay mechanics rather than relying solely on established templates. This signals industry shifts toward better integration of traditional PC game complexity with broader accessibility for couch play.
