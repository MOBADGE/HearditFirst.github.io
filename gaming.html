Crimson Desert Previews as Launch Nears After Five-Year Delay Pearl Abyss is preparing to release its action RPG, *Crimson Desert*, two weeks from now after a significant delay. Originally announced in 2020 with a planned 2021 release, the game missed its target by several years. Studio head of PR and marketing Will Powers acknowledged that the early reveal arguably set unrealistic expectations, contributing to the extended wait. Development involved building multiple game engines, which added complexity and time. To address the long buildup and speculation, Pearl Abyss is now sharing extensive previews and interviews to offer transparency and manage player expectations. This approach aims to rebuild trust and clarify the game's direction ahead of its debut.

US Department of Justice Seizes Major Hacker Forum LeakBase The United States Department of Justice has shut down LeakBase, one of the world's largest hacker forums with over 140,000 members. LeakBase hosted a vast archive of stolen data, including hundreds of millions of account credentials from high-profile cyberattacks. The site operated openly on the web without requiring special access, making it a major outlet for trading hacked information such as passwords and credit card numbers. Coordinated law enforcement actions across 14 countries led to arrests and evidence collection. The seizure highlights ongoing global efforts to disrupt cybercrime networks that threaten digital security in gaming and other sectors.

Nvidia CEO Highlights Company’s Role in Shaping Modern Gaming Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang spoke at the Morgan Stanley Technology, Media, and Telecom Conference, emphasizing the company's foundational impact on the video game industry. Huang stated that Nvidia helped "create the modern video game industry" through its work on graphics algorithms and GPU technology. He credited Nvidia's development of 3D graphics, ray tracing (RTX), and GPU computing frameworks for advancing game engines and visual fidelity. While some of his remarks, such as claiming the video game industry was created during his tenure, drew debate, the central point remains Nvidia’s deep integration in game development and hardware. He noted that game developers widely use Nvidia technologies, explaining why many PC games perform best on Nvidia GPUs.

Microsoft CEO Reflects on Intel and Apple’s Influence on Early Software Success Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella acknowledged during a Morgan Stanley interview the important roles Intel and Apple played in the development of Windows and Office, respectively. Nadella described these relationships as examples of “non-zero-sum” cooperation where others' success also benefits Microsoft. He referenced the historical "Wintel" partnership between Windows and Intel processors and noted that Office's early versions debuted on the Mac, which had a more mature graphical interface at the time. Nadella’s comments come amid ongoing shifts in Microsoft’s approach to openness and collaboration in the tech industry, particularly related to cloud services and AI integrations.

Intel Positions 18A Chip Node for External Customers, Nvidia Relationship Unclear Intel’s chief financial officer Dave Zinsner revealed at the Morgan Stanley Conference that the company’s latest 18A process node, previously intended only for internal use, is now suitable for external foundry customers. This marks an expansion of Intel’s foundry services, potentially accelerating partnerships with chip designers. However, despite Intel’s collaboration with Nvidia on combining CPU and GPU technologies, Zinsner clarified that their recent cooperation focuses on product integration rather than Intel manufacturing Nvidia GPUs at this time. Intel’s broader foundry ambitions align with its return to competitiveness after prior delays, but the specifics of future customers and manufacturing deals remain uncertain.

Deus Ex: Invisible War’s Console Design Choices Influenced by Publisher In a retrospective shared by director Harvey Smith, the limitations of *Deus Ex: Invisible War* compared to its predecessor were partially attributed to publisher Eidos’s belief that first-person shooters and RPGs did not sell well on consoles. This led to a strong push to develop the game primarily for the original Xbox, which had more restrictive memory and hardware capabilities than PCs. Consequently, the game featured smaller levels and tighter constraints to accommodate console hardware, resulting in less open-ended exploration than the first *Deus Ex*. Smith recalled that many players, especially series fans, were dissatisfied with these compromises. The case exemplifies how publisher strategies and platform targets can significantly impact game design decisions.
