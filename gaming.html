**Diablo 4’s Lord of Hatred Expansion Introduces Robust Crafting System**

Blizzard is adding a significant overhaul to Diablo 4’s item crafting with the upcoming Lord of Hatred expansion. The new system draws inspiration from Diablo 2’s Horadric Cube, allowing players to combine and upgrade items through recipes to create better gear. Importantly, low-tier items such as common or magic-tier drops will return even in endgame, but with “greater affixes” that enhance their potential—enabling these otherwise modest drops to be transformed into legendary items via crafting.

This change aims to deepen loot value and reduce the feeling of loot fatigue by making nearly any drop potentially useful, encouraging players to engage with crafting rather than simply salvaging unwanted gear. Complementing this, a new loot filter will help players focus on useful item stats, further refining item management. The system also revives the concept of “transfiguration” from Season 11, allowing players to gamble for bonus effects on items, although with toned-down power compared to earlier iterations. These updates collectively aim to refresh the progression loop and add more meaningful choices for item customization.

**Valve Responds to New York Loot Box Lawsuit with Strong Defense**

Two weeks after the New York Attorney General (NYAG) sued Valve alleging illegal gambling via loot boxes, Valve issued a detailed public response denying that its virtual item systems constitute gambling under state law. Valve highlighted that its loot boxes contain only cosmetic items, meaning players are not incentivized financially to engage, and compared loot boxes to physical collectible items like baseball cards.

Valve also pointed out efforts to combat fraudulent activities outside Steam using its items, including restricting over a million accounts involved with gambling, fraud, or theft, while reinforcing controls like trade cooldowns. The company criticized the NYAG’s approach for bypassing legislative processes and expressed willingness to comply if formal laws regarding loot boxes are enacted. Valve further cautioned that some proposed regulatory measures, such as age verification and restrictions on item trading, could negatively impact users and developers alike. This statement marks a rare direct public stance from Valve amid increasing legal scrutiny and related lawsuits.

**Minecraft’s Massive World Generation Update Led to Smaller, More Frequent Content Drops**

Mojang’s general manager Ryan Cooper revealed at GDC that the 2021 Caves & Cliffs update pushed the studio’s development processes to the limit. The ambitious overhaul of Minecraft’s world generation required updating foundational technology simultaneously, which was compared to “trying to build a skyscraper with hand tools.” This complexity contributed to Mojang’s decision to shift away from large, annual updates to smaller, more frequent content drops starting in 2024.

The change aims to deliver content more rapidly while avoiding the logistical challenges encountered with major updates. Mojang’s next planned content drop for 2026 is the Tiny Takeover update, focusing on new “tiny mobs.” This approach reflects a strategic move to maintain ongoing engagement without the massive scope and development cycles of past updates.

**Valve Reports Nearly 6,000 Games Earned Over $100K on Steam in 2025**

At GDC, Valve presented data showing that 5,863 games generated more than $100,000 in revenue on Steam during 2025, nearly doubling the figure from 2020. This growth indicates that despite concerns about market saturation, a wide range of developers—both large and small—are achieving commercial success. Valve highlighted how features like daily deals contributed by spotlighting 1,500 games last year, with many making their first appearance in these sales.

Valve emphasized the importance of tailored discovery methods to connect games with appropriate players, reinforcing Steam’s broad global user base. These statistics offer insight into the evolving ecosystem of Steam as a platform supporting diversified developer success.

**Microsoft Details Its Next-Gen Xbox-PC Hybrid Console Project Helix**

Microsoft provided new information on Project Helix, its upcoming Xbox console designed to merge the experiences of console and PC gaming. The system will support both Xbox and PC games and is powered by a custom AMD SOC optimized for next-generation DirectX features including ray tracing, machine learning upscaling, and neural rendering technologies.

Alpha development units are expected to be delivered to developers in 2027, suggesting a commercial release no earlier than 2028.
