**Inzoi Studio Reflects on Early Access Challenges**

Inzoi Studio’s head, Hyungjun "Kjun" Kim, has spoken about the difficult experience of launching the life-simulation game *Inzoi* in early access. Despite 29 years in game development, Kim admitted that creating a life sim is more complex than anticipated, noting the enduring success of *The Sims* series and the challenges of making an open-world life sim. Early access was deemed necessary because the team could not fully test the game themselves and relied on players to provide feedback. Kim expressed sympathy toward users who are effectively testing the game on the studio’s behalf while paying for it. Although *Inzoi* sold over a million copies in its first week, concurrent player counts have significantly declined, attributed partly to missing simulation content and awkward social interactions. The studio plans to improve the game with a content roadmap, enhanced mod support, and user-generated content features.

*Why it matters:* This perspective highlights the risks and realities for developers entering early access, especially in ambitious genres like life simulators. It underscores the importance of community involvement in refining games and the need for realistic expectations regarding content and performance at launch.

**Peak’s “Play It Your Way” Update Adds Extensive Customization**

The mountain-climbing survival game *Peak* has received a new update called "Play It Your Way," which introduces a broad set of custom settings for players. Users can toggle environmental challenges like weather effects, fall damage, spiders, and hunger, or adjust the item pool, such as choosing whether to include or exclude the blowgun weapon. The update includes a "Grapple Mode (Stupid)" that grants unlimited use of a rescue claw, allowing players to bypass standard game difficulty. Additionally, a "mini run" option enables players to attempt just one of the game’s biomes instead of the full sequence, catering to those with limited playtime. Campfires now save progress, providing rest points amid challenging segments.

*Why it matters:* This update reflects a growing trend toward player agency in difficulty and gameplay experience. By offering extensive customization, *Peak* accommodates diverse player preferences, potentially broadening its appeal and longevity without affecting core game progression or rewards.

**FTC Addresses Financial Service Restrictions After Steam NSFW Game Incident**

The U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has issued warning letters to Mastercard, Visa, PayPal, and Stripe concerning potential restrictions on customers based on their political or religious views. This follows Valve’s revelation that Mastercard-related policies indirectly pressured Steam to remove certain adult (NSFW) games. While Mastercard denied direct involvement, payment processors enforcing Mastercard’s "illegal or brand-damaging transactions" rules caused the delistings. FTC Chairman Andrew Ferguson emphasized that access to financial services is essential for participation in commerce and freedoms. The letters reference a 2025 executive order targeting financial institutions that restricted access to politically conservative groups. The FTC warns these companies could face investigations or enforcement actions if found to unlawfully deny services.

*Why it matters:* This development could impact how payment companies enforce content-related policies, potentially affecting creators of adult and controversial games. It illustrates ongoing tensions between corporate payment policies, platform content regulation, and regulatory oversight.

**New Steam FPS 'Too Many F*cking Nazis' Draws Controversy on Forums**

A recently announced first-person shooter titled *Too Many F*cking Nazis* on Steam features gameplay involving killing time-traveling Nazis using various weapons. The game’s blunt title and theme have stirred significant debate within its Steam community forums, attracting contentious and provocative commentary from users. Some forum posts have devolved into politically charged insults and calls for reporting the game over its depictions. The developers note the game is early in development, planning for more enemies and diversity. The backlash highlights the friction around politically themed games and online community reactions.

*Why it matters:* This case shows the challenges games with politically charged themes face in community management and discussion, reflecting broader cultural debates impacting the gaming space. It also points to developer-community dynamics for niche or provocative titles.

**Eidos Montreal Studio Experiences Leadership Change and Layoffs**

Eidos Montreal announced layoffs affecting 124 employees across production and support roles, alongside the departure of longtime studio head David Anfossi. The studio cited changing project needs for the workforce reduction. Previously, the studio had multiple layoffs since early 2024, including cancelled projects and a shift toward co-development work
