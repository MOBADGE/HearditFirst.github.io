Reflections on the Early Years of PC Gaming Industry

Recently uncovered audio from a 1989 Computer Game Developers Conference panel titled "The Golden Days of Computer Games" offers a rare glimpse into the early video game industry. Designers like Steve Cartwright and Richard Garriott recalled a smaller, more experimental time when experience was scarce, salaries were modest but negotiable, and market pressures were limited. Cartwright’s anecdote about doubling his salary to join Activision contrasts sharply with today’s industry realities. Danielle Bunten Berry lamented the loss of creative freedom, noting that early development was driven by "hey, that’s cool" rather than strict market demands. Richard Garriott emphasized the importance of team specialization and marketing in modern game production, highlighting changes that shaped the industry over the ensuing decades. The panel discussion underscores how today's complex development ecosystems evolved from humble, passion-driven beginnings.

Blizzard Hiring Suggests New Open-World Shooter Project

Blizzard Entertainment has posted a job listing seeking a Lead Designer for an unannounced "AAA open-world shooter" that will utilize Unreal Engine, a departure from the studio’s usual in-house engines. The listing requires at least 15 years of experience and signals Blizzard's intention to develop a large-scale project. Speculation links this hiring to the rumored Starcraft shooter project referenced in a 2024 Bloomberg book, which has seen multiple incarnations but no official announcement. Despite Blizzard’s recent layoffs and canceled titles, the move suggests an active effort to diversify their portfolio. Developer Jeff Hamilton indicated the role will be part of his team but offered no further details. The project might debut at Blizzard’s upcoming BlizzCon event, but no confirmation exists yet.

Valve Rolls Out Steam Storefront Redesign in Beta

Instead of an April Fool’s joke, Valve has quietly introduced a refreshed Steam storefront interface in the client beta channel. The redesign aims to improve content discovery by offering more detailed information on recommended games, including user reviews and micro-trailers that play on hover (with an option to disable). Other updates include larger artwork for discounts and events, a more responsive layout for higher-resolution monitors, infinite scrolling, and new wishlist and DLC sections. The changes result in denser, more cohesive use of screen space and quicker navigation, especially for the Discovery Queue. Valve has not announced a date for wider rollout, as the update is undergoing testing. This iteration reflects Valve’s interest in incremental usability improvements rather than major overhauls.

Diablo 4’s April Fool’s Event Features Giant Chicken Boss

Blizzard marked April Fool’s Day in Diablo 4 with a temporary world boss called Chik’han, The Fowl Beast, a large chicken enemy that drops unique "chicken-themed" loot. Items include a staff named "Cluckonomicon," which grants a "Henlightenment" buff, and the "Eggcecutioner" scythe that causes fallen enemies to drop eggs. Unlike typical Diablo events focused on standard loot and crafting materials, this boss delivers novelty gear with unusual effects. The event also offers an achievement titled "April Fowl’s Day 2026," suggesting it may become an annual feature. Players have responded positively to the humor and the distinct rewards, finding it a welcome change from more routine events.

Nintendo’s Controversial Pokémon Battling Patent Challenged by US Patent Office

The United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) issued a nonfinal rejection of Nintendo’s patent 12,403,397, which describes a specific system for battling summoned sub-characters in video games. The patent had caused concern within the industry due to fears over patenting common gameplay mechanics. However, the USPTO clarified that the patent was narrowly crafted around a unique set of battle mechanics—not the general concept of summoned creature battles. Following an ex parte reexamination initiated by the USPTO Director, examiners found the patent claims obvious in light of prior art, including previous patents and public knowledge in gaming. Nintendo can respond and appeal the decision, so the patent’s final status remains undecided. This development represents an important moment in ongoing debates about the patentability of gameplay systems.
