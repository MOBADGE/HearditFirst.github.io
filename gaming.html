Slay the Spire 2 Multiplayer Explained

Slay the Spire 2 recently added multiplayer co-op, expanding on its solo deckbuilding roguelite formula. To start co-op, one player hosts a multiplayer save and invites up to three Steam friends; there is no matchmaking or local co-op support yet. Each player uses their own character, and all must join from the beginning of a campaign—substituting players mid-run isn’t possible.

Co-op play treats the party as a unified team making shared map decisions but maintains individual inventories, gold, and cards. Battles scale enemy strength according to party size and require all players to end their turns before continuing. Players have separate energy pools and loot, and dead players are revived after battles if the team wins.

Ascension mode also works separately in multiplayer, with progression tied to the lowest-level player’s unlocks, requiring all players to unlock and coordinate those higher difficulties. This system preserves the challenge of the singleplayer while adapting to team play. Overall, the multiplayer maintains the core feel of Slay the Spire with some new co-op mechanics.

Slay the Spire 2 Sets Deckbuilder Records

Industry data confirms Slay the Spire 2 as the best-performing deckbuilder ever, far outpacing competitors. On its early access Steam launch, it peaked at 575,000 concurrent users—more than ten times the peak of the original Slay the Spire. It sold over three million copies in its first week at $24.99 each, generating an estimated $75 million before platform fees.

Daily active user numbers remain stable, averaging over a million across the first 11 days. Analysts attribute this success to strong anticipation built over seven years, a trusted developer reputation, and a lack of competing sequels. Unlike many card games that rely on spectating or streaming, Slay the Spire quietly draws in deep play sessions.

Mega Crit’s transparency about development and monetization, avoiding microtransactions and seasonal passes, likely helped player conversion. The strong launch points to the game appealing not only to roguelike fans but also to challenge-seekers new to the genre.

Death Stranding 2 Designed for Higher Completion Rates

Hideo Kojima and his team at Kojima Productions adjusted Death Stranding 2 based on feedback that the original game felt too slow. Their goal was to encourage more players to finish the story this time. Compared to the first game, worldbuilding is less elaborate and story elements more accessible through supplemental systems.

Data shows this approach worked: about 62% of players have completed Death Stranding 2’s final chapter, compared with roughly 40% for the original. The sequel also grants earlier access to vehicles and traversal tools, speeding up gameplay, though some players found this eased difficulty.

Kojima included a ‘To the Wilder’ difficulty mode on PC to cater to players who wanted a tougher challenge similar to the original’s terrain navigation. Overall, Death Stranding 2 focuses on balancing accessibility with depth to appeal to a broader audience.

OpenAI Shuts Down Sora Video App; Disney Withdraws Investment

OpenAI has discontinued its Sora video generation app less than six months after launching. The move aligns with OpenAI’s broader strategic shift away from video AI products towards productivity tools integrating ChatGPT and coding platforms.

Sora had gained attention for rapidly generating videos featuring copyrighted characters without permissions, prompting legal concerns from Japanese studios. Disney had planned a $1 billion investment in OpenAI, licensing its characters for Sora’s AI-generated content, but has since withdrawn following the app’s closure.

OpenAI plans to share timelines for preserving existing Sora creations and API details. This development reflects growing caution around AI-generated media and intellectual property issues.

City Builder Nova Roma Challenges Players with Angry Roman Gods

City builder Nova Roma, entering early access, adds a unique challenge by requiring players to please not just citizens but also demanding Roman gods. Players begin by establishing temples dedicated to different gods who grant bonuses but also have quests and resource demands.

Ignoring or neglecting a god often results in catastrophic events like floods or invaders, forcing players to balance appeasement carefully. The gods’ favor unlocks technology tree advancements. Unlike typical city sims where citizens complain about infrastructure, Nova Roma focuses on godly relationships adding layers of management complexity.
