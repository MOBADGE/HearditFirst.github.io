Pickmon Sparks Interest Among Palworld Fans Amid Legal Concerns

John "Bucky" Buckley, Palworld’s Head of Publishing & Communications, recently encountered a surge of nearly 8,000 Discord messages from fans alerting him to Pickmon, a newly announced creature-collecting game that draws strong comparisons to Pokémon and Palworld. Buckley learned about Pickmon while en route to the 2026 Game Developers Conference, where he acknowledged the community’s enthusiasm, though he noted little information is currently available about Pickmon's developer, PocketGame. This situation arises against the backdrop of Nintendo’s ongoing litigation against Palworld over Pokémon-related patents. While the legal outcomes remain uncertain, the attention on Pickmon underscores continuing challenges in the creature-collector genre regarding intellectual property and market originality.

Dead By Daylight Developers See Longevity Beyond a Decade

Behaviour Interactive’s asymmetrical horror multiplayer game Dead By Daylight is approaching its 10-year anniversary in June 2026. Senior Creative Director Dave Richard and Head of Partnerships Mathieu Cote discussed the game’s sustained success at GDC, attributing its endurance to timing, community support, and adaptability rather than an initial intent to become a live service. Merchandising elements like battle passes and cosmetic stores were introduced gradually, allowing the game to evolve over time. The developers expressed confidence that Dead By Daylight has “at least” another ten years ahead, though specific future content is being reserved for the upcoming anniversary celebrations. This outlook highlights the potential for long-term support in multiplayer titles when developers evolve with player expectations.

Arc Raiders’ Evolution: From High-Speed Movement to Tactical Gameplay

The third-person shooter Arc Raiders underwent significant changes during development, notably a reduction of player movement speed by around 60% when pivoting toward a PvPvE extraction shooter format. The game initially featured whimsical jump pads allowing players rapid aerial movement, contributing to a more heroic gameplay tone. These features were removed to align with a grounded, tactical gameplay vision. However, the Snap Hook mechanic, which enables players to zip around environments, remained and was adapted to the new pace. Despite its retained presence, the Snap Hook is more restrained than in early versions. This design shift reflects how player experience and narrative tone influenced the game's final form.

Official Google Chrome Support Coming to Arm-Based Linux Devices

Google announced plans to introduce official Chrome browser support for Linux devices running on Arm64 processors, expected in the second quarter of 2026. This follows earlier Chrome support for Arm-based macOS and Windows devices and addresses a growing user demand for a seamless browsing experience combined with Google's ecosystem on Linux. The announcement is significant as Arm-based hardware becomes more capable and widespread, including devices powered by Qualcomm's Snapdragon chips and upcoming Nvidia Arm processors. Providing native Chrome support could ease users’ transitions from Windows to Linux, particularly given some current dissatisfaction with Microsoft's platform.

Nvidia’s Path Tracing Ambitions and Support for Shader Stutter Reduction

At GDC 2026, Nvidia outlined ambitious goals for real-time path-traced graphics aiming for photorealism akin to filmed images, relying heavily on artificial intelligence and specialized hardware called RT cores. Although current path tracing is resource-intensive, Nvidia is focusing on advancing technologies like DLSS (Deep Learning Super Sampling) to mitigate performance costs. Additionally, Nvidia announced its collaboration with Microsoft to support Advanced Shader Delivery (ASD), a technology designed to reduce shader compilation stuttering in games. While path tracing continues to be a long-term focus, ASD promises more immediate improvements to gameplay smoothness and load times. Broad industry support for ASD, including from AMD, Intel, and Qualcomm, indicates potential widespread adoption in future game releases.

Legal Action Against Valve Over Music Licensing on Steam

The UK’s Performing Right Society (PRS) has initiated legal proceedings against Valve, alleging that Steam distributes games containing music from PRS members without obtaining the necessary licenses for the act of making that music available online. PRS argues that while individual games may have cleared music licensing with developers, Valve as the platform facilitating downloads and streaming requires a separate license. This lawsuit extends beyond just soundtracks to include all in-game music. PRS has a history of assertive copyright enforcement in public spaces, and it claims Valve has not properly engaged with licensing requests. The case introduces possible new legal complexities for digital distribution platforms concerning music rights in video games.
