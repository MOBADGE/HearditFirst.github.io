Fragmentary Order: New Hardcore Sci-Fi FPS from Escape from Tarkov Creator

A new game titled *Fragmentary Order*, labeled as a "hardcore sci-fi FPS," has been introduced by Rant Gaming and publisher Cor3 with involvement from Nikita Buyanov, known for *Escape from Tarkov*. Despite shared elements like music and assets, Buyanov confirmed it is not related to his previous projects. Set in the year 2251, the game places players in a solar system-wide battleground where they control "clone assets" in dangerous environments like a partially terraformed Mars. The gameplay resembles an extraction shooter with a tactical, multiplayer focus. Developers have released a seven-minute lore-heavy trailer and launched an immersive website where players can climb ranks to earn early access. Although still early in development, this setup suggests a gradual build-up to launch rather than immediate availability.

Monitor Reviews: Gigabyte GO27Q24G vs. MO27Q28GR

Two new Gigabyte OLED gaming monitors were reviewed with notable differences. The GO27Q24G uses older LG WOLED panel technology, priced around $499, making it more affordable but with reduced brightness, slightly duller colors, and a lower 240Hz refresh rate. It offers solid build quality, adjustable stand, and standard HDMI 2.1 and USB-C connectivity but lacks a USB-A hub. The panel's lower brightness and color vibrancy become noticeable in bright scenes, though it excels in darker HDR environments.

In contrast, the MO27Q28GR features LG's newer 4th Gen Primary RGB Tandem WOLED panel, commanding a $599 price tag. It has a higher refresh rate (280Hz), brighter full-screen and HDR performance, and improved color vibrancy along with enhanced HDR handling. Both monitors share fast pixel response times at 0.03 ms and similar industrial designs. The consensus suggests the extra $100 on the MO27Q28GR delivers a more consistently satisfying OLED experience, especially for users prioritizing display quality over cost savings.

Sam Altman Reflects on AI Power After Arson Attempt

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman addressed a recent incident where an incendiary device was thrown at his home. Though no one was injured, Altman used the occasion to discuss his views on AI's influence. He described AI as an immensely powerful tool with uncertain consequences and compared the drive to control artificial general intelligence (AGI) to the "ring of power" from *The Lord of the Rings*, highlighting how control over AGI can compel extreme behaviors. Altman advocates for democratizing AI access and shared responsibility rather than centralized control. Meanwhile, the person responsible for the attack has been arrested, and authorities have yet to confirm the motive. The broader debate about AI governance continues amid ongoing controversies around OpenAI's safety policies and government contracts.

Fake Windows Support Site Spreads Undetectable Malware

A new phishing scam involves a fake Windows support website offering a "cumulative update" for Windows version 24H2. The site distributes a malicious installer masquerading as an official update, which actually steals passwords and sensitive information. The malware escapes detection by antivirus programs using an Electron shell to conceal harmful JavaScript. A critical warning to users is to avoid domains like "microsoft-update.support," as Microsoft’s official support site is at support.microsoft.com. Actual Windows updates should only be installed through the system's native update interface. Cybersecurity firms have updated protections and issued guidance for those who may have downloaded the fake software.

Intel Nova Lake Leak Details Core Ultra 400-Series CPUs

New leaks reveal detailed information about Intel’s forthcoming Nova Lake desktop CPUs, known as Core Ultra 400-series. These chips range from Core Ultra 9 to Ultra 5 models with up to 8 performance cores (P-cores), 16 efficiency cores (E-cores), and additional low-power cores (LPE). A notable feature is the introduction of bLLC variants with substantially larger cache sizes—potentially up to 144 MB of last-level cache—aimed to compete with AMD’s X3D processors. The processors will support features like Thunderbolt 5 but maintain a maximum of 24 PCIe lanes, consistent with current generations. While clock speeds, pricing, and release dates remain unknown, these developments suggest Intel is advancing its CPU architecture with an emphasis on gaming and high-performance workloads.
