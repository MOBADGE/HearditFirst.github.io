Reflections on the "Golden Days" of Game Development

Recently uncovered audio from the 1989 Computer Game Developers Conference offers insight into the early days of PC game development and industry concerns that surprisingly resonate today. Veteran developers like Steve Cartwright and Richard Garriott shared anecdotes highlighting a time when the industry was smaller, wages could double with a single offer, and innovative packaging ideas—such as boxed games with cloth maps—were novel. Discussion revealed an early worry about market oversaturation, despite far fewer games releasing annually than now. Designer Danielle Bunten Berry expressed nostalgia for a period with fewer market constraints and more creative freedom. Richard Garriott advised awareness of the growing need for specialization and the increasing importance of marketing, forecasting industry shifts witnessed over ensuing decades. The panel recording provides a valuable historical perspective on the evolution of game development.

Blizzard Hiring for an Unreal Engine Open-World Shooter

Blizzard Entertainment is recruiting a "Lead Designer, Innovation" for an unannounced AAA open-world shooter utilizing Unreal Engine, marking a departure from the studio's typical use of in-house engines. The role requires extensive experience, suggesting Blizzard seeks a veteran designer, possibly including former staff affected by recent layoffs. While Blizzard has not confirmed the project’s identity, industry reporting and insider hints indicate this may relate to a third-person Starcraft shooter reportedly in early incubation under former Far Cry producer Dan Hay. Blizzard plans a BlizzCon event in September that might reveal further details. It is worth noting Blizzard’s history of canceled projects in this space, highlighting the uncertainties even established studios face with new ventures.

Valve's Steam Storefront Gets a Subtle Refresh

Instead of an April Fool’s joke, Valve rolled out a user interface update for the Steam storefront in its beta client. The redesign aims to improve navigation and visibility by making better use of screen space, especially on high-resolution monitors. Key changes include more informative "Featured and Recommended" sections with user review summaries, micro-trailers on hover, and expanded artwork in the "Discounts and Events" area. The Discovery Queue now appears as a transparent overlay with infinite scroll, and new sections highlight users’ wishlists and owned DLC. The update has not yet been released to all users, pending beta testing results. This change enhances usability without radically altering Steam’s design.

Diablo 4’s April Fowl’s Day Event Features a Giant Chicken Boss

In a thematic April 1 event, Blizzard introduced "Chik’han, The Fowl Beast," a large chicken world boss in Diablo 4 that drops unique themed loot. Items include the "Cluckonomicon" staff granting a special buff, and the "Eggcecutioner" scythe causing slain enemies to drop eggs. This event offers a humorous break from typical content and showcases Blizzard’s willingness to invest development effort into seasonal jokes beyond mere cosmetic or temporary boosts. The event’s achievement suggests Blizzard may repeat this event annually. Such additions provide variety for players between major updates or seasons, engaging the community with unexpected content.

US Patent Office Issues Nonfinal Rejection of Nintendo’s Pokémon Battling Patent

The US Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) issued a nonfinal decision rejecting all claims in Nintendo’s controversial US patent 12,403,397, which covers a specific system for battling summoned sub-characters. This patent originally raised alarm because of fears over broad protection of gameplay mechanics. However, the patent’s claims are narrowly defined around certain input and automated battle systems. The USPTO concluded these claims were obvious in light of prior art, including patents held by other companies and Nintendo’s own earlier filings. Nintendo may respond or appeal the decision, so its final status remains uncertain. This development reflects ongoing challenges in how gameplay mechanics are regulated by intellectual property law.

Garry’s Mod Successor s&box to Launch on Steam April 28

Facepunch Studios announced the Steam launch date for s&box (pronounced “sandbox”), the successor to Garry’s Mod, set for April 28. The release was quietly updated on the Steam page without a formal announcement. s&box uses a modified Source 2 engine and supports users creating games that they can export and sell on Steam without paying Facepunch royalties, pending licensing agreements. Pricing is expected around $10 to $20.
