Corsair Air 54000 Review: A Modern Successor with Innovative Cooling Design

Corsair has released the Air 5400, a successor to their popular 2014 Air 540 case, updating the design with several innovations. The most notable change is a triple-chamber layout that separates the main build area, cable management, and a dedicated radiator chamber for all-in-one liquid coolers. This design improves thermal performance by isolating the radiator‚Äôs heat from other components and maintaining ample airflow.

The case supports motherboards up to E-ATX and has extensive radiator and fan compatibility, though it is limited to 120 mm fans due to its unique ducting system. The panels are hinged for easy access, including a front-left glass panel that swings open to expose the main chamber, making building and maintenance straightforward.

Testing with a Ryzen 9 9950X3D and Corsair‚Äôs Nautilus 360 RS cooler showed excellent thermals, with CPU and GPU temperatures lower than many recent builds. However, only three fans come included with the case, so additional fans are necessary for optimal airflow. The Air 5400 weighs 16.7 kg and offers front Type-C USB ports but no Type-A, which may affect peripheral compatibility for some users.

Overall, the Air 5400 balances innovative cooling and practical design features, making it a strong choice for users who appreciated the original Air 540 but want modern capabilities.

DDR3 Motherboards Gain Popularity in China Amid Rising Memory Prices

Memory prices continue to rise sharply, impacting the PC hardware market. DDR5 pricing remains high due to supply constraints partly driven by AI industry demand, and DDR4 prices have also unexpectedly increased by nearly 10% in a single week, according to TrendForce.

As a result, Chinese DIY buyers are turning to older DDR3 motherboards paired with 6th to 9th Generation Intel CPUs as a cost-effective alternative. DDR3 hardware sales have reportedly doubled or tripled in the region. While this generation is older, it remains viable for certain games and applications, especially where the latest hardware pricing is prohibitive.

The trend underlines the wider hardware supply issues affecting modern PC components and how some buyers may revert to older platforms to continue gaming or building systems affordably.
