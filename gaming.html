Reflections on the Early PC Gaming Industry from 1989

Recently uncovered recordings from the 1989 Computer Game Developers Conference provide an interesting look back at the game industry's past. Key figures such as Robert Garriott and Steve Cartwright discussed concerns about market saturation even with far fewer games than today. Many shared nostalgic views, looking back to the early 1980s and describing a small, product-oriented industry where experimentation and "doing anything you want" were common. However, Steve Cartwright noted that the early days of easy salary doubling and casual hiring were over, highlighting how professionalization and specialization have since become necessary for sustained success in game development. Richard Garriott reflected on early distribution challenges, such as selling games in ziploc bags before boxed editions with maps and manuals became industry standards. The discussions reveal how foundational challenges and attitudes from 1989 echo but have evolved significantly in today’s gaming landscape.

Blizzard Hiring for an Unannounced AAA Open-World Shooter

Blizzard is recruiting a "Lead Designer, Innovation" for a new AAA open-world shooter built on Unreal Engine, marking a departure from its traditional in-house engines. The position requires extensive experience, suggesting Blizzard intends to bring onboard a seasoned designer to lead this project. While details remain sparse, industry speculation ties this hiring to the long-rumored Starcraft third-person shooter mentioned in a 2024 report. The project, reportedly incubated by former Far Cry producer Dan Hay, could be revealed at Blizzard's upcoming September BlizzCon event. However, given Blizzard’s recent track record of project cancellations and staff layoffs, the game's release remains uncertain, despite anticipation for potential Starcraft series revival.

Valve’s Steam Storefront Receives a Design Refresh

On April Fool’s Day, Valve opted against a joke and instead introduced a user-facing update to the Steam storefront in its beta client. The redesign aims to enhance navigability by displaying more content and clearer information. Highlights include richer game recommendations with detailed reasoning and review summaries, micro-trailers upon hovering over game covers, larger visuals for discounts and events, a transparent overlay for the Discovery Queue, and new wishlist and DLC sections. The update also supports higher resolution displays, improving responsiveness for users with wide or 4K monitors. While subtle, the refresh improves the overall experience and may become permanent following beta testing.

Diablo 4 April Fool’s Event Adds a Giant Chicken Boss

Blizzard’s Diablo 4 participated in April Fool’s Day with a new world boss: Chik’han, The Fowl Beast. This oversized chicken drops unique humorous loot such as the "Cluckonomicon" staff and "Eggcecutioner" scythe, which have quirky effects like causing enemies to drop eggs or granting buffs when interacting with the environment. Unlike typical temporary events focused on boosting progression with usual rewards, this boss adds a layer of novelty and amusement. The event received positive reactions for integrating real gameplay content with April Fool’s humor, and its exclusive achievement suggests it may return in future years.

US Patent Office Moves to Revoke Nintendo’s Pokémon Battling Patent

The US Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) has issued a nonfinal decision rejecting all claims of Nintendo’s controversial patent covering a system for battles involving summoned sub-characters, a mechanic notably part of Pokémon games. The patent describes specific mechanics, such as summoning characters to interact or battle automatically. The USPTO concluded that the claims were obvious in light of prior art, including other video game patents and publicly known game mechanics. The rejection marks a rare instance of the patent office questioning a major game mechanic patent post-issuance, though Nintendo can respond or appeal. This development may influence ongoing legal disputes concerning game mechanics patents and sets a precedent on the limits of patent scope in game design.

Garry’s Mod Successor s&box Launches April 28 on Steam

Facepunch Studios announced that s&box, the long-awaited successor to Garry’s Mod, will officially launch on Steam on April 28. The announcement came quietly via a Steam page update without extensive marketing. The studio recently secured a licensing agreement with Valve allowing creators to develop games within s&box and release them on Steam as standalone titles without sharing revenue with Facepunch. The game is expected to be priced between $10 and $20.
