**Windows Task Manager’s Surprising Developer Easter Egg**

Dave Plummer, the creator of the original Windows Task Manager, recently revealed that he accidentally left his personal phone number embedded in the app’s code during development in the 1990s. This odd detail was included as part of a debugging mechanism designed to catch rare CPU usage anomalies that exceeded 100%. By embedding his name and home phone number in an assert message, Plummer hoped any user encountering the bug internally would contact him directly. Surprisingly, despite the beta reaching thousands—possibly millions—no one ever called. The episode offers a glimpse into the informal, hands-on coding culture at Microsoft during that era and underscores the lengths developers went to troubleshoot complex issues directly.

**Crimson Desert Players Embrace Superhero Cosplay**

Players of the open-world RPG Crimson Desert have sparked renewed interest in the game through intricate cosplay recreations using the in-game dye and gear customization. The main character, Kliff, has been redesigned by the community as popular superheroes such as Captain America, Spider-Man, Superman, and more. The cosplay range even extends to iconic figures from gaming and anime, demonstrating the flexibility and creativity allowed by the game’s gear and dye systems. This trend highlights how player-driven customization and expression can extend a game's appeal beyond its original narrative and mechanics, offering fresh engagement opportunities despite some complexities in the game's dye system.

**Titanium Court Review: An Unconventional Genre Blend**

Titanium Court is an indie game that combines match-three puzzles, roguelite deckbuilding, autobattler mechanics, and visual novel storytelling. The player controls a protagonist thrust into leadership of a whimsical faerie court locked in a seemingly meaningless war. Gameplay alternates between puzzle phases where players match tiles to gather resources or disrupt enemies, followed by battlefield strategy using cards representing troops and defenses. While the writing is praised for its humor and eccentricity, the core match-three mechanics can feel repetitive and clash awkwardly with the higher-level strategic demands. Overall, the game is regarded as an ambitious and quirky experience that will appeal to players interested in unusual genre mashups and offbeat storytelling, though not all will enjoy the puzzle elements.

**TSMC Plans Sub-1 Nanometer Chip Production by 2029**

Reports indicate that semiconductor giant TSMC aims to begin trial production of sub-1 nanometer silicon chips by 2029, advancing beyond its current leading-edge 2-nanometer (N2) node expected to launch shortly. This development points to continued progress in transistor miniaturization, potentially enabling faster and more efficient processors and GPUs in the following decade. While commercial availability of such chips would come later, possibly in the 2030 timeframe, this roadmap maintains TSMC’s position at the forefront of semiconductor manufacturing. The announcement is notable as it outlines a path forward amid a challenging global memory supply landscape and ongoing demand driven by AI and gaming workloads.

**Aion 2’s Global Release Set for 2026 Despite Launch Issues**

NCSoft plans to launch Aion 2 globally in 2026, making the MMO sequel available across North America, Europe, Japan, and other regions with multiple language options. This comes after the game’s original release in Asia met with criticism over aggressive microtransaction systems, forcing the developer to issue quick apologies. Despite these issues, Aion 2 has reportedly generated significant revenue and attracted over a million subscribers in its early months. The global launch will test whether NCSoft has addressed prior monetization concerns sufficiently to appeal to a broader audience. Given the enduring profitability of free-to-play MMOs with such systems, the title’s financial performance will be key to monitoring how player reception evolves.
