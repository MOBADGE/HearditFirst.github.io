**Indie Pass Subscription Service Faces Criticism**

Indie Pass, a new subscription platform dedicated solely to indie games, is preparing to launch on April 13 at a monthly cost of $6.99. It offers access to a curated library of around 70 indie titles from the indie.io publisher's catalog, with developer earnings tied to how much subscribers play their games. However, the reception among players and developers has been largely critical. Key concerns include a compensation model that favors games designed to keep players engaged for long periods, the relatively low prices of indie games making subscription less appealing, player reluctance to not owning games outright, and the annoyance of adding yet another game launcher. Industry figures like 3D Realms co-founder George Broussard have described such services as harmful to developers. Despite criticism, Indie Pass argues it will aid discoverability in a saturated market, though its success remains uncertain amid tepid responses.

**ScummVM Adds Support for Niche FMV and Adventure Games**

ScummVM, the popular tool for running old PC adventure games on modern systems, has expanded its engine support recently. This update allows players to experience obscure titles like Crime Patrol 2: Drug Wars, a '90s full-motion video shooter with a campy anti-drug theme, as well as the Lovecraftian adventure game Necronomicon: The Dawning of Darkness. While some of these games, like Necronomicon, are recognized for their poor quality and low ratings, they hold niche interest for enthusiasts of video game history and quirky content. By enabling compatibility with a wider array of older games, ScummVM helps preserve gaming history and provides an avenue for players to explore unusual or forgotten titles.

**AI Startup Tesana Aims to Open Game Development to Millions**

Tesana, a startup developing an AI-driven game engine, is attempting to democratize game creation by letting users generate games through natural language prompts. Utilizing third-party AI tools combined with its proprietary system, Tesana converts text descriptions of environments, mechanics, and characters into playable game code. The company reports having 10,000 paying users shortly after launch. Co-founder Johannes Vermandois envisions a future where millions more people can create games, potentially increasing both the quantity and quality of indie games. However, skepticism exists about the trade-offs in control and uniqueness when relying on generative AI. While still in early stages, Tesana represents a new approach to lowering barriers in game development, with potential implications for how games are crafted and consumed.

**Slay the Spire 2 Introduces End-of-Run Badges in Beta Update**

The card-based roguelike Slay the Spire 2 has added a new feature called badges in its latest beta patch. These badges serve as a form of in-game recognition, highlighting notable accomplishments during each run, such as defeating bosses without losing health or uncovering easter eggs. The update provides additional balance tweaks, new card art, visual effects, and quality-of-life improvements. After a previous update triggered a significant backlash from the community, the current patch's reception has been more balanced, with mixed but less intense negative feedback. The badges are intended to offer players clearer insights into their run uniqueness and improve scoring, reflecting ongoing efforts to refine player experience before the game's full release.

**Popular YouTuber The Gaming Historian Retires, Shares Nintendo Court Documents**

Norman Caruso, known as The Gaming Historian, has announced his retirement from producing documentary-style gaming videos on YouTube after more than 15 years. Citing burnout following his most ambitious project covering The Oregon Trail, Caruso stated he no longer feels the drive to continue making content at his usual quality. As a final gesture to his audience, he has uploaded a sizeable collection of scanned court documents related to an early 1980s legal case involving Nintendo and Universal Studios to the Internet Archive. These documents offer detailed insights into the dispute over Donkey Kong's trademark claims and include developmental names considered for the iconic character. Caruso intends to focus on his history podcast moving forward, marking the end of an era for his video work.
