Bungie Addresses Cheating Issues in Marathon's Ranked Mode

Marathon, Bungie's latest multiplayer shooter, recently launched its Ranked competitive mode, but reports of cheating have become widespread. Players and streamers have expressed frustration, with some calling the mode "ruined" due to rampant cheating. In response, Bungie reiterated its strict zero-tolerance policy on cheating, confirming it has been issuing permanent bans to confirmed offenders since launch.

The studio is actively expanding its detection tools and telemetry to identify cheaters more effectively, with some improvements already implemented and more planned in the coming weeks. Bungie is also enhancing the in-game reporting system to make it easier to flag cheating and toxic behavior, and is considering sending notifications to players when action is taken on their reports. Additionally, better voice moderation and stream sniping protections are under consideration. Bungie encourages the community to continue reporting issues as the company commits ongoing resources to maintain competitive integrity.

Yoshihisa Kishimoto, Creator of Double Dragon and Kunio-kun, Passes Away

Yoshihisa Kishimoto, the influential game designer behind the Double Dragon and Kunio-kun series, died on April 2 at the age of 64. Kishimoto's career began in the 1980s with LaserDisc games, but he found lasting success with the beat-em-up genre through titles like Nekketsu Kōha Kunio-kun (Renegade) and Double Dragon.

His innovations helped define side-scrolling beat-em-ups, combining storytelling and cooperative gameplay. Despite being mostly tied to these franchises during his time at Technos Japan, Kishimoto also contributed to other games such as Super Dodge Ball and WWF Wrestlefest. After leaving Technos, he worked as a consultant and developed mobile games, with his last major work being Double Dragon 4 in 2017. His son announced the news on social media, expressing gratitude for his father's impact.

Steam Workshop Receives Major Redesign for Improved Browsing

Valve has started rolling out a significant update to the Steam Workshop interface, aiming to improve browsing speed and usability, especially on mobile devices and the Steam Deck. The redesign addresses slow page reloads by rewriting the page's code, allowing quicker updates when filtering or sorting content.

Key changes include wider display layouts with larger preview images, smarter filters configurable by game developers, and a new "Quick View" feature that lets users browse details without leaving the list view. The update also enhances responsiveness for different devices, improving accessibility for a broad range of players.

Currently in beta, the new Workshop interface is available to users who opt in, with Valve planning to iterate based on feedback before a full release.

Starfield's Latest DLC and Update Launch with Keith David Narration

Bethesda released the Free Lanes update and the Terran Armada DLC for Starfield on April 7. The Free Lanes update is free for all players and introduces open-space travel between planets along with new encounters, resources, and gameplay improvements. The Terran Armada DLC, priced at $10, adds a new faction and story elements involving military deserters using robot armies.

To promote the content, actor Keith David delivered a dramatic narration in a new trailer, garnering positive attention for his compelling delivery. The updates have coincided with a spike in concurrent player numbers on Steam, reaching their highest since late 2024, indicating renewed player interest. Bethesda emphasizes that the update is evolutionary rather than a major overhaul.

Majority of PC Gamer Readers Unlikely to Use Nvidia's DLSS 5

A recent poll of PC Gamer readers revealed skepticism toward Nvidia's upcoming DLSS 5 technology, with 71% stating they could never be convinced to enable it. Notably, 37% said they would refuse to use it regardless of its visual quality due to ethical concerns or personal preference.

DLSS (Deep Learning Super Sampling) is an AI-powered upscaling technology that Nvidia promotes for improving graphical performance. The lukewarm reception may reflect uncertainty about its practical benefits, potential performance costs, and AI-driven content consistency. Around 10% of respondents are already convinced they will use DLSS 5 upon release, and another 9% would enable it selectively based on game type and quality. Discussion around DLSS 5 continues as Nvidia prepares for its fall launch.
