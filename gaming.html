ZSA Voyager and Navigator: Ergonomics and Portability with Trade-offs

ZSA recently released the Voyager ergonomic keyboard and an optional Navigator trackball module. The Voyager is a much slimmer, 52-key version of their highly regarded Moonlander keyboard, designed for portability and minimal desk footprint. It is fully split, wired-only, and has just one level of tenting, prioritizing a low profile and easy transport over the adjustability of its predecessor.

While the Voyager offers quality mechanical switches and extensive customization via included extra keys and ZSA‚Äôs Oryx software, its limited number of keys presents challenges for users wanting a single keyboard for both work and gaming. The heavy reliance on layers can be awkward in games, limiting its versatility. The Navigator trackball attachment adds a pointing device but lacks its own buttons, requiring key presses on the keyboard for mouse clicks. This module feels less refined in build quality and usability, leading to a mixed impression overall.

At $365, the Voyager suits users prioritizing space-saving ergonomic typing but is less suitable as a full gaming keyboard. For those wanting more keys and comfort versatility, the Moonlander remains the better choice.

New Turn-Based RPG People of Note Combines Musical Theatre and Combat

People of Note, by Iridium Studios, presents an unusual blend of turn-based RPG mechanics with musical theatre elements. Highlighted by PC Gaming Show Tokyo Direct and recently featured at Day of the Devs, the game includes rhythm-based combat sequences alongside narrative songs spanning genres like rock and EDM.

The soundtrack is co-written by Jason Charles Miller, known for Final Fantasy 14 compositions, adding credibility to its musical ambitions. Gameplay shown indicates a Paper Mario-style combat system with timing-based bonuses. Although no release date is set, the game is expected next year and is available for wishlist on Steam, appealing to those seeking unconventional RPG experiences.

Nvidia RTX 3060 Graphics Cards Near End of New Supply

Reports indicate that Nvidia's RTX 3060 GPU, first launched nearly five years ago with 12GB VRAM, is finally selling out new stock. Manufacturers have reportedly cleared inventories through late 2025, with the model officially phasing out.

The RTX 3060 remains notable for its high VRAM relative to successor GPUs like the RTX 4060 and 5060, which offer less (8GB) with narrower memory buses. Despite newer hardware improvements and ray-tracing features, the 3060 retained value for users needing ample VRAM at an affordable price.

Its longevity is partly attributed to demand from users running smaller AI models or needing cost-effective 12GB GPUs amid ongoing memory shortages. The passing of this generation marks an end of accessible high-VRAM cards in the midrange segment for now.
