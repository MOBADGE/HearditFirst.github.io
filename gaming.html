Overwatch Draws Inspiration from Marvel Rivals for Major Hero Update

At the 2026 Game Developers Conference, Overwatch 2 director Aaron Keller revealed that Blizzard was influenced by NetEase’s Marvel Rivals when planning their latest update. Marvel Rivals launched with a large roster and frequent additions, generating significant excitement partly because of its big hero drops. This prompted Blizzard to consider what players' reactions might be if Overwatch introduced a large number of new heroes at once.

While Blizzard did not add as many as Marvel Rivals, they did release five new characters simultaneously, including unique hero Jetpack Cat, as part of Overwatch's largest seasonal update. The move appears to have positively impacted player engagement, with Overwatch shedding the “2” from its name and seeing increasing player counts over consecutive weeks—a departure from the initial launch pattern in 2022. Blizzard plans to add another five heroes before the end of 2026.

This shows how competition in the multiplayer shooter genre continues to shape content strategies, with Blizzard adapting by accelerating character releases to maintain player interest.

Baby Steps Puts Players to the Test with Untested Climbing Challenges

Game designers of the climbing puzzle game Baby Steps discussed their unconventional approach at GDC, which diverges from typical polished, guided single-player design. Co-creator Gabe Cuzzillo explained their choice to embrace what they call “indifference to the player,” deliberately including climbing challenges that were not fully tested beforehand.

One example are stacks of cans placed atop potentially climbable structures where no rewards await, serving as distractions. Despite these deliberate design “trolls,” players managed to climb all but one of these stacks. The unreachable stack became a sort of open challenge that pushed players to experiment creatively.

This design philosophy challenges traditional level design by encouraging players to discover solutions independently rather than following a prescribed path, representing a less curated but more exploratory gameplay experience.

Industry Division Over Generative AI at Game Developers Conference

The presence of generative AI at this year’s GDC highlighted a stark divide within the gaming sector. While representatives from venture capital and tech firms expressed enthusiasm for AI's potential in game development, a recent GDC survey found over half of developers view generative AI negatively. Only 7% of developers considered it beneficial.

Moritz Baier-Lentz, a venture capitalist investing heavily in AI companies, expressed being “shocked and sad” that game developers are “demonizing” generative AI. He suggested concerns largely stem from job security fears following industry layoffs. Other developer apprehensions include ethical issues around art usage, environmental impact, and skepticism about AI quality.

Industry leaders acknowledge current AI limitations but emphasize its potential to augment creativity rather than replace human ingenuity. The debate reflects broader tensions about AI's role and impact in creative industries.

Bungie to Revert Overly Punishing Audio Change in Marathon

Bungie has acknowledged player dissatisfaction with a recent update in the live-service shooter Marathon that increased the audible range of gunfire and explosions. This audio enhancement was intended to provide players with better situational awareness, encouraging more PvP engagements, but many felt it made stealth and survival overly difficult.

Players reported the change forced constant engagement, reducing strategic variety and enjoyment. Bungie described this adjustment as an “overcorrection” and plans to scale back the audio range to a more balanced level in an upcoming patch.

This response underlines the challenges developers face when tuning multiplayer dynamics that affect player experience and game flow.

Challenges of RPG Difficulty Balancing Explored Through The Outer Worlds 2

Obsidian senior systems designer Robert Donovan spoke at GDC about the complexity of balancing role-playing game difficulty, referencing their work on The Outer Worlds 2. Traditionally, RPGs feature a “reverse difficulty curve,” starting hard but becoming easier as players gain power. Obsidian attempted to maintain a consistent sense of challenge where “weak things are weak, and strong things are strong.”

They shifted from abstract health pools to a “hits to kill” approach, focusing on the number of hits required with a standard weapon to defeat enemies. This method ensures difficulty feels meaningful without overwhelming players, especially on Normal mode. Despite these efforts, testers had mixed opinions on difficulty—some found it too easy, others too hard, highlighting the subjective nature of game challenge.
