Japanese Indie Developer Pursues Legal Action Over Missing Crowdfunding Funds

Skeleton Crew Studio, the developer behind the upcoming spiritual successor to the detective visual novel *428: Shibuya Scramble*, is seeking legal action against its crowdfunding platform, Ubgoe. The campaign raised 55 million yen (about $340,000), but the studio has received less than half of that amount. Ubgoe claims the missing funds were "mistakenly wired to a different client." Despite a memorandum that promised full payment by mid-September 2025, only a fraction of the money was transferred. The platform has not provided satisfactory evidence explaining the delay. The situation is complicated by terms stating that the project owner, not the platform, bears responsibility for backer obligations. Although the developer secured additional support to continue development, it remains determined to recover the owed funds.

This case draws attention to risks indie developers face when using crowdfunding, especially regarding fund handling and contractual responsibilities.

Bungie Reduces Power of Marathon’s Top Weapon

Bungie has nerfed the Biotoxic Disinjector—the most powerful weapon in the endgame map Cryo Archive of the shooter *Marathon*. The weapon’s damage has been reduced by 35% after complaints that its grenade launcher mode allows players to quickly kill entire teams due to high damage, bouncing projectiles, and blast persistence. The change aims to "curb a little bit of its dominion in the game," though Bungie indicated further adjustments might follow.

The update comes after a change that made the weapon’s key access item, Subroutines, spawn more reliably, increasing the weapon’s availability and its impact on matches. Player reactions to the nerf are mixed; some feel it was justified, while others believe it does not sufficiently address the weapon’s dominance. Bungie faces the ongoing challenge of balancing rewarding skilled players with maintaining competitive fairness.

Star Wars Zero Company Studio Highlights Risk Taken by Lucasfilm and Respawn

Greg Foertsch, founder of Bit Reactor and developer of *Star Wars Zero Company*, praised Lucasfilm and Respawn for supporting a turn-based tactics game—a niche genre within the Star Wars franchise. Foertsch credited their willingness to take a risk on a new studio and genre as uncommon in the industry, where large companies often prefer safer, familiar projects.

Despite past examples of high-profile turn-based tactics games performing poorly commercially, such as *Marvel’s Midnight Suns*, Bit Reactor has benefited from this trust, allowing them to develop a unique Star Wars experience. The collaboration could encourage further experimentation with nontraditional game types within major franchises.

Narrative Direction of Star Wars Zero Company Focuses on Realistic Team Dynamics

Aaron Contreras, narrative director for *Star Wars Zero Company*, detailed the game’s approach to storytelling, emphasizing complex team dynamics rather than a typical "chosen one" hero fantasy. The game centers on a squad of diverse operators during the morally ambiguous Clone Wars period, requiring players to navigate interpersonal conflicts and varied worldviews within the team.

Contreras described a bond system that reflects these relational tensions, allowing for both camaraderie and friction, including potential romantic feelings among squad members. This focus on realistic leadership challenges differentiates the game from more conventional RPG narratives and deepens player engagement with the story.

Blockchain-Powered Game Storefront Robot Cache Reportedly Closing, Affecting User Libraries

Reports have surfaced that Robot Cache, a blockchain-based PC game storefront launched in 2018, is shutting down. Users claim they received emails indicating the store will close in 30 days, after which games purchased—even if fully downloaded—will become unplayable due to the platform’s verification system going offline. The company has not officially confirmed this closure.

Robot Cache aimed to allow users to resell games using blockchain technology but struggled to attract a sizable user base and generated minimal revenue in recent years. Community moderators have noted a lack of communication from the company for several years. If confirmed, this situation raises concerns about the long-term viability and preservation of games on DRM-dependent or blockchain-integrated platforms.
