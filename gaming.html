Starsand Island Temporarily Removed From Steam Over Unauthorized Visuals

The cozy life simulation game *Starsand Island*, praised by PC Gamer as a standout cozy game of 2026, has been temporarily pulled from Steam. Developer Seed Sparkle Lab disclosed that some core visual elements from a classic game — suspected by players to be *Tetris* — were included without proper authorization in a mini-game section. The studio cites this as an oversight in the planning phase and has submitted a revised build with the offending content removed.

Once approved, the game will return to Steam, and existing owners will receive a compensation outfit. Player progress will remain intact after the update. The developer also pledged to strengthen internal review processes to prevent repeat issues. Notably, this follows an earlier incident involving suspicious fake positive reviews shortly after the game's February launch. Meanwhile, *Starsand Island* remains available on Xbox.

This development is important because it highlights ongoing challenges developers face in ensuring proper licensing of in-game assets, and how such oversights can impact game availability on major platforms, affecting player access and developer reputation.

Most Players See Only Half of *Esoteric Ebb*, Says Creator

Christoffer Bodegård, creator of the RPG *Esoteric Ebb*, shared insights revealing that many players likely experience only about 40-60% of the game’s content in a single playthrough. The game contains expansive hidden areas, characters, and quests that players commonly miss, by design. Bodegård explains that this approach helps create the illusion of a larger world and enhances replayability.

Unlike RPGs with mutually exclusive story paths, *Esoteric Ebb* allows all achievements to be earned in one playthrough, though it is improbable the first time. Bodegård compares this experience to the hidden content in games like *Elden Ring*, where missing elements make the world feel more expansive.

This approach matters because it challenges traditional expectations of completeness in RPGs and suggests that deliberately limiting player exposure can be a design choice to encourage exploration and social discovery, fostering a sense of a living, breathing game world.

Amazon Luna Ends Support for Third-Party Game Purchases and Subscriptions

Amazon has announced that its Luna cloud gaming service will no longer support purchases of games or subscriptions from third-party stores including EA, Ubisoft, and GOG. Effective immediately, users cannot purchase these titles or subscriptions through Luna. Streaming access to previously purchased third-party games will end on June 10, 2026.

Players who bought third-party games via Luna can still access them on the original platforms, but Luna’s Bring Your Own Library feature, which allowed streaming games from third-party accounts, will also end on June 3. Save data for these titles can be downloaded for 90 days after removal, though compatibility with other services cannot be guaranteed.

Previously, Luna positioned itself as an alternative to Steam with a focus on cloud streaming, but this move further scales back its offerings, likely pushing users toward Luna Premium’s subscription model for continued access to a library of third-party games. This reflects ongoing difficulties in sustaining cloud gaming platforms amidst stiff competition and licensing complexities.

RAM Spot Prices Drop Slightly After Huge Increases

After a dramatic surge over the past year, spot prices for RAM have eased by about 5%, according to industry reporting. For context, 16GB DDR4 RAM modules saw prices spike more than 2,200% in the previous year, and DDR5 prices increased by around 600%. Current prices remain significantly above pre-crisis levels, with 16GB DDR4 modules trading near $74, compared to roughly $3.20 a year ago.

This minor price reduction breaks a long streak of rising prices but is not expected to translate into consumer savings soon. Market analysts and manufacturers anticipate continued high demand driven by AI workloads will keep memory prices elevated through at least 2027, possibly until 2028 when new production capacity comes online.

High RAM costs affect the PC gaming community by increasing the expense of building or upgrading systems. As memory prices stabilize only gradually, consumers face tough choices balancing performance and budget.
