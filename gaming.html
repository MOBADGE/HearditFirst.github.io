Pragmata: Controller vs Keyboard and Mouse

Capcom's upcoming action game Pragmata has sparked discussion about its control schemes following recent previews and demos. Director Cho Yonghee emphasized that the development team carefully designed both controller and keyboard/mouse controls to ensure effective gameplay across platforms. However, testing reveals that the hacking mechanics—an integral part of combat—feel smoother and more intuitive on a controller, particularly because the D-pad allows quicker navigation of hacking grids compared to the more cumbersome mouse input. Yonghee encourages players to try the free demo multiple times with both setups to determine personal preference, noting some players find keyboard and mouse too tricky. This insight matters as control comfort will impact how players experience Pragmata's unique blend of shooting and hacking, possibly affecting player satisfaction at launch.

Starfield's Free Lanes Update: Reducing Loading Screens

Bethesda's Starfield will receive the Free Lanes update on April 7, bringing a new Cruise Mode to spaceship travel. This mode allows players to traverse lanes between planets at varying speeds while encountering random events. A key benefit is the reduction of disruptive loading screens during travel; instead of abrupt transitions, the game will use a brief blur effect as players approach encounters or planets. This aims to foster a more seamless exploration experience, addressing a common criticism of Starfield’s original space traversal as laden with loading interruptions. Additional additions include a new moon jumper vehicle, improved NPC crew options, shared storage for outposts, collectible action figures, and a new crafting resource, X-Tech. Alongside the update, Bethesda will release the Terran Armada DLC featuring a new questline involving an army of robots. This update addresses player feedback about immersion, potentially improving ongoing engagement with Starfield.

Pragmata's Combat Focused on Hacking Mechanics

Further details from hands-on coverage of Pragmata underline that its gameplay distinguishes itself by requiring players to hack enemies before dealing significant damage. Director Cho Yonghee explained that combining hacking mechanics with third-person shooting was central from the start, aiming to add depth beyond traditional shooting. The hacking minigame involves navigating paths on a grid using the controller's D-pad or mouse. While controller input feels more fluid, mouse users may experience some clunkiness in view control during hacking. Producer Naoto Oyama added that players can balance their upgrade focus between hacking and shooting, allowing customization of combat style. The hacking system also extends beyond combat, helping players interact with the environment to access locked areas or terminals. This design choice might shape player engagement with Pragmata by blending tactical and action elements in combat and exploration.

Slay the Spire 2 Early Access Review

Mega Crit's Slay the Spire 2 entered early access in March 2026 with expanded content including five playable characters—three returning from the original game and two new additions. The game continues the roguelike deckbuilder formula where players build decks and strategize through turn-based battles. It includes a new cooperative multiplayer mode for up to four players, though currently requires friends to join since no matchmaking exists yet. While there is substantial content for early access players, such as all three acts and ten Ascension difficulty levels, some features are not present, notably a true ending and enhanced lore development. Critiques highlight that Slay the Spire 2 currently feels very similar to its predecessor, recycling much content, which may make it less appealing to veterans. However, it serves as a solid introduction for newcomers to the genre. The game is expected to remain in early access for up to two years as more content and features are added.

Antec Flux Pro Noctua Edition Review

The Antec Flux Pro Noctua Edition is a premium PC case combining the established Flux Pro design with Noctua's well-regarded quiet and efficient fans. Priced at around $400, it includes six high-quality Noctua fans, a fan controller, and a distinctive brown and walnut aesthetic. Testing indicates the Noctua fans provide superior airflow with reduced noise compared to the stock Flux Pro fans, resulting in slightly lower GPU and CPU temperatures during gaming benchmarks. The case supports various motherboard sizes and radiators up to 420mm, with tool-less panels and expansive cable management options. Though the price is significantly higher than the standard Flux Pro, the enhanced cooling and quiet operation make it suitable for users prioritizing a silent but well-cooled build. However, budget-conscious buyers may find the original Flux Pro sufficient.
