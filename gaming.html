**Qualcomm's Snapdragon X2 CPU Surpasses Laptop Competitors in Single-Core Performance** Qualcomm’s new Snapdragon X2 Arm CPU recently appeared in Geekbench 6 benchmarks, showing single-core performance scores about 30% higher than current leading x86 laptop processors from AMD and Intel. The tested Snapdragon X2 Elite Extreme X2E-96-100 model scored 4,033 points, compared with roughly 3,000 points for AMD’s Ryzen AI Max+ 395 and Intel’s Panther Lake chips. Despite this lead in raw CPU speed, the Snapdragon X2 remains an Arm-based chip, meaning most PC games require emulation to run, which can hinder real-world gaming performance. Furthermore, Qualcomm’s integrated Adreno GPU, though improved, still trails Intel’s latest integrated graphics in tests. The lack of widespread discrete GPU support on Arm laptops limits gaming prospects. Qualcomm and industry watchers are awaiting Nvidia's upcoming Arm-based PC chip for signs of better gaming viability on Arm platforms.

**Highguard Shuts Down After 37 Days, Director Reflects on Performance** Multiplayer shooter Highguard was discontinued just over a month after launch due to financial unsustainability. Studio head Chad Grenier revealed the game attracted over two million players globally, with a strong average session length of 91 minutes and nearly 3.5 matches per session. PlayStation 5 accounted for the largest share of players, more than double that of Xbox, with PC (Steam) being the smallest. Despite a committed player base, Tencent withdrew funding shortly after launch, forcing the studio to cease active development and prepare for closure on March 12. Grenier denied rumors of internal mismanagement, stating the team was critically engaged throughout. The situation highlights challenges facing live service games that fail to immediately hit revenue targets, regardless of audience size.

**Ubisoft Ends Support for Assassin’s Creed Shadows, Confirms Future Multiplayer Plans** Ubisoft announced that Assassin’s Creed Shadows is entering its final phase of support, with reduced update frequency and a planned stream on March 20 to share final content and future plans. The focus will shift to three upcoming Assassin’s Creed projects, including the narrative-driven Codename Hexe. Ubisoft clarified that the multiplayer-focused Codename Invictus is still under development, though not resembling earlier rumors of a party-style game. The publisher also confirmed ongoing efforts to reintroduce co-op gameplay to the series, building on lessons from a canceled co-op project initially tied to Shadows. Codename Hexe recently lost director Clint Hocking, with veteran Jean Guesden now leading. These developments suggest Ubisoft is reworking multiplayer approaches while maintaining narrative ambitions.

**Apple Launches Budget MacBook Neo at $599 Featuring A18 Pro Chip** Apple introduced the MacBook Neo, a new 13-inch laptop priced at $599, aimed at more affordable entry into its ecosystem. Powered by the A18 Pro chip, originally from the 2024 iPhone 16 Pro, and equipped with 8 GB of RAM, the Neo emphasizes efficiency and AI-related tasks rather than raw gaming power. Battery life is claimed to be around 16 hours, despite a modest 36.5 Whr battery. While not a high-powered gaming device, the MacBook Neo may appeal to students or users seeking a well-designed, lightweight machine for general use. Comparable ARM-based laptops from Samsung and Asus, featuring Qualcomm Snapdragon X chips and more RAM at similar or slightly higher prices, offer alternative options. Apple’s focus on AI workloads reflects broader industry trends towards machine learning integration in consumer devices.

**Meta Introduces FrameSync to Improve VR Responsiveness and Comfort** Meta is deploying FrameSync, a new frame timing technique in Horizon OS for Quest headsets, designed to reduce stale frames and latency in virtual reality experiences. Compared to the existing PhaseSync system, FrameSync aims to deliver smoother frame pacing, fewer consecutive stale frames that cause visual hiccups, and lower motion-to-photon latency. These improvements can help reduce VR-induced nausea and discomfort, particularly in graphically demanding applications. However, FrameSync may slightly increase CPU and GPU usage, potentially impacting battery life and thermal performance. Meta plans to make FrameSync the standard method for Horizon Store apps soon, offering developers time to adapt and the option to revert if issues arise. This adjustment reflects ongoing efforts to enhance VR usability as content complexity grows.
