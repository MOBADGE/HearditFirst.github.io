New Steam Game Pickmon Pushes Limits of Design Knockoffs

A newly listed Steam game called *Pickmon* drew attention for its extensive and blatant copying of characters and design elements from popular franchises such as Pokémon, The Legend of Zelda, Overwatch, and even the survival game *Palworld*. The game features creatures closely resembling familiar Pokémon like Pikachu, Charizard, and Lucario, but with minor tweaks and color changes. It claims to be an open-world survival title where players team up with their Pickmon partners for fighting, farming, and building industrial empires — including equipping them with guns, mirroring *Palworld*’s controversial style.

While the developers avoid directly infringing on Nintendo’s patents by replacing monster-catching mechanics with card-based systems instead of orbs, the sheer volume of deliberate design imitation has raised questions about originality and legal boundaries. Although not likely to breach intellectual property laws narrowly, Pickmon shows how far some games can go in borrowing recognizable elements. This trend reflects ongoing tensions between fostering creativity and policing visual similarities in gaming.

Valve Reports Steam Downloads Surpass 100 Exabytes in 2025

Valve revealed that Steam users downloaded over 100 exabytes of games in 2025, up from 80 exabytes in 2024. To put that into perspective, an exabyte equals one quintillion bytes or 1,000 petabytes. Steam users averaged 274 petabytes of installs and updates daily, illustrating the platform’s continued broad usage and increasing data demands. Concurrent users peaked at 42 million, growing steadily over the past five years.

Valve also highlighted that the revenue share paid out to developers on non-Valve games reached 76% in 2025, reflecting favorable terms introduced in prior years. This huge volume of digital distribution underscores Steam’s dominant position in PC gaming and its ongoing commercial success.

Daedalic Adventure Game Deponia Now Free on Steam; Publisher Sale Active

Daedalic Entertainment’s point-and-click adventure *Deponia* is currently free on Steam until March 16th. The first game in the trilogy is praised for its slapstick style and unique world, though sequels have been less well-received. Alongside this, Daedalic is hosting a publisher-wide sale offering steep discounts—up to 90% off—on titles like *Night of the Rabbit*, *Edna & Harvey: Harvey’s New Eyes*, and *The Dark Eye: Memoria*.

These sales offer an opportunity for fans of classic adventure games or those curious to explore them at a reduced cost. The promotion highlights the enduring appeal of narrative-driven games from Daedalic, even as the studio’s output has slowed.

Meta Argues Uploading Pirated Books Via BitTorrent Qualifies as Fair Use

In ongoing litigation related to Meta’s use of pirated books to train its AI language model LLaMa, Meta has introduced a new legal argument. It contends that its uploading of copyrighted books via BitTorrent—a file-sharing protocol whereby users both download and upload content—should be protected under the fair use doctrine. The company argues that mass downloading through torrents was necessary for acquiring the data efficiently, and because torrenting inherently involves sharing files, that activity is part of the transformative fair use involved in training AI.

Authors suing Meta dispute this defense, saying the company had long known of the uploading claims but raised fair use only recently to avoid full disclosure. The court’s decision on this matter will carry significant implications for AI training practices and intellectual property enforcement related to digital content.

EA Lays Off Staff from Battlefield Studios After Record Launch

Despite Battlefield 6’s record-breaking launch with over 7 million copies sold in three days and substantial player numbers continuing months later, Electronic Arts has laid off an undisclosed number of employees from the game’s development studios including Criterion, Dice, Ripple Effect, and Motive. EA described these cuts as a realignment to better focus on live service support.

The layoffs come amid challenges scaling ongoing content support and competition, as well as EA’s acquisition by Saudi Arabia’s Public Investment Fund prompting industry concerns over future studio closures. This development showcases the common industry trend that even successful game launches do not guarantee job security for development teams.
