Australia Introduces Age Verification for R18+ Online Games

Starting March 9, Australians will be required to verify their age before accessing online games rated R18+, including popular titles like *GTA Online*. This regulation targets online multiplayer experiences and does not affect singleplayer offline games. The Australian government is enforcing strict compliance, with fines reaching up to AU$50 million for publishers that fail to meet the requirement. Age verification methods will be more robust than simple date-of-birth entries, potentially involving credit card details, government IDs, or biometric data. The move reflects Australia’s broader efforts to regulate online content for adults, paralleled by recent social media restrictions for minors. While intended to protect children from inappropriate online material, the policy raises concerns about privacy and data security, particularly regarding third-party identity verification services.

Marathon Launch Brings New Gameplay and Early Issues

The multiplayer shooter *Marathon* has officially launched following a weekend server stress test. Early player feedback notes strong weapons and engaging maps but mentions the extensive loot management as a minor annoyance. The game features several classes, with Assassin and Vandal being favorites for solo players due to stealth and mobility advantages. A new playable class, Thief, introduces a unique playstyle centered on intel gathering and utility via drone and grapple tools.

Some players have experienced problems with Deluxe Edition bonuses and pre-order rewards not appearing correctly. Bungie is aware and working on fixes, advising Deluxe Edition owners to hold off on certain in-game purchases to avoid complications. A notable bug allowed players to access rare loot carried over from the test event into the full game, a quirk of the item borrowing system intended to prevent item trading but resulting in some unintended rewards.

Diablo 4's Next Season Lets Players Become the Butcher

Blizzard announced that the upcoming *Diablo 4* Season of Slaughter, launching March 11, will feature an experimental mechanic allowing players to transform into the iconic demon enemy, the Butcher. This ability activates by accumulating enough monster kills during a new open world event associated with "Helltide" zones. While players can’t fight the Butcher himself in dungeons, those transformed can engage other players in PvP areas.

The season emphasizes maintaining killstreaks to power up unique buffs active on special items, encouraging continuous combat. Alongside this event, Blizzard will offer free access to the Paladin class for a week to all players, regardless of owning the upcoming Lord of Hatred expansion. The expansion itself is scheduled for April 28 and will introduce a new Warlock class.

Pragmata Release Date Moves Up by One Week

Capcom has adjusted the release of the sci-fi adventure *Pragmata*, moving it from April 24 to April 17. The publisher did not provide an explanation beyond the announcement that the game now has April “all to itself,” implying a less crowded release window. The game had first been announced years ago and was subject to delays, raising concerns it might be canceled. The earlier release may help build momentum in an otherwise light month for major launches. Preview impressions describe the game’s aesthetic and design as reminiscent of mid-2000s shooters with linear levels and a clean, futuristic NASA-inspired visual style.

Epic Games Sues Former Contractor for Leaking Fortnite Secrets

Epic Games has filed a lawsuit against Hayden Cohen, a former contractor accused of leaking confidential information about upcoming *Fortnite* content under the pseudonym AdiraFN. The company alleges Cohen violated a non-disclosure agreement by sharing trade secrets on social media platforms, including details of unreleased collaborations such as *Kingdom Hearts* and *Game of Thrones*. Epic is seeking a permanent injunction to prevent further leaks, destruction of confidential materials, and financial damages. The case highlights ongoing industry tensions surrounding leaks and the legal consequences for workers who breach confidentiality agreements.

The clip showed an attack involving a historical US battleship and a World War II-era fighter aircraft, both inaccurate for the current conflict. The game's developer confirmed the footage’s origin.
