Teamgroup MP44Q 2TB NVMe SSD Review: Performance and Pricing Concerns

Teamgroup’s MP44Q is a budget PCIe 4.0 NVMe SSD featuring a 2TB capacity built with YMTC’s 232-layer 3D QLC NAND and a Maxiotech MAP1602A controller. It offers a five-year warranty and a durable endurance rating of 1000 TBW, which is comparatively higher than many budget QLC drives that typically offer around 600 TBW. Physically, the drive is a single-sided M.2 2280 design compatible with PCs, laptops, and consoles, with no heatsink option.

However, despite these specs, the MP44Q's real-world performance struggles in areas key to gaming and general use. Its random 4K read/write speeds are significantly lower than competitors such as the WD Blue SN5100, leading to longer game loading times. For example, in Final Fantasy XIV: Shadowbringers, the MP44Q recorded nearly 8 seconds of load time compared to about 7.1 seconds on the SN5100. Additionally, the MP44Q operates at moderate temperatures but delivers slower bandwidth and read/write access times than some similarly priced alternatives. At around $305, its price is high for a budget-oriented QLC drive, especially considering it is outperformed by cheaper options. The drive might only make sense as a secondary storage solution if found at a substantial discount.

Nexon CEO Comments on The First Descendant’s Decline

Nexon CEO Junghun Lee publicly addressed the rapid decline in player retention for The First Descendant. After a strong launch with over 10 million users in its first week, concurrent players have dropped to roughly 5,000. Lee cited "design issues" as the root cause, stating these problems are structural and “not fixed with a patch.” He grouped The First Descendant with other Nexon titles that had strong launches but lacked lasting player engagement, such as Dungeon & Fighter Mobile.

The company’s capital markets briefing indicated no clear plans to revive The First Descendant at present. This frank admission suggests that Nexon sees fundamental gameplay flaws rather than temporary bugs or content shortages as the main issue. For players and stakeholders, this underscores the challenges of sustaining interest in looter shooters amid a crowded market. It also contrasts with Nexon’s successes on other fronts, such as Arc Raiders and MapleStory.

Seasonic Unveils Focus ATX 3.1 'Sakura' Cherry Blossom-Themed Power Supply

Seasonic has teased a limited edition of its Focus ATX 3.1 power supply series featuring a cherry blossom-inspired "Sakura" design. The new model inherits the technical specifications of the existing Focus GX ATX 3.1 units, including 750W, 850W, and 1000W variants, full compliance with ATX 3.1 and PCIe 5.1 standards, and 80 PLUS Gold efficiency certification.

The power supply incorporates Seasonic’s patented three-phase thermal control with Digital Hybrid Fan Control offering fanless, silent, and cooling modes. It supports fully modular cabling with three PCIe 8-pin connectors and carries a 10-year warranty. Pricing for the standard versions ranges from $145 to $204, and the Sakura edition may command a premium. There is no announced release date yet.

Older GPUs See Continued Demand for AI Workloads, Says CoreWeave CDO

CoreWeave's co-founder and CDO Brannin McBee reported ongoing strong demand and rising prices for older-generation GPUs, particularly those based on Nvidia’s Ampere architecture (around 2020), in AI server markets. Despite newer GPUs like Hopper, Blackwell, and upcoming Rubin chips, the late 2010s models remain viable for many AI inference tasks that do not require the absolute latest hardware.

McBee explained that not all AI workloads demand cutting-edge GPUs, so there is an efficient market balance pairing hardware with task requirements. Pricing for Ampere GPUs increased throughout 2025, while Hopper-based GPUs largely held steady. The sustained demand contributes to continued hardware scarcity and pricing pressures noticeable across memory and storage markets, impacting PC gaming hardware indirectly.
