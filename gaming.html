**Task Manager’s Creator Reflects on Software’s Evolution and Efficiency**

Dave Plummer, the original creator of Windows Task Manager, recently shared insights into the development of the tool, revealing that its initial version was around 50 times smaller—under 80 KB—compared to today’s 4 MB size. Plummer attributes this to the constraints and priorities of early computing hardware, where small size directly correlated with speed and responsiveness. He emphasized that Task Manager needed to act instantly when system issues arose, without lagging under heavy dependencies. His programming experience on limited systems like the Commodore 64 influenced a mindset where every line of code had a tangible cost. While modern systems and software have grown more complex, Plummer notes that older software like Task Manager made deliberate choices to include only what was necessary, a concept not always prioritized in contemporary coding. His reflections highlight the shift in software design philosophy over three decades of Windows development.

**New Metro 2039 Game Confirmed, Reveal Scheduled for This Week**

Fans of the Metro series from 4A Games can expect a new entry titled *Metro 2039*. The full reveal is planned for April 16 during an Xbox online showcase. This will be the fourth mainline installment in the post-apocalyptic first-person shooter series based on Dmitry Glukhovsky’s novels, following *Metro 2033*, *Metro: Last Light*, and *Metro Exodus*. The previous games centered on survivors navigating the Moscow subway tunnels after nuclear devastation. While details remain sparse, the timing and announcement suggest the new game could arrive sooner rather than later. The last title concluded with indications of survivors remaining above ground, hinting at possible story directions. Players interested in catching up or replaying the series can currently find the older Metro games on sale across several digital stores.

**Starfield Director Explains and Defends Changes to New Game+ Mode**

Starfield’s director, Todd Howard, commented on recent adjustments to the game’s New Game+ (NG+) mode following the Free Lanes update. Originally, entering the NG+ reset much of a player’s progress except for skills and powers, intending to prompt reflection on what players are willing to sacrifice to continue. However, many players found the loss of items and relationships too punishing and avoided NG+. Bethesda has since added a feature allowing players to carry over up to 50 selected items. Howard explained that this change aims to encourage more players to engage with the multiverse concept and continue playing beyond the main story. The update also includes new upgrade paths for gear. While recognized as a departure from the original high-stakes design, these adjustments seek to improve player retention and enjoyment during replay cycles.

**Overwatch Announces Plans to Revise Hero Ban System**

Blizzard’s Overwatch 2 is preparing changes to its hero ban system, which currently allows teams to ban up to four heroes per match. The system uses player votes to determine which heroes are banned but can leave some player choices unreflected due to role limits and ties. Associate game director Alec Dawson explained that the team wants to provide players with more ways to communicate hero preferences during the ban phase to make the process feel fairer and more informed. Among proposed changes is a “Lobby Ban” that would serve as a fifth ban slot, potentially overriding the role-limit rules and considering votes from both teams. These improvements aim to allow teams greater control over the competitive meta and address frustrations with the current system.

**Custom Linux Lock Screens Inspired by Popular Games Gain Attention**

A developer named Darkkal44 has released a collection of custom Linux lock screens called Qylock, designed to mimic the visual styles of popular games such as Minecraft, NieR: Automata, Genshin Impact, and several anime-inspired titles. Built for display managers like SDDM and Quickshell, these themes provide Linux users with options to personalize login screens in a way not typically possible on Windows. The project appeals to users who appreciate deep customization and gaming aesthetics. While Windows remains dominant for many players, notably for gaming convenience, such community-driven projects illustrate Linux’s continued appeal as a platform for enthusiasts willing to tinker. Some even see these options encouraging consideration of moving away from Windows for daily use.
