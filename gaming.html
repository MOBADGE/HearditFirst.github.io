Blight: Survival Delays by Rebuilding Core Systems

Medieval zombie combat game *Blight: Survival* has gained significant interest, with over 1.5 million wishlists on Steam, making it one of the most anticipated titles. However, its release has been delayed as developer Haenir Studio, in partnership with publisher Behaviour Interactive, decided to pause development to rebuild the game’s core systems from scratch. According to creative director Ulrik Langvandsbråten, this step was necessary to fully realize their original vision, especially refining the game's combat to feel more physical and impactful. This approach aims to improve gameplay depth but means fans will have to wait longer for the release date, which remains unannounced. Regular updates and environmental previews have kept the community informed during this period of development.

Death Stranding 2 Adds Tougher PC Difficulty Mode

Following its recent PC launch, *Death Stranding 2* introduced a new difficulty setting called "To the Wilder" to respond to player feedback that the game’s existing hardest mode, Brutal, was still too easy. Created by Kojima Productions, this new mode increases challenges notably by intensifying effects like Timefall, a hazardous weather condition that damages equipment and resources. The design pushes players to carefully manage resources and experiment with lesser-used items to succeed. The mode also emphasizes cooperative social elements among players to help navigate difficulties. The addition reflects the developers’ willingness to adjust and expand gameplay options for dedicated players seeking greater challenges.

Crimson Desert’s Developer Addresses Criticism of Complex Controls

Following mixed feedback about *Crimson Desert*’s control scheme, Pearl Abyss’ PR and marketing director Will Powers responded to criticism by likening learning the controls to riding a bike—they come naturally with practice. The game has faced notable player frustration over the complexity and clunkiness of its controls, with some critics and community sections openly negative about the experience. While Powers acknowledged the controls require an adjustment period, co-creative director Dinga Bakaba of Arkane supported the idea that challenging control schemes can lead to rewarding gameplay experiences. Pearl Abyss meanwhile committed to monitoring community feedback and hinted at forthcoming improvements.

OpenTTD Clarifies Situation After Removal from Steam and GOG

The open-source remake of *Transport Tycoon Deluxe*, *OpenTTD*, was recently removed from Steam and GOG due to the re-release of the original *Transport Tycoon Deluxe* by rights holder Atari. While this has upset some fans, OpenTTD’s development team explained that Atari approached them to collaborate, balancing commercial interests with the free availability of OpenTTD, which remains free to download directly from its official website. Atari is supporting OpenTTD by contributing to server costs. The decision aims to ensure both the original game’s return and the continued vitality of OpenTTD, though the community remains divided on the change.

Subnautica 2’s Early Access Release Date Causes Legal Tension

The ongoing legal and management dispute around *Subnautica 2* intensified after the game’s May early access release date was announced by Krafton without consultation with Unknown Worlds’ reinstated CEO, Ted Gill. Gill’s legal team claims the announcement was unauthorized following a court ruling restoring his leadership and argues it has undermined marketing efforts and caused community confusion. Krafton maintains the date was preplanned and that Gill is free to adjust it. The situation adds stress amid the extended deadline tied to a $250 million earnout payment Krafton owes Unknown Worlds. The dispute illustrates ongoing friction that may affect the game’s launch and public perception.

Industry Survey Shows Strong Support for AI Disclosure on Steam

A recent survey by GamesIndustry.biz reveals that almost nine out of ten game industry workers believe Valve should require developers to disclose the use of generative AI on Steam storefronts. Many respondents expressed opposition to Valve’s current policy that limits disclosure to AI content consumed by players, rather than including AI used for internal development or efficiency. The survey also found that most developers do not currently use AI extensively for content creation but support transparency for players. The results reflect a growing demand within the industry for clearer communication about AI’s role in game development, suggesting that stricter disclosure policies may become standard.
