Ark: Survival Ascended’s New DLC Revives Elements from Atlas

Studio Wildcard is incorporating mechanics inspired by its earlier pirate survival game, *Atlas*, into the upcoming *Ark: Survival Ascended* expansion titled *Tides of Fortune*. *Atlas*, released in 2018, was ambitious with its massive networked ocean world and detailed shipbuilding, but it struggled due to technical issues and punishing gameplay loops that made progress fragile and recovery slow.

Co-founder Jeremy Stieglitz acknowledged these flaws, describing *Atlas* as “insanely punishing” with many design lessons on what not to do. However, elements like sailing and ship physics were praised. The new DLC aims to bring these features in a more accessible form within the *Ark* ecosystem, improving ocean visuals and physics to allow players to build large pirate ships and explore ocean environments more confidently. This marks a shift from *Ark*’s traditionally flat and uninspiring water systems to dynamic, networked ocean physics that also offer benefits for modders.

Monster Hunter Stories 3: Guide to Mutations and How to Obtain Them

*Monster Hunter Stories 3* features mutations—rare subspecies of monsters that offer stronger stats and improved abilities but are not mandatory to complete the main story. These mutations depend on the game’s ecosystem restoration mechanics, requiring players to hatch and release specific monsters and raise ecosystem ranks in certain habitats.

Key mutations include Pink Rathian, Dreadqueen Rathian, Ivory Lagiacrus, Hellblade Glavenus, and others such as Deadeye Yian Garuga and Soulseer Mizutsune. Each mutation has distinct requirements involving ecosystem rank and sometimes the presence of specific rival or elemental monster types. For example, Dreadqueen Rathian requires an S Rank Rathian or Pink Rathian plus nearby poisonous monsters, while Stygian Zinogre requires raising Zinogre’s rank to A after rescuing it. This detailed ecosystem interaction adds complexity and encourages players to engage deeply with monster management for stronger companions.

Overwatch Director Reflects on 5v5 to 6v6 Shift and Community Feedback

Aaron Keller, game director of *Overwatch*, addressed the long-standing controversy over the game’s transition from 6v6 to 5v5 teams in *Overwatch 2*. Introduced in 2022, the 5v5 format removed one tank per team and altered gameplay significantly, drawing criticism from many players who preferred the original setup.

Keller admitted that the development team waited too long—about a year—to respond meaningfully to the debate, hoping the controversy would subside on its own. This delay "cost us trust," he said during a panel at GDC 2026. Since then, Blizzard has tested and introduced limited-time 6v6 modes, which remain popular but have not fully resolved questions about the game's identity. Keller emphasized plans to continue experimental modes and more frequent communication with the community, recognizing the need to balance innovation with preserving the core competitive experience.

Jeff Kaplan Shares Perspectives on World of Warcraft’s Early Design and Internet Criticism

Jeff Kaplan, former Blizzard vice president and game designer known for his work on *World of Warcraft* and *Overwatch*, revealed that he originally wanted *WoW* to be a first-person game. During a recent 10-hour stream showcasing his new game *The Legend of California*, Kaplan explained his preference was influenced by *EverQuest*’s more immersive view, which made players more attentive to character appearance.

Kaplan also spoke candidly about his frustration with online gaming communities’ negative discourse, particularly criticism leveled at games players have never actually tried. He urged online users to stop disparaging games they don’t engage with, calling such behavior unproductive. His comments highlight ongoing tensions between developers and parts of gaming communities around feedback and online criticism.
